The Whitney Extension
on the Exterior of $[-1,1]^2$

Take a function $f$ defined on the closed square $F = [-1,1]^2$ and extend it to a function on all of $\mathbb{R}^2$. In two dimensions the choice of nearest point $p_k$ becomes geometrically rich, and the extension is genuinely non‑trivial.

⟡ Roll Tide ⟡

Given a closed set $F \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ and a function $f : F \to \mathbb{R}$, the Whitney extension is

$$ \mathscr{E}_0(f)(x) \;=\; \begin{cases} f(x), & x \in F, \\[6pt] \displaystyle \sum_k f(p_k)\,\varphi_k^{*}(x), & x \in \Omega := \mathbb{R}^N \setminus F, \end{cases} $$

where $\{Q_k\}$ is a Whitney decomposition of $\Omega$, $\{\varphi_k^*\}$ is the associated $C^\infty$ partition of unity, and each $p_k \in F$ is a fixed nearest point of $F$ to $Q_k$.

In one dimension with $F = [-1,1]$, every cube on the right of $F$ has $p_k = +1$ (the unique nearest point), so the extension collapses to the constant $f(+1)$ on $(1, \infty)$. That is why the 1‑D version of this exercise was — as you put it — not interesting at all. In two dimensions, the geometry of $F$ makes $p_k$ vary nontrivially with $k$, and the extension acquires structure.

The set $F$ and its distance function

Let $F = [-1, 1]^2$, the closed unit square (centered at the origin, side $2$). Its complement $\Omega = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus F$ is connected. The distance function decomposes piecewise: $$ d(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \sqrt{\bigl(\max(0, |x_1|{-}1)\bigr)^2 + \bigl(\max(0, |x_2|{-}1)\bigr)^2\,}. $$ On the four edge strips (one coordinate outside $[-1,1]$, the other inside), $d$ is a linear ramp orthogonal to the corresponding edge of $F$. On the four corner regions (both coordinates outside), $d$ is the Euclidean distance to the nearest corner of $F$.

Exterior Whitney decomposition

We tile $\Omega$ by closed dyadic squares $\{Q_k\}$ with disjoint interiors and side lengths comparable to their distance from $F$. Outer rings spread to infinity with side doubling at each level; inner rings accumulate dyadically toward $\partial F$:

$$ \begin{array}{l} \text{Outer rings (annular shells around } F\text{):} \\[4pt] \quad \text{Ring 0:}\;\;\text{cubes of side } 1/4 \text{ in } [-3/2,3/2]^2 \setminus [-5/4,5/4]^2 \\[2pt] \quad \text{Ring 1:}\;\;\text{cubes of side } 1/2 \text{ in } [-2,2]^2 \setminus [-3/2,3/2]^2 \\[2pt] \quad \text{Ring 2:}\;\;\text{cubes of side } 1 \text{ in } [-3,3]^2 \setminus [-2,2]^2 \\[2pt] \quad \text{Ring 3:}\;\;\text{cubes of side } 2 \text{ in } [-5,5]^2 \setminus [-3,3]^2 \\[2pt] \quad \quad \;\;\;\vdots \\[8pt] \text{Inner rings (toward }\partial F\text{):} \\[4pt] \quad \text{cubes of side } 1/8,\, 1/16,\, 1/32,\, \ldots \end{array} $$

Each ring at level $\lvert k \rvert$ has side $\ell_k$ comparable to the distance of its cubes from $F$: by construction, $\ell_k \le \operatorname{dist}(Q, F) \le 2\ell_k$ for every $Q$ in ring $k$, well within the Whitney bound $\le 4\,\ell_k$.

The nearest‑point map $p_k$

For each $Q_k$ we choose $p_k \in F$ minimizing $\operatorname{dist}(Q_k, p_k)$. Because $F = [-1,1]^2$ is convex, the closest point of $F$ to any external point $(x_1, x_2)$ is the projection $$\operatorname{proj}_F(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \bigl(\,\operatorname{clip}(x_1; -1, 1),\; \operatorname{clip}(x_2; -1, 1)\,\bigr).$$ We take $p_k = \operatorname{proj}_F(x^k)$ where $x^k$ is the center of $Q_k$. The map $\operatorname{proj}_F$ partitions $\Omega$ into eight regions:

$$ \begin{array}{ll} \textbf{Four edge strips:} & p_k \text{ slides along an edge of } \partial F. \\[2pt] \textbf{Four corner cones:} & p_k \text{ is fixed at a corner of } F. \end{array} $$

This is the geometrically rich behavior absent from 1‑D. In the corner cones, $\mathscr{E}_0(f)$ will be nearly constant equal to the corner value of $f$. In the edge strips, $\mathscr{E}_0(f)$ will vary in the direction parallel to the edge — tracking the boundary values of $f$ along that edge — and be roughly constant transverse to it.

The mollifier

The construction begins with a single $C^\infty$ bump that we then translate and dilate to fit each Whitney cube. Start from the smooth ramp $$ g(t) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 0, & t \le 0, \\[6pt] \displaystyle\frac{e^{-1/t}}{e^{-1/t} + e^{-1/(1-t)}}, & 0 < t < 1, \\[10pt] 1, & t \ge 1. \end{array} \right. $$ This $g$ belongs to $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$. The numerator $e^{-1/t}$ and its derivatives all vanish as $t \to 0^+$, and similarly $e^{-1/(1-t)}$ vanishes to all orders as $t \to 1^-$, so every derivative of $g$ matches the constant value ($0$ on the left, $1$ on the right) where the pieces meet — no kinks, no jumps. From $g$, build the one-dimensional bump $$ \psi(s) \;=\; g\!\left( \frac{3/4 - |s|}{1/4} \right) \;=\; g\bigl(3 - 4|s|\bigr). $$ The argument exceeds $1$ when $|s| \le 1/2$, so $\psi \equiv 1$ on $[-1/2, 1/2]$. The argument is non-positive when $|s| \ge 3/4$, so $\psi \equiv 0$ outside $[-3/4, 3/4]$. In between, $\psi$ slides smoothly from $1$ down to $0$. Since $g$ is $C^\infty$ and the inner expression $3 - 4|s|$ is locally constant near its only singular point $s = 0$ (where the argument equals $3 > 1$, on the plateau of $g$), $\psi$ is $C^\infty$ on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

Now tensor it for two variables: $$ \varphi(s_1, s_2) \;=\; \psi(s_1)\,\psi(s_2). $$ By construction $\varphi \in [0, 1]$, $\varphi \equiv 1$ on the unit cube $Q_0 = [-1/2,\,1/2]^2$, and $\varphi \equiv 0$ outside the inflated cube $Q_0^{*} = [-3/4,\,3/4]^2 = (1 + \varepsilon)\,Q_0$, with $\varepsilon = 1/2$.

Fig. 4The base mollifier $\varphi(s_1, s_2) = \psi(s_1)\,\psi(s_2)$. Drag to rotate. Square plateau over $Q_0 = [-1/2,\,1/2]^2$ where $\varphi \equiv 1$; smooth descent through the collar $Q_0^{*}\setminus Q_0$; identically $0$ beyond $|s_1| > 3/4$ or $|s_2| > 3/4$. Smoothness is inherited from the ramp $g$ via the chain and product rules.

For each Whitney cube $Q_k$ with center $x^k = (x_1^k, x_2^k)$ and side $\ell_k$, translate and rescale: $$ \varphi_k(x) \;=\; \varphi\!\left(\frac{x - x^k}{\ell_k}\right) \;=\; \psi\!\left(\frac{x_1 - x_1^k}{\ell_k}\right)\,\psi\!\left(\frac{x_2 - x_2^k}{\ell_k}\right). $$ Then $\varphi_k \equiv 1$ on $Q_k$, $\varphi_k \equiv 0$ outside the enlarged cube $Q_k^{*} = (1 + \varepsilon)\,Q_k$, and the chain rule gives the scale-aware derivative bound $$ \bigl|\partial^\alpha \varphi_k(x)\bigr| \;\le\; A_\alpha\,\ell_k^{-|\alpha|}, $$ with $A_\alpha$ depending only on the multi-index $\alpha$ — not on $k$ or $F$. So derivatives blow up exactly as fast as the cube shrinks, but no faster.

Partition of unity

Sum and normalize: $$ \Phi(x) \;=\; \sum_k \varphi_k(x), \qquad \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \frac{\varphi_k(x)}{\Phi(x)}, \qquad x \in \Omega. $$ Every $x \in \Omega$ lies in some Whitney cube $Q_k$, where $\varphi_k(x) = 1$, so $\Phi(x) \ge 1$ — the division is harmless. At any single point at most $4$ enlargements $Q_k^{*}$ overlap (one cube plus up to three neighbors meeting at a vertex), so the sum is locally finite and $\Phi(x) \le 4$. Tautologically, $$ \sum_k \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;\equiv\; 1, \qquad x \in \Omega, $$ and each $\varphi_k^{*}$ inherits $C^\infty$ smoothness from $\varphi_k$ and $\Phi$. This gives a $C^\infty$ partition of unity subordinate to the open cover $\{Q_k^{*}\}$. The general $d$-dimensional bound is $1 \le \Phi \le 2^d$.

The extension formula at work

With everything in place, the extension is $$\mathscr{E}_0(f)(x) \;=\; \sum_k f\bigl(p_k\bigr)\,\varphi_k^{*}(x), \qquad x \in \Omega.$$ At each $x \in \Omega$, this is a smooth convex combination of values $f(p_k)$ where the $p_k$ are nearby points on $\partial F$. Geometrically:

$$ \mathscr{E}_0(f)(x) \;\approx\; f\bigl(\operatorname{proj}_F(x)\bigr). $$

It is approximately a "smeared‑out" version of $f \circ \operatorname{proj}_F$ — the extension whose value at $x$ is the boundary value of $f$ at the closest boundary point. The partition of unity smooths the would‑be discontinuities along the rays where $\operatorname{proj}_F$ has kinks (the four diagonal rays from corners of $F$).
