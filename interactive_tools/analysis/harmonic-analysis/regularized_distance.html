Whitney Decomposition · Partition of Unity · Smoothing

The Regularized
Distance Function on $[-1,\,1]$

Smooth out the distance to the boundary while preserving its size, by gluing scale-adapted bumps together with a partition of unity.

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ be open with complement $F$. The distance function $$d(x) = \min\{|x-y| : y \in F\}$$ is Lipschitz of constant $1$, but in general is not smooth — it has corners along the medial axis of $\Omega$. There is, however, a $C^\infty$ replacement $\tilde d$ with the same size and controlled derivatives, satisfying:

$$ c^{-1} d(x) \;\le\; \tilde d(x) \;\le\; c\,d(x), \qquad |\nabla \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c, \qquad |\Delta \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c\, \tilde d(x)^{-1}. $$

Below we carry the construction out explicitly for the simplest non-trivial case, $\Omega = (-1,1)$, where the distance is a tent function. Each of the seven steps is illustrated.

The distance function $d(x) = 1 - |x|$

For $\Omega = (-1, 1)$ we have $F = \{-1, +1\}$ and $$d(x) = 1 - |x|, \qquad x \in (-1, 1).$$ This piecewise linear tent has a single kink, at the medial point $x=0$, where the derivative jumps from $+1$ to $-1$. It is exactly this kink that will be smoothed.

Fig. 1The distance $d(x) = 1 - |x|$ to the boundary $\{-1,\,+1\}$. The corner at $x = 0$ is the only obstruction to smoothness.

Whitney decomposition of $\Omega$

The Whitney covering lemma furnishes a covering of $\Omega$ by closed dyadic cubes $\{Q_k\}$ with disjoint interiors and the comparability $$\operatorname{diam}(Q_k) \;\le\; \operatorname{dist}(Q_k,\,F) \;\le\; 4 \operatorname{diam}(Q_k).$$ In one dimension "cubes" are intervals. We use the dyadic family

$$\begin{aligned} Q_1 &= [-1/2,\,1/2], &\ell_1 &= 1, \\ Q_2 &= [-3/4,\,-1/2], &\ell_2 &= 1/4, &\quad Q_3 &= [1/2,\,3/4], &\ell_3 &= 1/4, \\ Q_4 &= [-7/8,\,-3/4], &\ell_4 &= 1/8, &\quad Q_5 &= [3/4,\,7/8], &\ell_5 &= 1/8, \\ Q_6 &= [-15/16,\,-7/8], &\ell_6 &= 1/16, &\quad Q_7 &= [7/8,\,15/16], &\ell_7 &= 1/16, \\ &\;\;\;\vdots \end{aligned}$$

Each level halves the side length and accumulates toward the boundary $\{\pm 1\}$. Write $x^k$ for the center of $Q_k$ and $\ell_k = \operatorname{diam}(Q_k)$ for its side length.

Fig. 2The Whitney decomposition of $(-1,1)$, plotted on a log scale to expose all dyadic levels. Each colored bar is one cube $Q_k$, drawn at height $\ell_k$. Pieces shrink geometrically as they approach the boundary points $\pm 1$ marked with $\times$.

A $C^\infty$ mollifier $\varphi$

We now fix one bump function once and for all. The function $\varphi$ should satisfy $$0 \le \varphi \le 1,\qquad \varphi(x) = 1 \text{ on } Q_0 = [-1/2,\,1/2],\qquad \varphi(x) = 0 \text{ outside } (1+\varepsilon)Q_0 = [-3/4,\,3/4],$$ with $\varepsilon = 1/2$. The standard prescription is built from the smooth ramp $$ g(t) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \displaystyle\frac{e^{-1/t}}{e^{-1/t}+e^{-1/(1-t)}}, & 0 < t < 1, \\[8pt] 0, & t \le 0, \\[2pt] 1, & t \ge 1, \end{array} \right. $$ which is $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ with all derivatives vanishing at $t=0$ and $t=1$. We then set $$\varphi(x) \;=\; g\!\left( \frac{3/4 - |x|}{1/4} \right) \;=\; g\bigl(\, 3 - 4|x| \,\bigr).$$ Inside the plateau $|x|\le 1/2$ the argument exceeds $1$ so $\varphi \equiv 1$. Outside the support $|x| \ge 3/4$ the argument is $\le 0$ so $\varphi \equiv 0$. The transition lives in the collar $1/2 \le |x| \le 3/4$.

Fig. 3The base mollifier $\varphi$. plateau $\varphi \equiv 1$ on the unit cube $Q_0$; collar where $\varphi$ smoothly descends to zero; identically zero beyond.

Localized mollifiers $\varphi_k$

For each Whitney cube $Q_k$, translate and rescale $\varphi$ to fit: $$\varphi_k(x) \;=\; \varphi\!\left(\frac{x - x^k}{\ell_k}\right).$$ Then $\varphi_k(x) = 1$ on $Q_k$ and $\varphi_k(x) = 0$ outside the slightly inflated cube $Q_k^{*} = (1+\varepsilon)Q_k$. The chain rule yields the scale-aware bound $$\bigl|\partial^\alpha \varphi_k(x)\bigr| \;\le\; A_\alpha\, \ell_k^{-|\alpha|},$$ which scales with the size of $Q_k$ — derivatives blow up exactly as fast as the cube shrinks.

Fig. 4The first seven $\varphi_k$. Each is a translated, dilated copy of $\varphi$, tailored to its cube. Pieces near $x=0$ are wide; pieces near the boundary become exponentially narrow.

Partition of unity

Sum and normalize. Set $$\Phi(x) \;=\; \sum_k \varphi_k(x), \qquad \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \frac{\varphi_k(x)}{\Phi(x)}, \quad x \in \Omega.$$ Every $x \in \Omega$ lies in some $Q_k$, where $\varphi_k(x) = 1$, so $\Phi(x) \ge 1$ and the division is harmless. Moreover only finitely many $\varphi_k$ are nonzero at any given $x$ — in fact in this 1‑D setting at most three. Tautologically, $$\sum_k \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;\equiv\; 1, \qquad x \in \Omega,$$ giving a $C^\infty$ partition of unity subordinate to the cover $\{Q_k^*\}$.

Fig. 5The unnormalized sum $\Phi(x) = \sum_k \varphi_k(x)$. It satisfies $1 \le \Phi(x) \le 2$ throughout $\Omega$, with $\Phi = 1$ at the center of each cube (only one $\varphi_k$ active) and $\Phi = 2$ at the dyadic seams between adjacent cubes (two consecutive plateaus meeting). The dashed lines mark the exact bounds.

Fig. 6The partition of unity $\{\varphi_k^*\}$. Each colored curve is one $\varphi_k^*$; their pointwise sum is identically $1$ (dotted line). Smooth across every seam between adjacent cubes.

The regularized distance $\tilde d$

The construction is delivered in a single line. Define $$\boxed{\quad\tilde d(x) \;=\; \sum_k \operatorname{diam}(Q_k)\, \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \sum_k \ell_k\, \varphi_k^{*}(x), \qquad x \in \Omega.\quad}$$ At every point $x$, this is a smooth convex combination of the side lengths $\ell_k$ of the (few) Whitney cubes whose enlargements $Q_k^*$ contain $x$. Since each such cube satisfies $\ell_k \asymp d(x)$, the resulting $\tilde d(x)$ is comparable to $d(x)$.

Some texts denote this construction by $\Delta(x)$ rather than $\tilde d(x)$; the two are the same function. One could equivalently use the unnormalized $\sum_k \ell_k \varphi_k(x)$ — the resulting function differs from $\tilde d$ by the bounded factor $\Phi(x)$ and so is also comparable to $d$.

Verifying the three bounds

(i) Comparability. If $\varphi_k^*(x) > 0$ then $x \in Q_k^*$, hence $d(x) \asymp \ell_k$ by the comparability property of the Whitney decomposition. A convex combination of quantities each comparable to $d(x)$ is itself comparable to $d(x)$, giving $c^{-1} d \le \tilde d \le c\, d$.

(ii) Lipschitz gradient. $\tilde d$ is Lipschitz with constant independent of $F$: differentiating, $\partial \tilde d = \sum_k \ell_k\, \partial \varphi_k^*$, and the bounds $|\partial \varphi_k^*| \le A\, \ell_k^{-1}$ kill the $\ell_k$ factor. Only finitely many terms contribute at any $x$, so $|\tilde d\,'| \le c$ uniformly.

(iii) Higher-order blow-up. For the Laplacian — here, the second derivative — the same argument gives $|\partial^2 \tilde d(x)| \le \sum_k \ell_k\, A\, \ell_k^{-2} = A \sum_k \ell_k^{-1}$, and the surviving terms have $\ell_k \asymp d(x)$, so $|\partial^2 \tilde d(x)| \le c\, d(x)^{-1} \asymp c\, \tilde d(x)^{-1}$. Sharp.

Fig. 8The derivative $\tilde d\,'(x)$ in terracotta, plotted against $d\,'(x) = -\operatorname{sgn}(x)$ in faded ink.
