Two Dimensions · Whitney Decomposition · Partition of Unity

The Regularized
Distance Function on $(-1,1)^2$

Smoothing the boundary distance on the open square. The same construction that worked in 1‑D, now with cubes that nest in the plane and bumps that are tensor products.

For an open set $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ with complement $F = \mathbb{R}^N \setminus \Omega$, the distance function $d(x) = \min\{|x-y| : y \in F\}$ is Lipschitz of constant $1$ but typically not smooth: it kinks along the medial axis of $\Omega$. There is, however, a $C^\infty$ replacement $\tilde d$ — the regularized distance — satisfying:

$$ c^{-1} d(x) \;\le\; \tilde d(x) \;\le\; c\,d(x), \qquad |\nabla \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c, \qquad |\Delta \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c\, \tilde d(x)^{-1}. $$

We carry out the construction explicitly for the open square $\Omega = (-1,1)^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, where the distance function is a four‑sided pyramid with kinks along its two diagonal ridges. The recipe is the same as in one dimension: a Whitney decomposition into dyadic cubes, a smooth bump function, a partition of unity, then the weighted sum of cube diameters.

The distance function $d(x_1, x_2)$

For $\Omega = (-1, 1)^2$, the boundary is the square $\partial \Omega$ and a short calculation gives $$d(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \min\bigl(1 - |x_1|,\; 1 - |x_2|\bigr), \qquad (x_1, x_2) \in \Omega.$$ This is a piecewise linear pyramid: peak of height $1$ at the origin, descending linearly to $0$ along each side. The pyramid has four diagonal ridges $|x_1| = |x_2|$ where the gradient is discontinuous (the two arguments of the $\min$ trade dominance there). Off these ridges, $d$ is smooth and equals either $1 - |x_1|$ or $1 - |x_2|$.

Fig. 1The distance pyramid $d(x_1, x_2) = \min(1{-}|x_1|,\, 1{-}|x_2|)$. Drag to rotate. The four ridges along $x_2 = \pm x_1$ are the kinks where the gradient jumps — exactly what the regularization will smooth.

Whitney decomposition of $\Omega$

We tile $\Omega$ by closed dyadic squares $\{Q_k\}$ with disjoint interiors, with side lengths shrinking geometrically toward $\partial\Omega$ so that $$\operatorname{side}(Q_k) \;\asymp\; \operatorname{dist}(Q_k,\, \partial\Omega).$$ Concretely we take a single central square of side $1$ surrounded by nested annular rings of progressively smaller dyadic squares:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Level 0:} \quad & Q_0 = [-1/2,\,1/2]^2, & \ell_0 &= 1, & \text{1 square.} \\ \text{Level 1:} \quad & 20 \text{ squares of side } 1/4 \text{ in } [-3/4,3/4]^2 \setminus Q_0, & \ell_1 &= 1/4. \\ \text{Level 2:} \quad & 52 \text{ squares of side } 1/8 \text{ in the next ring}, & \ell_2 &= 1/8. \\ \text{Level 3:} \quad & 116 \text{ squares of side } 1/16, & \ell_3 &= 1/16. \\ & \;\;\;\vdots \end{aligned} $$

Each annular ring is obtained from the previous by stepping outward a distance equal to half the previous side, and tiling with squares whose side is exactly that step. The number of squares at level $k$ scales linearly with $k$ in this 1‑codimension boundary; in higher‑codimension settings it would scale polynomially.

Fig. 2The Whitney decomposition of $(-1,1)^2$, top‑down view. Each cell is one $Q_k$; color encodes the level. Squares accumulate dyadically toward the boundary. Three levels shown explicitly; finer levels would tile the visible margin near $\partial\Omega$.

A $C^\infty$ mollifier $\varphi$

We use the same one‑dimensional bump as in 1‑D, then take a tensor product. Set $$ g(t) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \displaystyle\frac{e^{-1/t}}{e^{-1/t}+e^{-1/(1-t)}}, & 0 < t < 1, \\[8pt] 0, & t \le 0, \\[2pt] 1, & t \ge 1, \end{array} \right. $$ which is $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$. Define $\psi(s) = g\!\left((3/4 - |s|)/(1/4)\right)$, the 1‑D bump that is $1$ on $[-1/2, 1/2]$ and $0$ outside $[-3/4, 3/4]$. The 2‑D bump is the product $$\varphi(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \psi(x_1) \,\psi(x_2).$$ By construction $0 \le \varphi \le 1$, with $\varphi = 1$ on $Q_0 = [-1/2, 1/2]^2$ and $\varphi = 0$ outside the slightly inflated square $Q_0^{*} = [-3/4, 3/4]^2$. Smoothness of $\varphi$ is inherited from $\psi$, and bounds on its derivatives follow from the product rule.

Fig. 3The 2‑D base mollifier $\varphi(x_1, x_2) = \psi(x_1)\psi(x_2)$. A square plateau over $Q_0$ where $\varphi \equiv 1$, smooth descent through the collar $Q_0^* \setminus Q_0$, identically zero beyond.

Localized mollifiers $\varphi_k$

For each Whitney cube $Q_k$ with center $x^k = (x^k_1, x^k_2)$ and side $\ell_k$, scale $\varphi$ to fit: $$\varphi_k(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \varphi\!\left(\frac{x_1 - x^k_1}{\ell_k}, \;\frac{x_2 - x^k_2}{\ell_k}\right) \;=\; \psi\!\left(\tfrac{x_1 - x^k_1}{\ell_k}\right)\,\psi\!\left(\tfrac{x_2 - x^k_2}{\ell_k}\right).$$ Then $\varphi_k = 1$ on $Q_k$ and $\varphi_k = 0$ outside $Q_k^{*} = (1+\varepsilon)Q_k$ (here $\varepsilon = 1/2$). The chain rule gives $$\bigl|\partial^\alpha \varphi_k(x)\bigr| \;\le\; A_\alpha\, \ell_k^{-|\alpha|}.$$ Below we plot the maximum of $\varphi_k$ over a few selected cubes — one at the center, four at level 1, two at level 2 — giving a sense of how each bump fits its scale.

Fig. 4The pointwise maximum of $\varphi_k$ over seven selected Whitney cubes (one of side $1$, four of side $1/4$, two of side $1/8$). Each plateau region is the cube itself; bumps near the boundary are correspondingly narrower.

Partition of unity

Sum and normalize. Set $$\Phi(x) \;=\; \sum_k \varphi_k(x), \qquad \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \frac{\varphi_k(x)}{\Phi(x)}, \qquad x \in \Omega.$$ Every $x \in \Omega$ lies in some $Q_k$ where $\varphi_k = 1$, so $\Phi \ge 1$. The upper bound is also sharp: at the corner where four adjacent plateaus meet (say $x = (1/2, 1/2)$), four distinct $\varphi_k$ are simultaneously equal to $1$. Hence $$1 \;\le\; \Phi(x) \;\le\; 4 \qquad \text{on}\quad \Omega.$$ The general $d$‑dimensional bound is $1 \le \Phi \le 2^d$. Tautologically $\sum_k \varphi_k^{*} \equiv 1$ on $\Omega$, giving a $C^\infty$ partition of unity subordinate to $\{Q_k^*\}$.

Fig. 5The unnormalized sum $\Phi(x_1, x_2) = \sum_k \varphi_k$. Bounded between $1$ and $4$ on $\Omega$, with maxima at the corners of cubes where four plateaus overlap.

Fig. 6One element $\varphi_k^*(x_1, x_2)$ of the partition of unity, here for $k$ corresponding to the level‑1 cube centered at $(0.625,\,0)$. Smooth, non‑negative, supported in $Q_k^*$, and one piece of a partition that sums identically to $1$.

The regularized distance $\tilde d$

The construction is delivered in a single line: $$\boxed{\quad\tilde d(x) \;=\; \sum_k \operatorname{side}(Q_k)\,\varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \sum_k \ell_k\,\varphi_k^{*}(x), \qquad x \in \Omega.\quad}$$ At each point $x$, this is a smooth convex combination of the side lengths of the (few) Whitney cubes whose enlargements $Q_k^*$ contain $x$. Since each such cube has $\ell_k \asymp d(x)$, the resulting $\tilde d(x)$ is comparable to $d(x)$. The smoothness comes for free because $\varphi_k^*$ is smooth, and the gradient bound comes from the same calculation that worked in 1‑D — the $\ell_k^{-1}$ factor in $|\nabla \varphi_k|$ is exactly cancelled by the $\ell_k$ weight.

Fig. 7The regularized distance $\tilde d(x_1, x_2)$. Same overall pyramid shape as $d$, but the four ridges along $x_2 = \pm x_1$ have been smoothed out into rounded edges. Drag to rotate and compare with Fig. 1.

Verification of the three bounds

It remains to confirm numerically that $\tilde d$ satisfies the three Davies bounds. We do this in two views: a one‑dimensional slice along the diagonal $x_2 = x_1$ (where the kink of $d$ is most visible), and a heatmap of the pointwise ratio $\tilde d / d$.

Fig. 8Slice along the diagonal $x_2 = x_1$, where $d(t,t) = 1 - |t|$ has a sharp corner at $t = 0$. The smoothed curve $\tilde d(t,t)$ rounds the corner. Outside a neighborhood of the kink the two curves nearly coincide.

Fig. 9The pointwise ratio $\tilde d(x_1, x_2)/d(x_1, x_2)$ on $\Omega$ (computed away from $\partial\Omega$ where $d$ is too small for stable division). Bounded between two positive constants — a quantitative witness to comparability.

Fig. 10The gradient magnitude $|\nabla \tilde d(x_1, x_2)|$, computed by central differences. Finite throughout $\Omega$ — i.e., $\tilde d$ is uniformly Lipschitz. Numerical peaks reflect the same diameter‑ratio effect as in 1‑D.
