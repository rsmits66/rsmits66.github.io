An equilateral triangle of altitude 2, base AB. Split the base into 2n pieces to get triangles Tj=A2jA2j+2C; cut each at its midpoint A2j+1 and slide the right half over the left — a heart with two arms — then do it again. Press sprout and watch: because sliding never changes a segment's direction, the Perron tree still points a needle every way the original did, while its area falls toward 0.

During a step the left halves stay put while the sliding halves drift across them; the deepening crimson where they pile up is exactly where area is saved. The set area shrinks monotonically toward its infimum 0, never reached — the same fact that lets the Kakeya needle turn fully inside an arbitrarily small region with no minimal area.

An honest caveat. This elementary construction approaches 0 only very slowly (about 1/log of the piece count): even tens of thousands of triangles hover near a fifth of the original. Tiny ε is real in the limit but not renderable — the trend, not any finite frame, is the theorem.
