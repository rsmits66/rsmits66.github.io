The Regularized Distance Function on the Exterior of the Square

Smoothing the boundary distance on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus [-1,1]^2$. The obstacle is convex, so the raw distance is already $C^1$ — but not $C^2$. The cure is the same recipe, now run on dyadic cubes that shrink toward the square and grow toward infinity, and built from the classical bump function rather than a flat‑topped one.

For an open set $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ with complement $F = \mathbb{R}^N \setminus \Omega$, the distance function $d(x) = \min\{|x-y| : y \in F\}$ is Lipschitz of constant $1$ but typically not smooth. There is a $C^\infty$ replacement $\tilde d$ — the regularized distance — satisfying:

$$ c^{-1} d(x) \;\le\; \tilde d(x) \;\le\; c\,d(x), \qquad |\nabla \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c, \qquad |\Delta \tilde d(x)| \;\le\; c\, \tilde d(x)^{-1}. $$

We carry out the construction for the exterior of the closed unit square, $\Omega = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus [-1,1]^2$. Because the obstacle $F = [-1,1]^2$ is convex, the nearest‑point projection onto $F$ is single‑valued and varies continuously — so $d$ is in fact $C^1$ on all of $\Omega$. This is gentler than the interior square, where $d$ was a pyramid with genuine gradient kinks along a medial axis. Here the defect sits one derivative higher: the second derivatives of $d$ jump across the four corner rays, and the Laplacian blows up like $d^{-1}$ as one approaches a corner of the square. The regularization produces a genuinely $C^\infty$ companion whose Laplacian growth is no worse than the optimal rate $\tilde d^{-1}$.

The recipe is unchanged: a Whitney decomposition into dyadic cubes, a smooth mollifier, a partition of unity, then the weighted sum of cube diameters.

The distance function $d(x_1, x_2)$

For $\Omega = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus [-1,1]^2$, the distance from a point to the closed square is the classical distance to a box:

$$ d(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \sqrt{\bigl(|x_1| - 1\bigr)_+^{2} \;+\; \bigl(|x_2| - 1\bigr)_+^{2}}\,, \qquad (t)_+ := \max(t,\,0). $$

The exterior splits into eight pieces. In the four edge regions — directly N, S, E, W of the square — the nearest boundary point lies on a side and $d$ is linear; e.g. on the east region $\{x_1 > 1,\ |x_2| \le 1\}$ one has $d = x_1 - 1$. In the four corner regions — the diagonal quadrants — the nearest point is a vertex of the square and $d$ is radial; e.g. on the NE region $\{x_1 > 1,\ x_2 > 1\}$ one has $d = \sqrt{(x_1-1)^2 + (x_2-1)^2}$.

Across each corner ray (such as $\{x_1 = 1,\ x_2 > 1\}$, separating an edge region from a corner region) both $d$ and $\nabla d$ match — the gradient is the continuous unit vector pointing away from the nearest boundary point. But the Hessian does not match: $\Delta d = 0$ on the linear edge regions, while $\Delta d = 1/d$ on the radial corner regions, and $1/d \to \infty$ at the four vertices. So $d \in C^1(\Omega) \setminus C^2(\Omega)$ — exactly the regularity gap the bound $|\Delta\tilde d| \le c\,\tilde d^{-1}$ is designed to control.

Whitney decomposition of the exterior

We tile $\Omega$ by closed dyadic squares $\{Q_k\}$ with disjoint interiors and side lengths comparable to the distance from the boundary, $$\operatorname{side}(Q_k) \;\asymp\; \operatorname{dist}(Q_k,\, \partial\Omega).$$ The exterior is unbounded, so the decomposition runs both ways: cubes shrink geometrically toward the square and grow geometrically toward infinity. We tile $\Omega$ by square annular frames, each one cube thick, with side doubling from one frame to the next:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Frame } 0:\quad & \|x\|_\infty \in [2,\,3], & \ell &= 1, & 20 \text{ squares.} \\ \text{Frame } 1:\quad & \|x\|_\infty \in [\tfrac32,\,2], & \ell &= \tfrac12, & 28 \text{ squares.} \\ \text{Frame } 2:\quad & \|x\|_\infty \in [\tfrac54,\,\tfrac32], & \ell &= \tfrac14, & 44 \text{ squares.} \\ \text{Frame } 3:\quad & \|x\|_\infty \in [\tfrac98,\,\tfrac54], & \ell &= \tfrac18, & 76 \text{ squares.} \\ & \;\;\;\vdots \end{aligned} $$

Each frame is obtained from the next by stepping outward a distance equal to its own side length. Toward the square the frames continue indefinitely ($\ell = \tfrac1{16}, \tfrac1{32}, \dots$, with $140, 268, \dots$ squares); toward infinity they continue with $\ell = 2, 4, \dots$. The number of squares per frame grows linearly as one descends — characteristic of a co‑dimension‑$1$ boundary.

Fig. 2The Whitney decomposition of $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus [-1,1]^2$, top‑down. The dark block is the obstacle $F$; each cell is one $Q_k$, colored by frame. Four frames are drawn; finer frames tile the visible collar near $\partial\Omega$, coarser frames continue past the window.

The standard mollifier $\phi$

For the bump we use the classical mollifier of distribution theory — the function everyone meets first, and the one whose properties are best understood:

$$ \phi(s) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \displaystyle e^{-1/(1 - s^2)}, & -1 < s < 1, \\[8pt] 0, & |s| \ge 1. \end{array} \right. $$

$\phi$ is $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$, supported exactly on $[-1,1]$, and has a single maximum $\phi(0) = e^{-1}$. The two‑dimensional base bump is the tensor product $$\varphi(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \phi(x_1)\,\phi(x_2),$$ which is $C^\infty$, supported on the open square $(-1,1)^2$, with a single peak $\varphi(0,0) = e^{-2}$.

Unlike the flat‑topped bump often used in this construction, $\phi$ has no plateau: it is strictly below its maximum at every point but the origin. We will see in Step 5 that this costs only a cosmetic piece of bookkeeping — the partition of unity, and every estimate that matters, go through unchanged.

Fig. 3The $2$‑D base mollifier $\varphi(x_1, x_2) = \phi(x_1)\,\phi(x_2)$. A single smooth peak of height $e^{-2}$, decaying to zero — together with all its derivatives — at the edge of the unit square.

Localized mollifiers $\varphi_k$

For each Whitney cube $Q_k$ with center $x^k = (x^k_1, x^k_2)$ and side $\ell_k$, we scale $\varphi$ to sit on a slightly enlarged cube. With dilation factor $1+\varepsilon$ (we take $\varepsilon = 1$), $$\varphi_k(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \phi\!\left(\frac{2(x_1 - x^k_1)}{(1+\varepsilon)\,\ell_k}\right)\;\phi\!\left(\frac{2(x_2 - x^k_2)}{(1+\varepsilon)\,\ell_k}\right).$$ Then $\varphi_k$ is supported on the enlarged cube $Q_k^{*} = (1+\varepsilon)\,Q_k$ and is strictly positive on its interior — in particular on all of the cube $Q_k$ itself, which sits well inside $Q_k^*$. The chain rule gives the scale‑aware derivative bounds $$\bigl|\partial^\alpha \varphi_k(x)\bigr| \;\le\; A_\alpha\, \ell_k^{-|\alpha|}.$$ Below we plot the pointwise maximum of $\varphi_k$ over a handful of cubes drawn from every frame — bumps that grow narrower as their cubes approach the square.

Fig. 4The pointwise maximum of $\varphi_k$ over six selected Whitney cubes spanning all four drawn frames. Each bump is a single smooth hump — no plateau — scaled to its cube; bumps near the obstacle are correspondingly narrower and steeper.

Partition of unity

Sum and normalize. Set $$\Phi(x) \;=\; \sum_k \varphi_k(x), \qquad \varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \frac{\varphi_k(x)}{\Phi(x)}, \qquad x \in \Omega.$$ Every $x \in \Omega$ lies in some Whitney cube $Q_k$, and there $\varphi_k(x) > 0$ — so $\Phi(x) > 0$ everywhere on $\Omega$ and the quotient is well defined and $C^\infty$. Tautologically $\sum_k \varphi_k^{*} \equiv 1$, a $C^\infty$ partition of unity subordinate to the enlarged cubes $\{Q_k^*\}$.

With a flat‑topped bump one gets the tidy bound $1 \le \Phi \le 2^d$ by counting overlapping plateaus. The standard mollifier has no plateau, so $\Phi$ is no longer pinned to integers. But that bound was never essential: the Whitney decomposition has bounded overlap — only finitely many enlarged cubes meet at any point, and their sizes stay comparable — so $\Phi$ is still squeezed between two positive constants $0 < m \le \Phi \le M$. That is all the construction asks of it. The numerical $\inf$ and $\sup$ over the window are reported on the next two figures.

The regularized distance $\tilde d$

The construction is delivered in a single line: $$\boxed{\quad\tilde d(x) \;=\; \sum_k \operatorname{side}(Q_k)\,\varphi_k^{*}(x) \;=\; \sum_k \ell_k\,\varphi_k^{*}(x), \qquad x \in \Omega.\quad}$$ At each point $x$, this is a smooth convex combination of the side lengths of the few Whitney cubes whose enlargements $Q_k^*$ contain $x$. Since each such cube has $\ell_k \asymp d(x)$, the result $\tilde d(x)$ is comparable to $d(x)$. Smoothness is free, because every $\varphi_k^*$ is $C^\infty$; the gradient bound comes from the cancellation of the $\ell_k^{-1}$ in $|\nabla\varphi_k|$ against the $\ell_k$ weight.

Verification of the bounds

We confirm numerically that $\tilde d$ behaves. Three views: a one‑dimensional slice across two corner rays, the pointwise ratio $\tilde d / d$, and the gradient magnitude.

Fig. 8Slice along $x_2 = \tfrac32$, which crosses the corner rays at $x_1 = \pm 1$. The raw $d$ (dotted) is flat over the edge region and curved over the corner regions: continuous in value and slope, but with a jump in curvature at $x_1 = \pm 1$. The regularized $\tilde d$ (crimson) is smooth throughout and comparable to $d$ — equal up to a fixed factor, not identically equal.

Fig. 9The pointwise ratio $\tilde d / d$, shown on an annular band around the square (the near‑rim sliver and the far field are masked, where the finite drawn decomposition no longer resolves the local scale). Trapped between two positive constants — a quantitative witness to comparability.

Fig. 10The gradient magnitude $|\nabla \tilde d(x_1, x_2)|$, by central differences. Finite throughout $\Omega$ — so $\tilde d$ is uniformly Lipschitz — with mild ridges along frame interfaces, the same dyadic diameter‑ratio effect seen in lower dimensions.
