§2. Haar functions on dyadic intervals

Definition.

The Haar function on a dyadic interval I is

hI(x) = (1/√|I|) (χIr(x) − χIl(x)),

a step function with ∫hI = 0 and ∫|hI|² = 1. As I ranges over a dyadic grid 𝒟 the family {hI} is an orthonormal basis of L²(ℝ) — the basis used in §3 and §4.

What you're seeing. The dark curve is f, the crimson step function is the partial sum f𝒟(x) = ΣI ⟨f, hI⟩ hI(x) restricted to the four levels j ∈ {−1, 0, 1, 2} of the current grid 𝒟β,r. Coefficients ⟨f, hI⟩ are computed by midpoint integration on each I.

Truncation, scaling, and offset. The full series in the formula is over all dyadic I; truncating to j ≤ 2 leaves out the coarse-scale "scaling" component, so for f with non-zero mean over the window (e.g. x²/8, e^(−x²), χ_[0,2)) the partial sum is offset from f by roughly that mean. For mean-zero f (linear, sine, the Haar atom) the approximation hugs the curve.

Why Ш makes sense as a "discrete H". Petermichl's shift maps each Haar function hI to the difference (hIl − hIr)/√2 of its children's Haars — a discrete version of taking a derivative across the midpoint. The full Hilbert transform is recovered as an average of these shifts over the random dyadic grid family from §1: averaging breaks the artificial dyadic structure and restores translation invariance.

Convergence has two pieces. The error decomposes as ‖HN f − Hf‖₂ ≤ (statistical fluctuation) + (truncation bias from using only nine dyadic scales), and the two pieces have independent rates.

Statistical, ∝ 1/√N. Шβ,r is a partial isometry on the Haar series — every hI is sent to a unit-norm image and the images are pairwise orthogonal across I — so ‖Шβ,r f‖₂ ≤ ‖f‖₂ uniformly in (β, r). Independence of samples gives the standard Monte Carlo rate ‖HN f − 𝔼 HN f‖₂ ≲ (8 ln 2 / π) ‖f‖₂ / √N ≈ 1.77 ‖f‖₂ / √N. So N = 100 produces noise of order ‖f‖₂/10 — the visible wobble; N = 10⁴ drops it well below the line width. Pointwise the same √N rate holds by CLT, with the constant being the standard deviation of the scalar Шβ,r f(x).

Truncation, ∝ 2−Ls. Dropping Haar coefficients ⟨f, hI⟩ at scales |I| ∉ [2−L, 2L] leaves a fixed bias whose size is set by the Haar / Besov regularity of f. The smooth presets (Gaussian, Lorentzian, x/(1+x²)) sit in every Sobolev space, so their bias decays like 2−Ls for any s — at L = 4 already invisible. Indicators have jump discontinuities (regularity Ḣ1/2−ε), giving the much slower rate 2−L/2: that's the ~10% residual near |x| = 3 that does not shrink no matter how many samples you add. Click +100 a dozen times on the Lorentzian and the curve locks onto Hf to within a fraction of a percent; do the same on the indicator and the edges stay visibly thick — the thickness is bias, not noise.

Hytönen's representation. Petermichl's identity is the prototype: in 2012 Hytönen proved that every Calderón–Zygmund operator is an average of dyadic shifts of bounded complexity, with convergence in operator norm on L² and on the relevant weighted spaces. The statistical-plus-truncation rate decomposition above is universal across that family, and reducing sharp weighted bounds for H to bounds for the discrete shifts is the route by which the A2 conjecture was settled.
