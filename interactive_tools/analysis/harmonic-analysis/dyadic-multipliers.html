§ 04   what to look for

Three things to notice.

The frequency content selects itself. The bright spots of |f̂| sit in particular dyadic tiles. Click a tile that contains a bright spot: |Sρf| rises to a non-trivial envelope. Click a tile that's empty: |Sρf| flattens to nearly zero. Reading off which tiles are non-empty is reading off the frequency profile of f.

The diagonal grating is a delta. Picking the cosine grating cos(6x + 4y), the spectrum collapses to two single points, ±(6, 4). Only the two tiles containing these points respond; every other dyadic tile sees zero envelope.

Sharp edges spread. The disk indicator has a slowly-decaying spectrum (a Bessel function of the magnitude). Many tiles contribute non-trivially — each one a different band-limited envelope. This is the resolution of the disk into Littlewood–Paley pieces.

§ 05   on the magnitude

Why the surface is the envelope.

For real f, the Fourier transform satisfies f̂(−ξ) = f̂(ξ). Because ρ is asymmetric about the origin, χρ·f̂ breaks this conjugate symmetry, so Sρf is genuinely complex-valued. Its real part oscillates inside the band; its modulus does not. We display |Sρf|, the envelope of the band-limited piece.

Try the diagonal grating cos(6x + 4y) and click the tile containing +(6, 4): the envelope is essentially constant. The oscillation is concentrated entirely in the phase, not the modulus — the projection onto a one-sided band is a complex exponential whose magnitude doesn't move.

§ 06   the identity

Watching f assemble itself.

Switch to Accumulate ρ mode. Now clicks add tiles to a running set rather than replacing the selection, the third panel shows the partial reconstruction Re(Σρ ∈ S Sρf), and the band-energy bar marches toward 100% as you fill in the spectrum. Hit Select all and the partial reconstruction snaps to f — to within the Nyquist row, which carries no measurable energy for any of the smooth test functions.

Three tiles outside the dyadic grid bring this reconstruction to completion: the central DC square at the origin (bin (0, 0)) carrying the mean of f, and two cross strips along the ξ2 = 0 and ξ1 = 0 axes. For the Gaussian bump, DC alone carries 13% of the total energy — try selecting only DC, or select everything except DC, to see the offset that produces.

The progress bar is the L² identity made visible: ‖f‖22 = Σ ‖Sρf‖22. By orthogonality (each band lives in a disjoint Fourier support), the partial sum's energy is simply the sum of the chosen tiles' energies — no overlap, no overshoot. That's why the bar reaches exactly 100% at the limit, and why ‖f − f̃‖22 equals the complement.
