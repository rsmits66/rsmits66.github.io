§ I. The setup

A set $A$ is countable when there exists a bijection $f \colon \mathbb{N} \to A$ — equivalently, when its elements can be written as a list $f(1),\, f(2),\, f(3),\, \ldots$ with no repetitions and no omissions. The set we will work with throughout this project is the positive rationals,

$$\mathbb{Q}^+ = \bigl\{\, p/q : p, q \in \mathbb{N},\ \gcd(p, q) = 1 \,\bigr\},$$

understood as fractions in lowest terms. It is a standard theorem — one you may already have seen — that $\mathbb{Q}^+$ is countable.

What is far less standard is that there are many genuinely different bijections $f \colon \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+$, and the choice of bijection has dramatic visual and structural consequences. In this project you will study two such bijections in detail. Both are classical; both are correct; and they look almost nothing alike.

§ II. The Calkin–Wilf enumeration

The first bijection comes from a binary tree of fractions, introduced by Neil Calkin and Herbert Wilf in 2000.

Definition (Calkin–Wilf tree)

The Calkin–Wilf tree is the infinite binary tree whose root is the fraction $1/1$, and where each node $a/b$ has

$$\text{left child} = \frac{a}{a + b}, \qquad \text{right child} = \frac{a + b}{b}.$$

The Calkin–Wilf sequence $(x_n)_{n \ge 1}$ is the sequence obtained by reading the tree in breadth-first order: $x_1 = 1/1$, then the next two terms are the children of $x_1$, then the next four terms are the grandchildren, and so on.

The first sixteen terms read

$$\tfrac{1}{1},\ \tfrac{1}{2},\ \tfrac{2}{1},\ \tfrac{1}{3},\ \tfrac{3}{2},\ \tfrac{2}{3},\ \tfrac{3}{1},\ \tfrac{1}{4},\ \tfrac{4}{3},\ \tfrac{3}{5},\ \tfrac{5}{2},\ \tfrac{2}{5},\ \tfrac{5}{3},\ \tfrac{3}{4},\ \tfrac{4}{1},\ \tfrac{1}{5}.$$

What makes this tree extraordinary is the following theorem, which we accept without proof for the purposes of this project. (You are welcome to attempt the proof yourself; it is short and very pretty.)

Theorem (Calkin & Wilf, 2000)

The map $n \mapsto x_n$ is a bijection from $\mathbb{N}$ onto $\mathbb{Q}^+$. That is, every positive rational $p/q$ in lowest terms appears exactly once in the Calkin–Wilf sequence.

Two computational tools will recur in the project tasks. The first is the parent map, which takes a node back to its predecessor in the tree:

$$\frac{a}{b} \;\longmapsto\; \begin{cases}\dfrac{a}{\,b - a\,} & \text{if } a < b \quad (\text{node is a left child}),\\[6pt] \dfrac{a - b}{b} & \text{if } a > b \quad (\text{node is a right child}).\end{cases}$$

The case $a = b$ combined with $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ forces $a = b = 1$, the root, where iteration stops. The second tool is an algebraic shortcut due to Newman: the entire sequence can be generated without ever materializing the tree.

Newman’s recurrence

The Calkin–Wilf sequence satisfies

$$x_1 = 1, \qquad x_{n+1} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\lfloor x_n \rfloor + 1 - x_n}.$$

Three features of Fig. 1 deserve attention before you begin the project. First, most points cluster in $[0, 1]$. This is not an accident: the left subtree of the root contains exactly the rationals $< 1$, and breadth-first traversal visits half of every level there. Second, there is a hard ceiling: the maximum of $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ never exceeds $\lfloor \log_2(n+1) \rfloor$. Third, the plot has a reciprocal mirror symmetry across the line $y = 1$ — if you replaced every $x_n > 1$ by $1/x_n$, the upper half would collapse onto the lower half exactly.

§ III. Cantor’s anti-diagonal enumeration

The second bijection is older and conceptually simpler. The idea is to organize fractions $p/q$ by a complexity parameter that bounds both the numerator and the denominator at once: the sum $s = p + q$.

Definition (Cantor’s anti-diagonal enumeration)

For each $s = 2, 3, 4, \ldots$ in turn, list the reduced fractions $p/q$ with $p + q = s$ and $p, q \ge 1$, in increasing order of $p$. Concatenating these lists gives an enumeration of $\mathbb{Q}^+$.

The first dozen terms read

$$1,\ \tfrac{1}{2},\ 2,\ \tfrac{1}{3},\ 3,\ \tfrac{1}{4},\ \tfrac{2}{3},\ \tfrac{3}{2},\ 4,\ \tfrac{1}{5},\ 5,\ \tfrac{1}{6},\ \ldots\,.$$

The visual signature of Fig. 2 is a cascade of ascending fans: each anti-diagonal block contributes one fan that climbs from very small (near $1/s$) to very large (near $s$). Successive fans climb higher and higher, and there is no upper envelope at all — while Fig. 1 stays below $\log_2(n + 1)$, Fig. 2’s upper envelope grows like $\sqrt{n}$.

A useful observation. The number of reduced fractions on the anti-diagonal $p + q = s$ equals $\varphi(s)$, the Euler totient function. (You will be asked to prove this in Task IV.) Consequently the fans alternate length according to the totient — short at composites with small $\varphi(s)$, long at primes.

§ IV. The moral

Both Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 depict bijections $\mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+$. Both are valid certificates that $\mathbb{Q}^+$ is countable. Yet they are radically different sequences. Calkin–Wilf has a logarithmic ceiling, a reciprocal mirror symmetry, and an algebraic recurrence so clean it can be inverted by reading the binary expansion of $n$. Cantor has unbounded growth, a totient-driven block structure, and a positional formula that reduces to a $\gcd$ computation.

The two pictures share the same set, the same cardinality, and the same theorem — and almost no other features.

This is the lesson worth carrying away. When you write $\lvert A \rvert = \aleph_0$, you are not committing to any particular ordering of $A$. You are asserting only that the set of orderings is non-empty — in fact, uncountably so. The eye prefers Cantor (visible structure, predictable fans); the algebra prefers Calkin–Wilf (the recurrence is breathtakingly clean). Existence of a bijection is the whole content of countability. Every other property — the envelope, the symmetry, the density profile — is a feature of the particular bijection, not of the underlying countability claim.

§ V. Project tasks

The following six tasks form the substance of the project. Each is followed by a hint set off in italics; please attempt the task on your own before reading the hint. Tasks I–V are required; Task VI is open-ended and must be addressed in your write-up but is graded on quality of investigation rather than on a single correct answer.

  1. Sixteen Calkin–Wilf terms. Compute $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{16}$ in lowest terms by hand, and verify that the sixteen fractions you obtain are all distinct. Then verify by direct comparison that your list matches the sixteen fractions displayed in § II.
    Hint. Do not iterate Newman’s recurrence in floating point: rounding error accumulates so quickly that the sequence diverges from its true values within roughly ten steps. Instead, run an exact integer recurrence on the pair $(a_n, b_n)$ where $x_n = a_n / b_n$. The Newman recurrence translates to $$a_{n+1} = b_n, \qquad b_{n+1} = b_n\bigl(2\lfloor a_n / b_n \rfloor + 1\bigr) - a_n.$$ You can verify by induction that this preserves $\gcd(a_n, b_n) = 1$, so distinct pairs $(a_n, b_n)$ correspond to distinct fractions in lowest terms.
  2. Locating fractions in the Calkin–Wilf order. Find the index $n$ for which $x_n = 3/7$ in the Calkin–Wilf sequence. Then find the index for which $x_n = 7/3$. State the relationship between the two indices and prove that it must hold for every reciprocal pair $a/b$ and $b/a$ at the same depth in the tree.
    Hint. Use the parent map. Iterate from $a/b$ back to the root $1/1$, recording a $0$ for each step in which $a < b$ (the node is a left child) and a $1$ for each step in which $a > b$ (the node is a right child). Reverse the recorded bit string, prepend a $1$ for the root, and read in binary — that is $n$. The reciprocal symmetry says: if $n$ has binary expansion $1\, b_1 b_2 \ldots b_L$, then $1/x_n$ sits at the index whose expansion is $1\, \overline{b_1}\overline{b_2}\ldots\overline{b_L}$. Equivalently, the two indices differ by an XOR with $2^L - 1$.
  3. The logarithmic ceiling. Prove that $x_{2^k - 1} = k$ for every $k \ge 1$, where $x_n$ denotes the $n$th term of the Calkin–Wilf sequence. Conclude that $$\max_{1 \le i \le n} x_i \;=\; \lfloor \log_2(n + 1) \rfloor.$$
    Hint. The right child of $a/b$ is $(a + b)/b$. Iterating from the root $1/1$ produces the sequence $2/1,\, 3/1,\, 4/1, \ldots$ — the “right spine” of the tree. In breadth-first order the rightmost node at depth $k$ (measuring depth from $0$ at the root) sits at position $2^{k+1} - 1$. To prove the maximum claim, observe that any node at depth $\le k$ lies on a path with at most $k$ right-moves and any number of left-moves — and a left-move from $a/b$ produces $a/(a + b)$, which is strictly less than $1$ for any $a, b \ge 1$.
  4. Counting Cantor’s anti-diagonals. Prove that for every $s \ge 2$, the number of reduced fractions $p/q$ with $p + q = s$ and $p, q \ge 1$ is exactly $\varphi(s)$, the Euler totient function. Use this to write a closed-form expression for the position of any reduced fraction $p/q$ in Cantor’s anti-diagonal enumeration of $\mathbb{Q}^+$.
    Hint. For the count: notice that $\gcd(p, q) = \gcd(p, p + q) = \gcd(p, s)$, so reduced anti-diagonal pairs are in bijection with integers $p \in \{1, 2, \ldots, s - 1\}$ satisfying $\gcd(p, s) = 1$ — exactly the count $\varphi(s)$. For the position formula, the index of $p/q$ in the enumeration is $$\bigg(\sum_{s' = 2}^{p + q - 1} \varphi(s')\bigg) + \#\bigl\{\,p' < p : \gcd(p',\, p + q) = 1\,\bigr\} + 1.$$ The first sum counts entries on all earlier anti-diagonals; the second counts entries on your own anti-diagonal that come before you.
  5. Universal density and divergence. Prove that for every bijection $f \colon \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+$, the following hold:
    1. $(f(n))$ is dense in $(0, \infty)$;
    2. $(f(n))$ does not converge;
    3. $\liminf_{n \to \infty} f(n) = 0$ and $\limsup_{n \to \infty} f(n) = +\infty$.
    Conclude that the qualitative differences you observe between Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 are differences in how these properties are realized, not in whether they are realized.
    Hint. Surjectivity is the key tool. For (a), every nonempty open interval $(\alpha, \beta) \subset (0, \infty)$ contains some rational, which must therefore equal $f(n)$ for some $n$. For (b), suppose $f(n) \to L$; then for large $n$ all values cluster within $\varepsilon$ of $L$, but $\mathbb{Q}^+$ contains infinitely many points outside $(L - \varepsilon, L + \varepsilon)$, each of which must be hit at some index, contradicting the cluster condition. (c) is a strengthening: for every $M > 0$, the set $\{r \in \mathbb{Q}^+ : r > M\}$ is infinite, so $f$ visits it at infinitely many indices, forcing $\limsup = +\infty$. Symmetric argument for $\liminf = 0$ using the set $\{r : r < 1/M\}$.
  6. Open investigation: log-scale comparison. Re-plot both Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 with the vertical axis on a logarithmic scale — that is, plot $(n, \log x_n)$ instead of $(n, x_n)$. What new symmetries become visible? In particular, what does the Calkin–Wilf reciprocal mirror look like under $y \mapsto \log y$, and how does the visual character of the Cantor enumeration change? Discuss whether the log-scale plots make the two enumerations look more alike or less alike, and explain why.
    Hint. Under $y \mapsto \log y$, reciprocals $r$ and $1/r$ become $\log r$ and $-\log r$ — reflections across the new horizontal axis $y = 0$. So the Calkin–Wilf reciprocal mirror becomes a literal axial symmetry of the plot. For Cantor: each anti-diagonal $p + q = s$ comes with reciprocal partners $(p, q) \leftrightarrow (q, p)$, so the Cantor plot is also axially symmetric in log-scale — but the symmetry is within each anti-diagonal block rather than between halves of the whole sequence. A strong write-up will distinguish symmetries that any bijection $\mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+$ must exhibit (in some form) from symmetries particular to the chosen bijection.
