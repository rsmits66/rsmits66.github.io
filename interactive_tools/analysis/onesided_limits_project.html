Piecewise & One-Sided
Limits

A Project in Real Analysis

When a function is defined piecewise, when does it have a limit at the seam? And how does that question relate to one-sided limits?

in the inquiry-based tradition

To the student

This project has five problems. The first is the most important — a general "gluing" theorem about limits of functions defined piecewise. Problems 2 through 5 are an extended application: they unfold one-sided limits and show that the gluing theorem, applied to a function broken at $x_0$ into its left and right halves, recovers the familiar relationship between one-sided and two-sided limits.

You are expected to write out a complete proof of every numbered problem and to give the requested examples in full. The hint boxes are nudges, not solutions; resist the urge to read them until you have wrestled with the statement on your own.

1.The piecewise gluing theorem

Theorem.

Suppose $f \colon A \to \R$ and $g \colon B \to \R$ are such that $A \cap B = \emptyset$, and $x_0$ is an accumulation point of $A$ and also an accumulation point of $B$. Define $h \colon A \cup B \to \R$ by $$h(x) = \begin{cases} f(x) & \text{if } x \in A, \\ g(x) & \text{if } x \in B. \end{cases}$$ Then $h$ has a limit at $x_0$ if and only if $f$ and $g$ each have a limit at $x_0$ and $$\lim_{x \to x_0} f(x) \;=\; \lim_{x \to x_0} g(x).$$

Problem 1 Prove the theorem.
Hint The two directions are independent.
  • ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\lim_{x \to x_0} h(x) = L$. Given $\eps > 0$, take the $\delta$ that works for $h$. For $x \in A$ with $0 < |x - x_0| < \delta$, what is $h(x)$? The same $\delta$ should work for $f$ — and the assumption that $x_0$ is an accumulation point of $A$ is what guarantees there are such $x$ in $A$. Repeat for $g$.
  • ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $f$ and $g$ both tend to $L$ at $x_0$. Get a $\delta_1$ from the limit of $f$ and a $\delta_2$ from the limit of $g$. Take $\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$ and split into cases $x \in A$ and $x \in B$ to get one bound that works for $h$ on all of $A \cup B$.
Why both accumulation hypotheses? They are exactly what makes the individual limits of $f$ and $g$ defined. Without accumulation of $A$ at $x_0$ there is no $f$-limit to talk about, only a vacuous statement about an empty set of nearby points; similarly for $B$. The theorem is therefore a clean if-and-only-if rather than a statement with hidden vacuous cases.

2.One-sided limits

Calculus textbooks routinely speak of one-sided limits. The gluing theorem above turns out to govern their relationship to ordinary two-sided limits, but to make the connection precise we first need careful definitions.

Definition Let $f \colon D \to \R$, and suppose $x_0$ is an accumulation point of $\{x \in D : x > x_0\}$. We say $f$ has a right-hand limit $L$ at $x_0$ provided that for every $\eps > 0$ there is a $\delta > 0$ such that $$x_0 < x < x_0 + \delta \text{ and } x \in D \;\Longrightarrow\; |f(x) - L| < \eps.$$ When this holds we write $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to x_0^+} f(x) = L$.
Problem 2 State carefully the definition of left-hand limit. Use the same template as the right-hand definition above, with the appropriate symmetric changes. Introduce notation $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to x_0^-} f(x)$ for the left-hand limit.
Hint Mirror the right-hand definition across $x_0$. The accumulation hypothesis is on $\{x \in D : x < x_0\}$, and the implication is $x_0 - \delta < x < x_0$ and $x \in D$ implies $|f(x) - L| < \eps$. The figure below shows the picture your definition should match.

3.Examples

The next two problems exhibit the two qualitative possibilities a function can display at a single point — one-sided limits that fail to agree, and one-sided limits that do.

Problem 3 Give an example of a function that has a left-hand limit and a right-hand limit at a point but does not have a (two-sided) limit at that point.
Hint Any "jump" works. The simplest is $f \colon \R \setminus \{0\} \to \R$ given by $f(x) = -1$ for $x < 0$ and $f(x) = 1$ for $x > 0$, taken at the point $x_0 = 0$. Compute both one-sided limits, then explain why no single $L$ can serve as a two-sided limit. (What happens with $\eps = \tfrac12$?)
Problem 4 Give an example of a function that has a left-hand limit and a right-hand limit at a point and also has a (two-sided) limit at that point.
Hint Any continuous function works at any point of its domain — try $f(x) = x^2$ at $x_0 = 0$. For something more revealing, define $f$ piecewise by two different formulas that happen to agree in the limit, e.g. $$f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 - x^2 & \text{if } x \le 0, \\ 1 + x^3 & \text{if } x > 0. \end{cases}$$ Compute both one-sided limits at $x_0 = 0$ and check that they match.

4.One-sided and two-sided limits

The examples in Problems 3 and 4 are not coincidences — they reflect a clean general principle, which Problem 5 asks you to state and prove.

Problem 5 State and prove a theorem that relates the left-hand limit and right-hand limit of a function to its (two-sided) limit. Be careful about the hypotheses your statement requires.
Hint The statement should be: under the appropriate accumulation hypotheses on the left and right of $x_0$, $f$ has a limit at $x_0$ if and only if both one-sided limits exist at $x_0$ and are equal.

For the proof, do not start over. Apply the gluing theorem of Problem 1 with $$A = \{x \in D : x < x_0\}, \qquad B = \{x \in D : x > x_0\},$$ taking the two pieces of $h$ to be $f$ restricted to $A$ and $f$ restricted to $B$. Verify that $A \cap B = \emptyset$, that the accumulation hypotheses match, and that "$h$ has a limit at $x_0$" is the same as "$f$ has a limit at $x_0$" — the value of $f$ at $x_0$ itself never enters the definition of a limit. Translate "$f|_A$ has a limit at $x_0$" into "$f$ has a left-hand limit at $x_0$", and similarly on the right. The conclusion of Problem 1 is now exactly the statement you want.
Notice how Problem 1 was not just a warm-up: it is the engine that makes Problem 5 a one-line consequence rather than a fresh $\eps$–$\delta$ argument. This is a recurring pattern in analysis. A well-stated general theorem about gluing and splitting often dispatches several specific cases at once, cleanly and without repetition.

What you have proved

Theorem (one-sided and two-sided limits).

Let $f \colon D \to \R$, and suppose $x_0$ is an accumulation point of both $\{x \in D : x < x_0\}$ and $\{x \in D : x > x_0\}$. Then $f$ has a limit at $x_0$ if and only if both $$\lim_{x \to x_0^-} f(x) \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to x_0^+} f(x)$$ exist and are equal, in which case their common value is $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to x_0} f(x)$.

This is the precise version of a fact that calculus courses usually present without proof: that one-sided limits "test" for a two-sided limit. The accumulation hypothesis on each side is what makes the test sharp; without it, one of the one-sided limits would be vacuous and the equivalence would degenerate.

The deeper takeaway is that this whole second half of the project rested on a single general theorem about gluing two functions at a common accumulation point. The cases left-of-$x_0$ and right-of-$x_0$ are just one particular application; Problem 1 covers many others as well — for instance, gluing the rationals to the irrationals, or gluing two sequences approaching $x_0$ from arbitrary directions. The pattern is the same every time.
