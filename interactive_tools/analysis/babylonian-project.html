Sequences and convergence

The Divide-and-Average Method

An ancient algorithm for computing square roots

The purpose of this project is to establish the convergence of a familiar iterative process for approximating square roots — the divide-and-average method. The idea is so natural that it was known to Babylonian mathematicians nearly four thousand years ago: if your current guess $a$ for $\sqrt{2}$ is too large, then $2/a$ will be too small; and averaging the two gives a new guess that is better than either.

Let $a_1 = 2$, and for $n \geq 2$ define $$a_n \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\!\left(a_{n-1} + \frac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right).$$ We will show that $\{a_n\}$ converges to $\sqrt{2}$.

Before proving anything, let us compute. Starting from $a_1 = 2$ and pressing the recursion forward gives the following sequence of rational approximations. The true value $\sqrt{2} = 1.41421356\ldots$ is shown for comparison.

Our job is to prove that this sequence converges (and to $\sqrt 2$) without appealing to the computation. The argument proceeds along a classical route: establish a lower bound for the sequence, show it is monotonically decreasing, invoke the Monotone Convergence Theorem to get some limit, and then identify that limit by passing to the limit in the recursion.

The four steps
  1. $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$ for all $n$
  2. The sequence is decreasing
  3. The sequence converges to $\sqrt{2}$
  4. Generalization to $\sqrt{a}$

PreliminariesThe strategy and a key tool

A bounded, monotone sequence of real numbers converges.

Step 1 shows $\{a_n\}$ is bounded below by $\sqrt{2}$; Step 2 shows it is decreasing. Together these give convergence via the Monotone Convergence Theorem. Step 3 identifies the limit by the standard technique of passing to the limit: if $a_n \to L$, then the recursion $a_n = \frac{1}{2}(a_{n-1} + 2/a_{n-1})$ implies $L = \frac{1}{2}(L + 2/L)$, and solving for $L$ pins down the value.

Figure 1A geometric picture of the iteration

The divide-and-average step has a geometric interpretation that makes its cleverness transparent. Imagine a rectangle of area $2$ with one side equal to your current guess $a$. Then the other side must be $2/a$. If $a$ is too large, the rectangle is long and thin; if $a$ is too small, it is short and stubby. A square of area $2$ has side $\sqrt{2}$. Averaging the two sides of the rectangle produces a new rectangle — closer to being a square than the one you started with.

The four stepsProofs to complete

Step 1

Prove that $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$ for all $n$.

The hint from the project: use algebra to write $\left(a_n + \tfrac{2}{a_n}\right)^2 - 8$ as a square, thereby establishing $\left(a_n + \tfrac{2}{a_n}\right)^2 \geq 8$.

Hint

First, a quick induction shows $a_n > 0$ for all $n$ (so you can freely divide by $a_n$). Next, expand $\left(a_n + \tfrac{2}{a_n}\right)^2 - 8$ and recombine to recognize the perfect square $\left(a_n - \tfrac{2}{a_n}\right)^2$. That identity is the algebraic heart of the proof.

For $n \geq 2$, the recursion says $a_n = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(a_{n-1} + \tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right)$, so $a_n^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}\!\left(a_{n-1} + \tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right)^2$. Use the bound on $\left(a_{n-1} + \tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right)^2$ to conclude $a_n^2 \geq 2$. Don't forget to check $a_1$ separately.

Solution

Every term is positive. We first note, by induction, that $a_n > 0$ for all $n$. The base case $a_1 = 2 > 0$ is immediate. If $a_{n-1} > 0$, then $\tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}} > 0$, so their average $a_n$ is positive as well.

The algebraic identity. For any $x > 0$, $$\left(x + \tfrac{2}{x}\right)^2 - 8 \;=\; x^2 + 2 \cdot x \cdot \tfrac{2}{x} + \tfrac{4}{x^2} - 8 \;=\; x^2 - 4 + \tfrac{4}{x^2} \;=\; \left(x - \tfrac{2}{x}\right)^2.$$ Since $\left(x - \tfrac{2}{x}\right)^2 \geq 0$, this gives $$\left(x + \tfrac{2}{x}\right)^2 \;\geq\; 8 \qquad \text{for all } x > 0. \quad (\star)$$

Conclusion. Check the base case directly: $a_1 = 2 \geq \sqrt{2}$. Now let $n \geq 2$ and apply $(\star)$ with $x = a_{n-1} > 0$: $$4 a_n^2 \;=\; \left(a_{n-1} + \tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right)^2 \;\geq\; 8,$$ so $a_n^2 \geq 2$. Since $a_n > 0$, taking positive square roots yields $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$. Q.E.D.

Step 2

Prove that $\{a_n\}$ is decreasing.

Show that $a_{n+1} \leq a_n$ for every $n \geq 1$. Use the bound you just proved in Step 1.

Hint

Compute $a_n - a_{n+1}$ directly from the recursion. You should be able to factor out $\tfrac{1}{2a_n}$ and be left with $a_n^2 - 2$, which is $\geq 0$ by Step 1.

Solution

Fix $n \geq 1$. We compute $$a_n - a_{n+1} \;=\; a_n - \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(a_n + \tfrac{2}{a_n}\right) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}a_n - \tfrac{1}{a_n} \;=\; \frac{a_n^2 - 2}{2 a_n}.$$

By Step 1, $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$, so $a_n^2 \geq 2$, i.e., $a_n^2 - 2 \geq 0$. Also $a_n > 0$, so $2 a_n > 0$. Therefore $$a_n - a_{n+1} \;=\; \frac{a_n^2 - 2}{2 a_n} \;\geq\; 0,$$ which gives $a_{n+1} \leq a_n$. The sequence is decreasing (monotonically non-increasing). Q.E.D.

Step 3

Prove that $\{a_n\}$ converges to $\sqrt{2}$.

Combine Steps 1 and 2 to get convergence; then pass to the limit in the recursion to identify the limit.

Hint

The Monotone Convergence Theorem gives you a limit $L$. To find $L$, note that since $a_n \to L$, also $a_{n-1} \to L$ (it's the same sequence, just reindexed). Take limits on both sides of the recursion $a_n = \tfrac{1}{2}(a_{n-1} + 2/a_{n-1})$. But first: why is $L \neq 0$, so that $2/a_{n-1} \to 2/L$ is legitimate?

Solution

Existence of the limit. From Step 1, the sequence is bounded below by $\sqrt{2}$. From Step 2, it is decreasing. By the Monotone Convergence Theorem, $\{a_n\}$ converges to some limit $L$: $$\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \;=\; L.$$ Moreover, $L \geq \sqrt{2}$ since every term satisfies $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$ and limits preserve weak inequalities. In particular $L > 0$, so dividing by $a_n$ (or by $L$) is safe.

Identifying the limit. The sequence $\{a_{n-1}\}$ is just $\{a_n\}$ reindexed, so it has the same limit $L$. Using the algebra of limits — the sum, constant multiple, and quotient rules (the quotient rule applies because $L > 0$): $$L \;=\; \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \;=\; \lim_{n \to \infty} \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(a_{n-1} + \tfrac{2}{a_{n-1}}\right) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(L + \tfrac{2}{L}\right).$$ Multiplying through by $2L$: $$2L^2 \;=\; L^2 + 2, \qquad \text{so} \qquad L^2 \;=\; 2.$$ Since $L > 0$, we conclude $L = \sqrt{2}$. Q.E.D.

Step 4

Generalize to $\sqrt{a}$ for any positive real number $a$.

State the generalized recursion, identify what must be modified in the arguments from Steps 1–3, and explain why they still work.

Hint

Replace every $2$ inside the recursion with $a$. The starting value must be positive and can be chosen freely — but for the "decreasing" and "bounded below by $\sqrt{a}$" arguments to work from step one, you want to start above $\sqrt{a}$. Any $b_1 \geq \sqrt{a}$ works; a convenient choice is $b_1 = \max(1, a)$ or simply $b_1 = a$ (when $a \geq 1$) or $b_1 = 1$ (when $a < 1$).

The algebraic identity from Step 1 becomes $\left(x + \tfrac{a}{x}\right)^2 - 4a = \left(x - \tfrac{a}{x}\right)^2$. Verify this.

Solution

Let $a > 0$. Choose any $b_1 > 0$ with $b_1 \geq \sqrt{a}$ (for instance $b_1 = a$ if $a \geq 1$, or $b_1 = 1$ if $0 < a < 1$), and define $$b_n \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(b_{n-1} + \tfrac{a}{b_{n-1}}\right) \qquad (n \geq 2).$$ We claim $b_n \to \sqrt{a}$. The argument mirrors Steps 1–3.

Generalized identity. For every $x > 0$, $$\left(x + \tfrac{a}{x}\right)^2 - 4a \;=\; x^2 + 2a + \tfrac{a^2}{x^2} - 4a \;=\; x^2 - 2a + \tfrac{a^2}{x^2} \;=\; \left(x - \tfrac{a}{x}\right)^2 \;\geq\; 0,$$ so $\left(x + \tfrac{a}{x}\right)^2 \geq 4a$.

Lower bound. An easy induction shows $b_n > 0$ for all $n$. For the base case, we assumed $b_1 \geq \sqrt{a}$. For $n \geq 2$, applying the identity with $x = b_{n-1}$: $$4 b_n^2 \;=\; \left(b_{n-1} + \tfrac{a}{b_{n-1}}\right)^2 \;\geq\; 4a, \qquad \text{so} \qquad b_n^2 \geq a,$$ giving $b_n \geq \sqrt{a}$ (since $b_n > 0$).

Decreasing. Exactly as in Step 2, $$b_n - b_{n+1} \;=\; \frac{b_n^2 - a}{2 b_n} \;\geq\; 0,$$ where the inequality uses $b_n^2 \geq a$ and $b_n > 0$.

Limit. The sequence is decreasing and bounded below by $\sqrt{a}$, so by the Monotone Convergence Theorem it converges to some limit $L \geq \sqrt{a} > 0$. Passing to the limit in the recursion, $$L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(L + \tfrac{a}{L}\right) \;\Longrightarrow\; 2L^2 = L^2 + a \;\Longrightarrow\; L^2 = a,$$ and so $L = \sqrt{a}$. Q.E.D.

Remark on the choice of $b_1$. Even if $b_1$ is chosen below $\sqrt{a}$, the next term $b_2$ automatically lands above $\sqrt{a}$ (because of the identity), and from $b_2$ onward the argument proceeds identically. So in fact any positive starting value $b_1 > 0$ works — the sequence merely takes one step to enter the regime where it is monotone decreasing.

EpilogueA calculus remark — why so fast

The table at the beginning of this project suggested that the number of correct digits doubles at each step. We can now see why, at least informally. If $\varepsilon_n = a_n - \sqrt{2}$ is the error at step $n$, a short calculation shows $$\varepsilon_{n+1} \;=\; \frac{\varepsilon_n^2}{2 a_n}.$$ The error at step $n+1$ is proportional to the square of the error at step $n$. So if at some point your error is about $10^{-6}$, the next step's error is about $10^{-12}$, then $10^{-24}$, and so on. This is the defining feature of quadratic convergence, and it is the reason the method remains the algorithm of choice inside modern floating-point square-root routines.

The divide-and-average iteration is the oldest known instance of what we now call Newton's method, applied to the function $f(x) = x^2 - a$. Newton's method in full generality produces quadratically convergent sequences for finding roots of differentiable functions. The Babylonians, four millennia ago, discovered the simplest nontrivial case.
