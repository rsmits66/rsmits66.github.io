A Note on Linear Algebra Vol. II

The Legendre Polynomials
by Gram–Schmidt

The monomials $1, x, x^2, x^3, \ldots$ are linearly independent on $[-1,1]$, but they aren't orthogonal. So orthogonalize them. What falls out is classical — and satisfies a beautiful three-term recurrence.

§ 01The Setup

Same inner product, new interval. On $C[-1, 1]$, put

$$\langle f, g \rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\, g(x)\, dx.$$

Start with the monomial sequence

$$v_0 = 1, \quad v_1 = x, \quad v_2 = x^2, \quad v_3 = x^3, \quad \ldots$$

These are linearly independent — no nontrivial linear combination of finitely many is the zero function. But they are not orthogonal:

$$\langle 1, x^2 \rangle = \int_{-1}^{1} x^2\,dx = \frac{2}{3} \neq 0.$$

So we run the Gram–Schmidt procedure. The interesting question is: what basis falls out?

§ 02The Procedure

Same Gram–Schmidt as anywhere else. We're going to keep the basis orthogonal rather than orthonormal — defer the square-root normalization until we actually need unit-norm vectors. The orthogonal projection formula carries a denominator:

$$u_k \;=\; v_k \;-\; \sum_{j=0}^{k-1}\, \frac{\langle v_k,\, u_j\rangle}{\langle u_j,\, u_j\rangle}\, u_j.$$

The $u_j$ produced by this process are mutually orthogonal but not unit-norm; the projection coefficients used to subtract earlier components are $\langle v_k, u_j\rangle / \|u_j\|^2$. When we eventually want to project a function $f$ onto this basis, we'll do

$$f \;\approx\; \sum_{n=0}^{N}\, a_n\, u_n, \qquad a_n \;=\; \frac{\langle f, u_n\rangle}{\|u_n\|^2}.$$

One observation pays for itself a dozen times below: on the symmetric interval $[-1, 1]$, the integral of an odd function vanishes. So whenever $v_k\, u_j$ is odd — i.e., $k + j$ is odd — that inner product is zero, and the term drops out. Even functions stay coupled to even functions; odd to odd.

§ 03Step by Step

Step 0 orthogonalize $1$

Nothing to subtract: $u_0 = 1$.

$$\|u_0\|^2 \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\!dx \;=\; 2.$$

Step 1 orthogonalize $x$

The only previous vector is $u_0 = 1$, and

$$\langle x, u_0 \rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} x\,dx \;=\; 0$$

(odd integrand, symmetric interval). So $u_1 = x$, with

$$\|u_1\|^2 \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} x^2\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{2}{3}.$$

Step 2 orthogonalize $x^2$

Two inner products to compute:

$$\langle x^2, u_0\rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\! x^2\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{2}{3}, \qquad \langle x^2, u_1\rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\! x^3\,dx \;=\; 0$$

(the second is odd). So

$$u_2 \;=\; x^2 \;-\; \frac{2/3}{2}\cdot 1 \;=\; x^2 - \tfrac{1}{3},$$

and

$$\|u_2\|^2 \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\!\bigl(x^2 - \tfrac{1}{3}\bigr)^2\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{2}{5} - \tfrac{4}{9} + \tfrac{2}{9} \;=\; \tfrac{8}{45}.$$

Step 3 orthogonalize $x^3$

By parity, $\langle x^3, u_0\rangle$ and $\langle x^3, u_2\rangle$ both vanish (each integrand is odd). The only surviving projection is onto $u_1$:

$$\langle x^3, u_1\rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\! x^4\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{2}{5}.$$

Subtracting,

$$u_3 \;=\; x^3 \;-\; \frac{2/5}{2/3}\, x \;=\; x^3 - \tfrac{3}{5}\, x, \qquad \|u_3\|^2 \;=\; \tfrac{8}{175}.$$

Switching to the classical normalization

The four polynomials above are monic — leading coefficient $1$ — because Gram–Schmidt on monic monomials produces monic outputs. They are perfectly good orthogonal polynomials, but they aren't quite the classical Legendre polynomials. The classical $P_n$ are the same polynomials rescaled so that $P_n(1) = 1$, which gives much cleaner integer coefficients downstream and makes the recurrence (next section) snap into integer form. Multiplying $u_n$ by the right scalar:

$$P_0 = 1, \qquad P_1 = x, \qquad P_2 = \tfrac{3}{2}\!\left(x^2 - \tfrac{1}{3}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}(3x^2 - 1), \qquad P_3 = \tfrac{5}{2}\!\left(x^3 - \tfrac{3}{5}x\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}(5x^3 - 3x).$$

And the norms scale by the square of that factor:

$$\|P_n\|^2 \;=\; \frac{2}{2n+1}.$$

Check: $\|P_2\|^2 = (3/2)^2 \cdot (8/45) = 9/4 \cdot 8/45 = 2/5$ ✓; $\|P_3\|^2 = (5/2)^2 \cdot (8/175) = 25/4 \cdot 8/175 = 2/7$ ✓.

§ 04The Three-Term Recurrence

Doing Gram–Schmidt by hand to get $P_4, P_5, P_6, \ldots$ is unbearable. We don't have to: every orthogonal polynomial system on a symmetric weight obeys a three-term recurrence, and for Legendre it is

Bonnet's Recurrence $$\boxed{\;(n+1)\, P_{n+1}(x) \;=\; (2n+1)\, x\, P_n(x) \;-\; n\, P_{n-1}(x).\;}$$

This is not a special trick. It's a consequence of a single linear-algebraic fact: the operator "multiply by $x$" is self-adjoint with respect to this inner product. That's all you need.

Proof

Two ingredients carry the entire argument.

(i) Multiplication by $x$ is self-adjoint. For any $f, g$,

$$\langle xf,\, g\rangle \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1}\! x\, f(x)\, g(x)\, dx \;=\; \langle f,\, xg\rangle.$$

(ii) $P_n$ is orthogonal to every polynomial of degree $< n$. Because any such polynomial is a linear combination of $P_0, P_1, \ldots, P_{n-1}$, each individually orthogonal to $P_n$ by construction.

Now consider $x P_n$. It is a polynomial of degree $n+1$, so it expands uniquely in the orthogonal basis $\{P_0, P_1, \ldots, P_{n+1}\}$:

$$x P_n \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{n+1} \alpha_k\, P_k, \qquad \alpha_k \;=\; \frac{\langle x P_n,\, P_k\rangle}{\|P_k\|^2}.$$

Most of the $\alpha_k$ are zero. For $k \leq n-2$, use (i) and then (ii):

$$\langle x P_n,\, P_k\rangle \;=\; \langle P_n,\, x P_k\rangle \;=\; 0,$$

because $x P_k$ has degree $k+1 \leq n-1$, and $P_n$ is orthogonal to every such polynomial. For $k = n$, parity finishes the job: $P_n^2$ has parity $2n$ (even), so $x P_n^2$ is odd, integrating to zero on $[-1, 1]$, hence $\alpha_n = 0$.

So only three coefficients survive:

$$x P_n \;=\; \alpha_{n+1} P_{n+1} \;+\; \alpha_{n-1} P_{n-1}.$$

A three-term recurrence — purely from self-adjointness and orthogonality, before we touched any specific formula. To extract the constants, match leading terms on each side. The leading coefficient of $P_n$ works out to $k_n = \binom{2n}{n}/2^n$ (by induction on the recurrence itself, with $k_0 = k_1 = 1$). The $x^{n+1}$ coefficient on the left is $k_n$; on the right it is $\alpha_{n+1} k_{n+1}$. A short computation gives

$$\alpha_{n+1} \;=\; \frac{k_n}{k_{n+1}} \;=\; \frac{n+1}{2n+1}.$$

For $\alpha_{n-1}$, use self-adjointness one more time: $\alpha_{n-1}\|P_{n-1}\|^2 = \langle xP_n, P_{n-1}\rangle = \langle P_n, xP_{n-1}\rangle$. Writing $xP_{n-1} = (k_{n-1}/k_n) P_n + (\text{lower})$, the inner product picks out the $P_n$ component, giving

$$\alpha_{n-1} \;=\; \frac{k_{n-1}}{k_n}\cdot\frac{\|P_n\|^2}{\|P_{n-1}\|^2} \;=\; \frac{n}{2n+1}.$$

Substituting and multiplying through by $(2n+1)$:

$$(2n+1)\, x\, P_n \;=\; (n+1)\, P_{n+1} \;+\; n\, P_{n-1},$$

which rearranges into the stated recurrence.

Now $P_4, P_5, P_6$ are arithmetic, not integration:

$$4 P_4 \;=\; 7 x P_3 \;-\; 3 P_2 \;\Longrightarrow\; P_4 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{8}\!\left(35 x^4 - 30 x^2 + 3\right),$$ $$5 P_5 \;=\; 9 x P_4 \;-\; 4 P_3 \;\Longrightarrow\; P_5 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{8}\!\left(63 x^5 - 70 x^3 + 15 x\right),$$ $$6 P_6 \;=\; 11 x P_5 \;-\; 5 P_4 \;\Longrightarrow\; P_6 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{16}\!\left(231 x^6 - 315 x^4 + 105 x^2 - 5\right).$$

You could continue indefinitely with nothing but multiplication and subtraction. The whole Legendre family — orthogonal polynomials Adrien-Marie Legendre wrote down in 1782 — falls out of one inner-product identity.

Each $P_n$ has parity $n$: even index → even function, odd index → odd. Each has $n$ zeros, all strictly inside $(-1, 1)$. Each satisfies $P_n(1) = 1$ and $P_n(-1) = (-1)^n$. The values oscillate between $-1$ and $+1$ on the interval, growing in frequency with $n$ — visible at a glance:

§ 06Approximating Other Functions

Once you have an orthogonal basis, projection is mechanical. For a function $f$ on $[-1, 1]$, the projection coefficients are

$$a_n \;=\; \frac{\langle f,\, P_n\rangle}{\|P_n\|^2} \;=\; \frac{2n+1}{2}\!\int_{-1}^{1}\! f(x)\, P_n(x)\, dx,$$

and the partial sum

$$S_N(x) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{N} a_n\, P_n(x)$$

is the best polynomial approximation of degree $\leq N$ to $f$ in the least-squares sense. Pick a function below, watch the partial sums march in.

A few coefficients, to make the rate of decay concrete. For $f(x) = e^x$, the integrals come out in closed form:

$$a_0 = \sinh(1) \approx 1.175, \quad a_1 = \frac{3}{e} \approx 1.104, \quad a_2 = \tfrac{5}{2}\!\left(e - \tfrac{7}{e}\right) \approx 0.357, \quad a_3 \approx 0.0703, \quad a_4 \approx 0.00996, \quad a_5 \approx 0.00110, \ldots$$

The coefficients of $e^x$ collapse geometrically fast. That is the signature of a smooth (analytic) function in an orthogonal polynomial expansion: spectral accuracy. Compare with $f(x) = |x|$, where the integrals are nicer and the answer is exact:

$$a_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad a_2 = \tfrac{5}{8}, \quad a_4 = -\tfrac{3}{16}, \quad a_6 = \tfrac{13}{128}, \quad a_8 = -\tfrac{17}{256}, \;\ldots$$

(the odd $a_n$ vanish because $|x|$ is even). These decay only like $1/n^2$ — far slower, because the kink at the origin is the slowest feature for smooth basis functions to resolve. For $\mathrm{sgn}(x)$, decay drops to $1/n$, and the partial sums never converge uniformly: Gibbs' phenomenon, this time wearing polynomial clothes.

§ 07The Moral

The Fourier basis and the Legendre basis look like wildly different objects — trig functions on a circle, polynomials on an interval. But the linear algebra underneath is identical. Pick an inner product, pick a sequence to orthogonalize, run Gram–Schmidt or (when the inputs are already orthogonal) read off the projection coefficients. Whether you land on $\sin nx, \cos nx$, $P_n(x)$, or some other classical family — Chebyshev, Hermite, Laguerre, each one is Gram–Schmidt with a different interval or weight — depends only on what you started with and what inner product you chose.

The three-term recurrence is the linear algebra paying you back. It is not a curiosity of Legendre polynomials specifically; it is what self-adjointness of multiplication-by-$x$ forces on any orthogonal polynomial system. The constants $\alpha_{n\pm 1}$ change family to family. The fact that there are only two of them never does.

The functions on $[-1, 1]$ form a vector space. It happens to be infinite-dimensional. Everything else is bookkeeping.
