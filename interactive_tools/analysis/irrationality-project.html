Well-ordering in action

On the Irrationality of √p for Non-Square p

A proof by infinite descent

The purpose of this project is to use the well-ordering principle to prove that certain square roots are irrational. The argument is a single, carefully engineered contradiction: assume the square root is rational and the well-ordering principle will hand you a smallest witness $m$; a little algebra then manufactures an even smaller witness, which cannot exist.

If $p$ is a positive integer that is not the square of an integer, and $x^2 = p$, then $x$ is irrational.

The theorem sweeps up a great many familiar irrationals in a single statement:

√2, √3, √5, √6, √7, √8, √10, √11, √12, √13, √14, √15, √17, √18,  …  are all irrational.

Only the values of $p$ that are perfect squares — $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, \ldots$ — produce integer (hence rational) roots.

The four steps
  1. $A$ is nonempty; extract the minimum $m$
  2. Locate $x$ between consecutive integers $k$ and $k{+}1$
  3. Construct $z = mx - mk$ and show $0 < z < m$
  4. Show $z \in A$ — the contradiction

PreliminariesTools and setup

Two preliminary observations and one indispensable tool power the entire argument.

Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{N}$ has a smallest element.

Since $1 = 1^2$ is itself a perfect square, the hypothesis that $p$ is not a perfect square forces $p \geq 2$. Consequently $x = \sqrt{p} \geq \sqrt{2} > 1$.

Assume, for contradiction, that $x$ is a positive rational number with $x^2 = p$. Define $$A = \{\,n \in \mathbb{N} : nx \text{ is an integer}\,\}.$$ The whole proof will revolve around the smallest element of $A$.

Before diving into the four steps, a picture of what is about to happen. Imagine the number $x$ sitting between two consecutive integers $k$ and $k+1$ on the number line. Its fractional part $x - k$ is some number strictly between $0$ and $1$. Now multiply everything by $m$. The interval $[k, k+1]$ is stretched into $[mk,\, mk + m]$, a stretch of length $m$. The fractional part becomes $z = m(x - k)$, still strictly between $0$ and $m$ — but now, miraculously, $z$ is also an integer:

The four stepsProofs to complete

Each step below is followed by a hint and a full solution. Work on each part yourself first; reveal the hint only when stuck, and the solution only to check.

Step 1

Show that $A$ is nonempty, and let $m$ be its smallest member.

Recall $A = \{n \in \mathbb{N} : nx \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ and that we are assuming $x$ is a positive rational. You must exhibit at least one element of $A$, then invoke the well-ordering principle.

Hint

The word rational is there to be unpacked. Write $x$ as a ratio of two positive integers — what does that immediately tell you about one specific multiple of $x$?

Solution

Since $x$ is a positive rational, we may write $x = a/b$ for some positive integers $a, b \in \mathbb{N}$. Then $$b \cdot x \;=\; b \cdot \tfrac{a}{b} \;=\; a,$$ which is an integer. Hence $b \in A$, so $A$ is nonempty.

Since $A \subseteq \mathbb{N}$ is a nonempty set of positive integers, the well-ordering principle furnishes a smallest element $m \in A$. By construction, $m$ is a positive integer and $mx$ is an integer. Q.E.D.

Step 2

Show that there exists $k \in \mathbb{N}$ with $k < x < k+1$.

Two facts must come together here: $x$ is at least $\sqrt{2}$, and $x$ is not an integer.

Hint

Why is $x > 1$? Why is $x$ not itself an integer? Once you have both, $x$ must fall strictly between two consecutive positive integers — take $k$ to be the larger-or-equal one just below $x$.

Solution

Why $x > 1$. Since $p$ is a positive integer that is not a perfect square and $1 = 1^2$ is a perfect square, we must have $p \geq 2$. Therefore $x = \sqrt{p} \geq \sqrt{2} > 1$.

Why $x$ is not an integer. If $x$ were an integer, then $x^2 = p$ would express $p$ as the square of an integer — directly contradicting our hypothesis. So $x \notin \mathbb{Z}$.

Producing $k$. Let $k = \lfloor x \rfloor$, the greatest integer that does not exceed $x$. Because $x > 1$ we have $k \geq 1$, so $k \in \mathbb{N}$. By the defining property of the floor, $k \leq x < k + 1$. And since $x$ is not an integer, equality $x = k$ is ruled out: we have $$k \;<\; x \;<\; k+1. \qquad \square$$

Step 3

Let $z = mx - mk$. Show that $0 < z < m$.

You have the inequalities from Step 2. Multiply through by the positive integer $m$ and rearrange.

Hint

The inequality $k < x < k+1$ can be multiplied through by $m$ (a positive integer, so the inequalities are preserved). Subtract $mk$ from all three parts of the resulting chain.

Solution

Because $m > 0$, multiplying each part of $k < x < k+1$ by $m$ preserves the inequalities: $$mk \;<\; mx \;<\; mk + m.$$ Subtracting $mk$ throughout gives $$0 \;<\; mx - mk \;<\; m,$$ that is, $0 < z < m$. Q.E.D.

In particular $z \neq 0$, which will matter in Step 4 when we claim $z \in \mathbb{N}$.

Step 4

Show that $z$ and $xz$ are both integers; hence $z \in A$. Derive the contradiction.

This is the payoff. Once $z$ is in $A$, the fact that $z < m$ collides with the fact that $m$ was the smallest element of $A$.

Hint

For $z$: $mx$ is an integer because $m \in A$; $mk$ is an integer because $m, k \in \mathbb{Z}$. Difference of two integers.

For $xz$: multiply out $x(mx - mk)$ and remember that $x^2 = p$. You should find that $xz$ is expressed as $\text{(integer)} - \text{(integer)}$, so it too is an integer.

Then $z \in A$ by definition. What does $z < m$ force?

Solution

$z$ is an integer. Since $m \in A$, the number $mx$ is an integer. Since $m, k \in \mathbb{Z}$, so is $mk$. Therefore $$z \;=\; mx - mk$$ is a difference of integers, hence an integer. Combined with Step 3, $z$ is a positive integer: $z \in \mathbb{N}$.

$xz$ is an integer. Here we use $x^2 = p$. Compute: $$xz \;=\; x(mx - mk) \;=\; m x^2 - k(mx) \;=\; mp \;-\; k(mx).$$ Now $mp$ is an integer ($m, p \in \mathbb{Z}$), and $k(mx)$ is an integer ($k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $mx \in \mathbb{Z}$ since $m \in A$). So $xz$ is a difference of integers, hence an integer.

$z$ belongs to $A$. We have shown $z \in \mathbb{N}$ and $zx = xz \in \mathbb{Z}$. These are exactly the requirements for membership in $A$, so $z \in A$.

The contradiction. By Step 3, $z < m$. But $m$ was chosen as the smallest element of $A$, which would require $m \leq z$. The inequalities $z < m$ and $m \leq z$ cannot both hold, so our original assumption — that $x$ is rational — must be false.

Therefore $x$ is irrational, as was to be shown. Q.E.D.

EpilogueWhy this works

The technique used here is called infinite descent, and it is one of the most elegant tools in elementary number theory. The idea is simple: if a counterexample to a claim existed, you would be able to use it to build a smaller counterexample. Iterating, this would produce an infinite descending chain of positive integers — which the well-ordering principle forbids.

In our proof, the well-ordering principle did its work in advance: we singled out the smallest element $m$ of $A$, and then the "descent step" (the construction of $z \in A$ with $z < m$) only had to be performed once to contradict minimality. The well-ordering principle is thus not merely an accessory to the argument; it is the whole engine.

Finally, note how mild the hypothesis is. We assumed only that $p$ is a non-square positive integer and that $x^2 = p$. We never needed unique factorization or any other arithmetic heavy machinery. The method extends, with care, to rule out rational roots of many polynomial equations — a fact whose grown-up version is the Rational Root Theorem.
