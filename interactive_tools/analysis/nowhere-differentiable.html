§1 — Overview

At first, it seems impossible: a function that is continuous at every point of ℝ yet has no derivative anywhere. Such functions were long believed not to exist — until Weierstrass shocked the mathematical world in 1872. The construction here is Rudin's elegant variant from Principles of Mathematical Analysis, built from a single lacunary series.

§8 — Key Takeaways

Continuity. φ is Lipschitz. The factors (¾)ⁿ give a convergent geometric series bound. Uniform convergence → continuity.
No derivative. For every x and every m, the perturbation δ_m is chosen so |γ_m|=4ᵐ while the tail vanishes. The quotient ≥ (3ᵐ+1)/2 → ∞.
Lacunary series. The gaps 4ⁿ are the key: they map a typical x into a chaotic location far from where it started, frustrating any local linear behavior.
Historical context. Weierstrass (1872) first exhibited such a function, shocking analysts who believed continuity implied "almost everywhere" differentiability.
