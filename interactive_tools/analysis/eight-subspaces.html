The Eight Fundamental Subspaces

For a bounded linear operator $A \in \mathcal{B}(X,Y)$ between Banach spaces

From four subspaces to eight

For a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, Strang's picture lives entirely inside two ambient spaces $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathbb{R}^m$ because the dual $(\mathbb{R}^k)^* = \mathbb{R}^k$ is canonically identified with the space itself. In the Banach setting that identification is gone: $X^*$ and $Y^*$ are genuinely separate Banach spaces.

So we now have four ambient spaces: the domain $X$, its dual $X^*$, the codomain $Y$, and its dual $Y^*$. Each carries a distinguished closed subspace (the kernel or closed range of $A$ or $A^*$) and a complementary quotient. That gives eight objects in total, linked by:

  • the operator $A : X \to Y$ and its adjoint $A^* : Y^* \to X^*$, defined by $\;(A^* y^*)(x) = y^*(Ax)$;
  • the duality pairings $X \leftrightarrow X^*$ and $Y \leftrightarrow Y^*$;
  • the annihilator operations $S \mapsto S^\perp$ (subspace of $X$ to subspace of $X^*$) and $T \mapsto {}^\perp T$ (subspace of $X^*$ to subspace of $X$).

What follows lists the eight objects, draws the picture, and writes out every relationship that links them.

The eight subspaces

The four ambient spaces $X$, $X^*$, $Y$, $Y^*$, each split into a closed subspace and a quotient.

Domain side — $X$ and $X^*$
1 closed subspace of $X$

$N(A) \;\subset\; X$

The kernel of $A$. Always closed because $A$ is bounded. The first identity says $N(A) = {}^\perp R(A^*)$: it is exactly the set of $x$ annihilated by every functional in the range of $A^*$.

2 quotient Banach space

$X / N(A)$

The effective domain. $A$ factors as $X \twoheadrightarrow X/N(A) \xrightarrow{\tilde A} Y$ with $\tilde A$ injective. The quotient norm makes $X/N(A)$ a Banach space.

3 closed subspace of $X^*$

$\overline{R(A^*)} \;\subset\; X^*$

The closed range of the adjoint. Always sits inside $N(A)^\perp$; equals $N(A)^\perp$ if and only if $R(A)$ is closed in $Y$ (the Closed Range Theorem).

4 quotient of $X^*$

$X^* / \overline{R(A^*)}$

When $R(A)$ is closed this becomes $X^* / N(A)^\perp \cong N(A)^*$: the dual of the kernel. The complementary piece in $X^*$.

Codomain side — $Y$ and $Y^*$
5 closed subspace of $Y$

$\overline{R(A)} \;\subset\; Y$

The closed range of $A$. Equals $R(A)$ exactly when the range is closed. By Hahn–Banach, $\overline{R(A)} = {}^\perp N(A^*)$: the set of $y$ killed by every functional that annihilates the range.

6 quotient (the cokernel)

$Y / \overline{R(A)}$

Measures how far $A$ is from surjective — the obstruction to solving $Ax = y$. Its dual is canonically $N(A^*)$.

7 closed subspace of $Y^*$

$N(A^*) \;\subset\; Y^*$

The kernel of the adjoint — equivalently, $R(A)^\perp$. Detects the unsolvable equations: $y \in \overline{R(A)} \iff y^*(y) = 0$ for every $y^* \in N(A^*)$.

8 quotient of $Y^*$

$Y^* / N(A^*)$

Naturally isomorphic to $\overline{R(A)}^*$. $A^*$ factors as $Y^* \twoheadrightarrow Y^*/N(A^*) \xrightarrow{\widetilde{A^*}} X^*$.

The Big Picture

Two "books": each pairs a space with its dual along the shared edge (the duality pairing). The operator $A$ and its adjoint $A^*$ link the books in opposite directions.

kernel-side: N(A), N(A*) range-side: R(A), R(A*) primal space: X, Y dual space: X*, Y* duality pairing (book hinge)

The four annihilator identities (always hold)

For any $A \in \mathcal{B}(X,Y)$ — no hypothesis on the range — the following are immediate from the definition of $A^*$ and Hahn–Banach:

(I) $\;N(A) = {}^\perp R(A^*)$   — $\;x \in N(A) \Longleftrightarrow (A^*y^*)(x) = 0$ for all $y^* \in Y^*$.
(II) $\;N(A^*) = R(A)^\perp$   — $\;y^* \in N(A^*) \Longleftrightarrow y^*(Ax) = 0$ for all $x \in X$.
(III) $\;\overline{R(A)} = {}^\perp N(A^*)$   — Hahn–Banach: a closed subspace equals the pre-annihilator of its annihilator.
(IV) $\;\overline{R(A^*)} \;\subseteq\; N(A)^\perp$   — but equality requires $R(A)$ to be closed. Weak-$*$ closure of $R(A^*)$ does equal $N(A)^\perp$ always.

Canonical duality pairings between the eight subspaces

Each closed subspace pairs with a quotient on the dual side via a natural isometric isomorphism. These are independent of the closed-range hypothesis — they hold for any closed subspace $M$ of any Banach space $Z$ via $(Z/M)^* \cong M^\perp$ and $M^* \cong Z^*/M^\perp$.

(a) $\;(X/N(A))^* \;\cong\; N(A)^\perp \;\subset\; X^*$   — the dual of the effective domain is the annihilator of the kernel.
(b) $\;N(A)^* \;\cong\; X^* / N(A)^\perp$   — the dual of the kernel is the quotient by its annihilator.
(c) $\;\big(Y / \overline{R(A)}\big)^* \;\cong\; \overline{R(A)}^\perp \;=\; N(A^*)$   — the dual of the cokernel is the kernel of the adjoint.
(d) $\;\big(\overline{R(A)}\big)^* \;\cong\; Y^* / N(A^*)$   — the dual of the closed range is the corresponding quotient of $Y^*$.

Schematically, the four annihilator pairings (gold dashes in the diagram) are:

$N(A) \;\subset\; X$ ⇄ $X^* / N(A)^\perp \;\cong\; N(A)^*$
$X/N(A) \;$ ⇄ $\; N(A)^\perp \;\subset\; X^*$  (containing $\overline{R(A^*)}$)
$\overline{R(A)} \;\subset\; Y$ ⇄ $Y^*/N(A^*)$
$Y/\overline{R(A)} \;$ ⇄ $\; N(A^*) \;\subset\; Y^*$

The Closed Range Theorem (Banach)

The single hypothesis that turns the inclusion in (IV) into equality, and that makes $A$ and $A^*$ behave symmetrically. The following four statements are equivalent:

1 $R(A)$ is closed in $Y$
2 $R(A^*)$ is closed in $X^*$
3 $R(A) \;=\; {}^\perp N(A^*)$
4 $R(A^*) \;=\; N(A)^\perp$

Equivalently, $A$ is bounded below on the quotient: there exists $c > 0$ with $\|Ax\|_Y \;\ge\; c\,\|x + N(A)\|_{X/N(A)}$ for all $x$. Closed range is what upgrades the always-true inclusion $\overline{R(A^*)} \subseteq N(A)^\perp$ to an equality — and dually for $R(A) \subseteq {}^\perp N(A^*)$.

Induced bijections (Strang's row-space ↔ column-space map)

Strang's matrix theorem says $A$ restricts to a bijection $C(A^T) \to C(A)$. The Banach analogue runs through quotients on the domain side and through subspaces on the dual side. Both maps are always continuous bijections; they are topological isomorphisms exactly when the Closed Range Theorem applies.

Primal $\;\tilde A : X / N(A) \;\hookrightarrow\; \overline{R(A)} \;\subset\; Y$   — continuous injection with dense image; topological iso onto $R(A)$ iff $R(A)$ is closed.
Dual $\;\widetilde{A^*} : Y^* / N(A^*) \;\hookrightarrow\; \overline{R(A^*)} \;\subset\; X^*$   — same statement on the dual side.

When $R(A)$ is closed, these two bijections are dual to each other: $\widetilde{A^*}$ is the Banach-space adjoint of $\tilde A$ under the identifications $(X/N(A))^* \cong N(A)^\perp$ and $\big(\overline{R(A)}\big)^* \cong Y^*/N(A^*)$. The whole picture — eight subspaces, four annihilator pairings, two induced bijections — commutes.

Hilbert special case — orthogonal direct sums return

When $X$ and $Y$ are Hilbert spaces, the Riesz representation theorem gives canonical isometric anti-isomorphisms $X \cong X^*$ and $Y \cong Y^*$. Under these:

  • Annihilators become orthogonal complements;
  • Quotients have canonical isometric realizations as orthogonal complements inside the space itself;
  • The two "books" collapse onto themselves — $X$ and $X^*$ identify, as do $Y$ and $Y^*$.

The eight subspaces reduce to Strang's four, organized into two orthogonal direct sums:

$X \;=\; N(A) \;\oplus\; \overline{R(A^*)}, \qquad Y \;=\; \overline{R(A)} \;\oplus\; N(A^*).$

$A$ restricts to a bounded bijection $\overline{R(A^*)} \to \overline{R(A)}$ with dense image (an isomorphism iff $R(A)$ is closed). This is Strang's theorem verbatim.

Fredholm operators — rank-nullity survives as the index

$A \in \mathcal{B}(X,Y)$ is Fredholm when $\dim N(A) < \infty$, $R(A)$ is closed, and $\dim Y/R(A) < \infty$. (The closed range is automatic once both defect dimensions are finite.) Strang's identities $\dim N(A) + \mathrm{rank} = n$ and $\dim N(A^T) + \mathrm{rank} = m$ subtract to $n - m$; in infinite dimensions, the analogous invariant is the Fredholm index:

$\mathrm{ind}(A) \;=\; \dim N(A) \;-\; \dim N(A^*) \;=\; \dim N(A) \;-\; \dim \big(Y/R(A)\big)$

The second equality uses the duality $(Y/R(A))^* \cong N(A^*)$ from pairing (c), which makes their dimensions equal in the finite case. The index is invariant under:

  • norm-continuous deformation of $A$ through Fredholm operators;
  • compact perturbations: $\mathrm{ind}(A + K) = \mathrm{ind}(A)$ for $K$ compact (Atkinson);
  • composition: $\mathrm{ind}(BA) = \mathrm{ind}(A) + \mathrm{ind}(B)$.

For a matrix this recovers $\mathrm{ind}(A) = n - m$, independent of the entries.
