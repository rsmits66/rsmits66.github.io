A Note on Linear Algebra Vol. I

Fourier Series
an orthogonal basis

An infinite orthogonal basis, a projection formula, and a small mountain of integration by parts — all on the interval $[0, 2\pi]$.

§ 01The Inner Product

Linear algebra, all the way down. Treat continuous functions on $[0,2\pi]$ as vectors in the space $C[0,2\pi]$, and equip them with the inner product

$$\langle f, g \rangle \;=\; \int_0^{2\pi} f(x)\, g(x)\, dx.$$

This is symmetric, bilinear, and positive definite, so it behaves like the dot product. Two functions are orthogonal when their integrated product vanishes; the norm of $f$ is $\|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f, f\rangle}.$

The whole claim — the entire pitch — is that the trigonometric family

$$\{\, 1,\; \cos x,\; \sin x,\; \cos 2x,\; \sin 2x,\; \cos 3x,\; \sin 3x,\; \ldots \,\}$$

is an orthogonal set in this inner product space. Normalize the vectors. You get an orthonormal basis. Project any reasonable function onto it. You get its Fourier series. That's it. That's the trick.

§ 02Orthogonality, via the Angle Formulas

Three identities do all the work. Each is just a rearrangement of the sum-and-difference formulas you already know:

$$\cos A \cos B = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\bigl[\cos(A-B) + \cos(A+B)\bigr]$$ $$\sin A \sin B = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\bigl[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)\bigr]$$ $$\sin A \cos B = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\bigl[\sin(A+B) + \sin(A-B)\bigr]$$

Each product becomes a sum of two single trig functions, and over a full period $[0, 2\pi]$, every $\sin(kx)$ and every nonzero $\cos(kx)$ integrates to zero. That's the whole argument; the lemmas below are bookkeeping.

Lemma · cos ⊥ cos

For nonnegative integers $m \neq n$,

$$\int_0^{2\pi} \!\cos(mx)\cos(nx)\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\int_0^{2\pi}\!\bigl[\cos((m-n)x) + \cos((m+n)x)\bigr]\,dx \;=\; 0.$$

When $m = n \geq 1$, the integrand is $\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[1 + \cos(2nx)\bigr]$, integrating to $\pi$.

Lemma · sin ⊥ sin

Identically, for $m \neq n$ both $\geq 1$,

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\!\sin(mx)\sin(nx)\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\int_0^{2\pi}\!\bigl[\cos((m-n)x) - \cos((m+n)x)\bigr]\,dx \;=\; 0,$$

and $\int_0^{2\pi}\!\sin^2(nx)\,dx = \pi$.

Lemma · sin ⊥ cos (always)

For every $m, n$,

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\!\sin(mx)\cos(nx)\,dx \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\int_0^{2\pi}\!\bigl[\sin((m+n)x) + \sin((m-n)x)\bigr]\,dx \;=\; 0,$$

since each $\sin(kx)$ has integral zero over a full period (and is literally zero when $k = 0$).

The constant function $1$ is also orthogonal to every $\sin(nx)$ and $\cos(nx)$ for $n \geq 1$, since each integrates to zero on its own. So the entire family is orthogonal.

§ 03Normalize the Basis

Reading the diagonals from the lemmas above:

$$\|1\|^2 = 2\pi, \qquad \|\cos(nx)\|^2 = \pi, \qquad \|\sin(nx)\|^2 = \pi \quad (n \geq 1).$$

So the orthonormal basis is

$$\mathcal{B} \;=\; \left\{\, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}},\;\; \frac{\cos(nx)}{\sqrt{\pi}},\;\; \frac{\sin(nx)}{\sqrt{\pi}}\,\right\}_{n \,\geq\, 1}.$$

For any $f \in C[0,2\pi]$, orthogonal projection — the same formula you used to decompose a vector along orthogonal axes in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — gives

$$f \;\sim\; \sum_{u \in \mathcal{B}} \langle f, u\rangle\, u.$$

Collecting the $\pi$ factors and using the convenient $a_0/2$ convention, this is exactly the classical form

$$\boxed{\;f(x) \;\sim\; \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigl[\, a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\,\bigr]\;}$$

with

$$a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\!\int_0^{2\pi}\! f(x)\cos(nx)\,dx, \qquad b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\!\int_0^{2\pi}\! f(x)\sin(nx)\,dx.$$
The constant term $a_0/2$ is just $\langle f, 1\rangle / \|1\|^2$ — the projection onto the constant function. The factor of two there is a notational habit, not a deep truth.

Computing the coefficients is now an integration problem. For polynomial $f$, that means integration by parts. Repeatedly.

§ 04Tabular Integration by Parts

To compute $\int x^3 \cos(nx)\,dx$ honestly requires integrating by parts four times. The standard $u\,dv$ notation is a mess after the second pass. The DI table (also called "tic-tac-toe IBP") cleans it up: differentiate the polynomial column down to zero, integrate the trig column an equal number of times, and read the answer off the diagonals with alternating signs.

The recipe

Build two columns side by side. In the D column, write your polynomial and keep differentiating until you hit zero. In the I column, write the other factor and keep integrating it the same number of times. Then multiply each D entry by the entry one row down in the I column, attaching alternating signs $+,\,-,\,+,\,-,\ldots$ starting with $+$.

Sign D — differentiate I — integrate
+$x^3$$\cos(nx)$
−$3x^2$$\dfrac{\sin(nx)}{n}$
+$6x$$-\dfrac{\cos(nx)}{n^2}$
−$6$$-\dfrac{\sin(nx)}{n^3}$
+$0$$\dfrac{\cos(nx)}{n^4}$

Pairing each $D$ entry with the next row's $I$ entry, attaching the sign on the $D$ row, and stopping when $D$ hits zero:

$$\int x^3 \cos(nx)\,dx \;=\; \frac{x^3 \sin(nx)}{n} \;+\; \frac{3x^2 \cos(nx)}{n^2} \;-\; \frac{6x \sin(nx)}{n^3} \;-\; \frac{6 \cos(nx)}{n^4} \;+\; C.$$

Differentiate that mess to check it really does give $x^3 \cos(nx)$ — the cross terms collapse exactly the way they should.

Evaluating at the endpoints

Now evaluate from $0$ to $2\pi$. Because $\sin(2\pi n) = 0$ and $\cos(2\pi n) = 1$ for every integer $n$, every sine term vanishes at both endpoints and only the cosine terms contribute. The $x^2\cos(nx)/n^2$ term gives $(2\pi)^2 \cdot 3 / n^2 - 0$, and the constant cosine terms cancel beautifully:

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\! x^3 \cos(nx)\,dx \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{3 (2\pi)^2}{n^2}}_{\text{at } 2\pi} \;-\; \underbrace{\frac{6}{n^4}}_{\text{at } 2\pi} \;-\; \underbrace{\left(-\frac{6}{n^4}\right)}_{\text{at } 0} \;=\; \frac{12\pi^2}{n^2}.$$

So $a_n = (1/\pi)(12\pi^2/n^2) = 12\pi / n^2$ for $f(x) = x^3$. A parallel DI table starting with $\sin(nx)$ in the integrate column delivers $b_n = 12/n^3 - 8\pi^2/n$. Same machinery, every time.

§ 05A Small Catalog of Coefficients

Running the same procedure on a handful of presets, here are the Fourier series on $[0, 2\pi]$. Each converges to the function on the open interval $(0, 2\pi)$ and to the average $\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[f(0) + f(2\pi)\bigr]$ at the endpoints — the periodic extension introduces a jump there, and the series can only see the average.

Written out

Plugging in $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ gives the first handful of coefficients explicitly:

$$1 \;\sim\; 1$$ $$x \;\sim\; \pi \;-\; 2\sin x \;-\; \sin 2x \;-\; \tfrac{2}{3}\sin 3x \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 4x \;-\; \cdots$$ $$\begin{aligned} x^2 \;\sim\; \tfrac{4\pi^2}{3} \;&+\; 4\cos x \;+\; \cos 2x \;+\; \tfrac{4}{9}\cos 3x \;+\; \tfrac{1}{4}\cos 4x \;+\; \cdots \\ \;&-\; 4\pi\sin x \;-\; 2\pi\sin 2x \;-\; \tfrac{4\pi}{3}\sin 3x \;-\; \pi\sin 4x \;-\; \cdots \end{aligned}$$ $$\begin{aligned} x^3 \;\sim\; 2\pi^3 \;&+\; 12\pi\cos x \;+\; 3\pi\cos 2x \;+\; \tfrac{4\pi}{3}\cos 3x \;+\; \tfrac{3\pi}{4}\cos 4x \;+\; \cdots \\ \;&+\; (12 - 8\pi^2)\sin x \;+\; \bigl(\tfrac{3}{2} - 4\pi^2\bigr)\sin 2x \;+\; \bigl(\tfrac{4}{9} - \tfrac{8\pi^2}{3}\bigr)\sin 3x \;+\; \cdots \end{aligned}$$ $$e^x \;\sim\; \frac{e^{2\pi}-1}{2\pi} \;+\; \frac{e^{2\pi}-1}{\pi}\!\left[\,\frac{\cos x - \sin x}{2} \;+\; \frac{\cos 2x - 2\sin 2x}{5} \;+\; \frac{\cos 3x - 3\sin 3x}{10} \;+\; \cdots\,\right]$$

A few sanity checks worth pausing on. The constant $1$ is its own Fourier series — it lives along a single basis vector, with no component anywhere else. The function $f(x) = x$ has zero cosine content because $x - \pi$ is odd about the midpoint $x = \pi$, and the sines $\sin(nx)$ are exactly the basis of functions odd about $\pi$ on this interval. For $e^x$, the ratio $b_n / a_n = -n$ is the fingerprint of differentiating a function that equals its own derivative.

And $x^3$ at the midpoint $x = \pi$? The series gives $2\pi^3 + \sum 12\pi (-1)^n / n^2 = 2\pi^3 + 12\pi \cdot (-\pi^2/12) = \pi^3$. The function value, exactly.

§ 06Reconstruction

Pick a function. Move the slider. Watch finite sums of trig polynomials chase the target. Near $x = 0$ and $x = 2\pi$, where the periodic extension is discontinuous, the partial sums overshoot the jump by a fixed percentage no matter how large $N$ grows — that's Gibbs' phenomenon, a permanent artifact of approximating a discontinuity with smooth basis functions.

What you are looking at is orthogonal projection in real time. Every new term is one more axis in an infinite-dimensional vector space, picked off by an inner product. The function isn't being "approximated" in any vague sense — it's being decomposed into its components along an orthonormal frame, exactly the way a vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is decomposed along $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}$. The space is just bigger, and the basis is trigonometric.
