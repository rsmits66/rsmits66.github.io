Boundary maps on a box torus

A polytope of genus one, triangulated; the chain complex; and the homology that finally has somewhere to go.

Take a flat box and bore a square hole through it. The boundary surface is topologically a torus — genus 1, Euler characteristic $0$. Below we triangulate it explicitly (16 vertices, 48 edges, 32 triangles), write down both boundary maps, and watch $H_1$ stop being zero. The two surviving 1-classes are the cycles every torus has: a meridian around the tube, a longitude around the hole.

IThe polytope, triangulated

The polytope is the surface of $\,[-X,X]\times[-Y,Y]\times[-H,H]\,$ with the rectangular tunnel $\,[-x,x]\times[-y,y]\times[-H,H]\,$ removed. Eight outer corners and eight inner corners give $V = 16$. The faces, before triangulation, are: four outer side rectangles (E, N, W, S), four inner side rectangles facing into the tunnel, and the top & bottom annuli (each a square with a square hole). We triangulate every rectangle by one diagonal, and each annulus by splitting it into 4 trapezoids and giving each one a diagonal — that gives $E = 48$ and $F = 32$.

$\chi \;=\; V - E + F \;=\; 16 - 48 + 32 \;=\; 0 \;=\; \chi(T^2)\,. \quad \checkmark$

IIOrderings & orientations

Three ordered lists, fixed once and for all. Edges run lower-index → higher-index. Triangles are oriented so their normals point outward from the solid material — outward from the bulk for outer faces, into the tunnel for inner faces, and $\pm z$ for the top/bottom annuli. With this convention, the sum of all 32 triangles is a 2-cycle.

IIIThe boundary $\partial_1\colon C_1 \to C_0$

For each oriented edge $e_k = [v_i, v_j]$ with $i < j$, the boundary is

$\partial_1\, e_k \;=\; v_j - v_i\,.$

The corresponding column of $\partial_1$ has $-1$ in row $i$, $+1$ in row $j$, and zeros elsewhere — the signed incidence matrix of the 1-skeleton.

∂₁   (16 × 48)
+1 −1 0

Two checks visible from the picture: every column has exactly one $+1$ (green) and one $-1$ (crimson) — every edge has two endpoints. Every row sum is $0$ — at each vertex, the number of edges arriving equals the number leaving. The 12 columns from $e_{37}$ to $e_{48}$ (face and annulus diagonals) are concentrated in the upper or lower halves of the matrix according to whether the diagonal is on the bottom-half ($v_1\!-\!v_{12}$) or crosses to the top half.

    Each column has exactly three nonzero entries — one triangle's three signed boundary edges. Each row has exactly two nonzero entries with opposite signs: every edge sits in exactly two triangles, and because all 32 are oriented outward, those appearances cancel. The "∂² = 0" geometry sits in those row patterns.

    VVerifying $\partial_1 \circ \partial_2 = 0$

    Take triangle $t_{17} = [v_1, v_9, v_{10}]$, one of the bottom-annulus triangles. From the list,

    $\partial_2 t_{17} \;=\; e_{13} + e_{25} - e_{41}\,,$

    VIHomology

    The dimensions follow from two ranks, computed at page load by row-reducing the matrices above:

    $\mathrm{rank}(\partial_1) \;=\;$ …$,\qquad \mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) \;=\;$ …$.$

    $H_0$ — connectedness

    The 1-skeleton is connected (every vertex is reachable from every other through the edges), so $\mathrm{im}(\partial_1)$ is the 15-dimensional subspace $\{\sum a_i v_i : \sum a_i = 0\} \subset \mathbb{R}^{16}$, and

    $\dim H_0 \;=\; 16 - \mathrm{rank}(\partial_1) \;=\; 16 - 15 \;=\; 1\,.$

    $H_2$ — top dimension

    $H_2 = \ker(\partial_2)$. The fundamental class $[\Sigma] = t_1 + t_2 + \cdots + t_{32}$ lies in this kernel: every edge appears in exactly two triangle boundaries with opposite signs, so $\partial_2 [\Sigma] = 0$. The connectedness argument from before shows the kernel can't be larger than this one-dimensional span, so

    $\dim H_2 \;=\; 32 - \mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) \;=\; 32 - 31 \;=\; 1\,.$

    $H_1$ — the holes

    By rank–nullity for $\partial_1$,

    $\dim \ker(\partial_1) \;=\; 48 - 15 \;=\; 33\,.$

    The image of $\partial_2$ has rank $31$, and is contained in $\ker(\partial_1)$. So

    $\dim H_1 \;=\; 33 - 31 \;=\; 2\,.$

    The two-dimensional homology has explicit generators visible on the polytope:

    L · longitude, the bottom outer square $\;v_1 \to v_2 \to v_3 \to v_4 \to v_1\;$ written as $\;e_1 + e_2 + e_3 - e_4\,.\;$ It loops once around the donut hole. It is a 1-cycle ($\partial_1$ of it $= 0$, immediate check), and it is not a boundary — it would have to bound a disk on the surface, but every disk it could bound has been removed.

    M · meridian, the cross-section $\;v_1 \to v_9 \to v_{13} \to v_5 \to v_1\;$ written as $\;-e_9 + e_{21} - e_{29} + e_{25}\,.\;$ It loops once around the tube of the donut, threading the hole. It is also a non-bounding 1-cycle.

    Click the L and M buttons on the widget above and you can see them traced out. You can verify (by augmenting the matrix $\partial_2$ with these two columns and recomputing the rank) that they are linearly independent in $H_1$: the page does this on load — augmented rank = …, which is $\mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) + 2$, confirming both L and M are nonzero in $H_1$ and not multiples of each other modulo boundaries.

    The result

    $H_0 \,\cong\, \mathbb{R}\,, \qquad H_1 \,\cong\, \mathbb{R}^2\,, \qquad H_2 \,\cong\, \mathbb{R}\,.$

    This is the homology of $T^2$. The two-dimensional $H_1$ is the entire reason the torus is interesting in algebraic topology — it's where holes show up and refuse to be filled. Euler check:

    $\chi \;=\; \dim H_0 - \dim H_1 + \dim H_2 \;=\; 1 - 2 + 1 \;=\; 0 \;=\; V - E + F\,. \quad \checkmark$

    Coefficients in $\mathbb{R}$. Over $\mathbb{Z}$ the same calculation gives $H_0 \cong H_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}$ and $H_1 \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$, since the box torus surface is orientable and torsion-free. — Roll Tide —
