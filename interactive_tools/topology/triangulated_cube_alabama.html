Boundary maps on a triangulated cube

Orderings, orientations, the matrices in full, and the homology that follows.

The surface of a cube — split each square face along one diagonal — is one of the smallest closed triangulated 2-manifolds you can write down: 8 vertices, 18 edges, 12 triangles. Below we fix orderings once, write the boundary maps as explicit integer matrices, verify $\partial_1 \circ \partial_2 = 0$, and read off the homology. The verdict, which topology demands, is the homology of $S^2$.

IThe triangulation

Take the unit cube $[0,1]^3$, its 8 corner vertices, and its 12 cube-edges; on each of the 6 square faces, add one diagonal. The result is a simplicial 2-complex with 8 vertices, $12 + 6 = 18$ edges, and 12 triangles. The Euler check is immediate:

$V - E + F \;=\; 8 - 18 + 12 \;=\; 2 \;=\; \chi(S^2)\,. \quad \checkmark$

IIOrderings & orientations

Three ordered lists, fixed once and for all. Edges run from the lower-indexed vertex to the higher-indexed one. Triangles are oriented so that, by the right-hand rule, the normal points outward from the cube — this consistency is what allows the sum of all twelve triangles to be a 2-cycle.

The chain complex is

$0 \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbb{R}^{12} \;\xrightarrow{\;\partial_2\;}\; \mathbb{R}^{18} \;\xrightarrow{\;\partial_1\;}\; \mathbb{R}^{8} \;\longrightarrow\; 0\,.$

So $\partial_1$ is an $8 \times 18$ matrix and $\partial_2$ an $18 \times 12$ matrix. We write both, in full.

IIIThe boundary $\partial_1\colon C_1 \to C_0$

For each edge $e_k = [v_i, v_j]$ with $i < j$, the boundary is

$\partial_1 \, e_k \;=\; v_j - v_i\,.$

The corresponding column of $\partial_1$ has $-1$ in row $i$, $+1$ in row $j$, zeros elsewhere — the signed incidence matrix of the 1-skeleton. (It's the same matrix Kirchhoff would write down for circuits on this graph.)

Two checks the matrix wears on its sleeve: every column has one $+1$ and one $-1$ (each edge has two endpoints), and every row sum is $0$ (each vertex appears as head of as many edges as it appears as tail).

IVThe boundary $\partial_2\colon C_2 \to C_1$

For an oriented triangle $t = [v_a, v_b, v_c]$ the boundary is

$\partial_2 [v_a, v_b, v_c] \;=\; [v_b, v_c] \;-\; [v_a, v_c] \;+\; [v_a, v_b]\,.$

Each $[v_i, v_j]$ then matches one of our oriented edges $e_k$ (lower index → higher index) — possibly with a minus sign if the orientation is reversed. Computing for all twelve triangles:

  • ∂t₁ = −e₁ − e₂ + e₁₃
  • ∂t₂ = +e₄ − e₃ − e₁₃
  • ∂t₃ = +e₅ + e₆ − e₁₄
  • ∂t₄ = +e₇ − e₈ + e₁₄
  • ∂t₅ = +e₁ + e₁₀ − e₁₅
  • ∂t₆ = −e₅ − e₉ + e₁₅
  • ∂t₇ = +e₃ + e₁₂ − e₁₆

Each column has exactly three nonzero entries — a triangle's three signed boundary edges. Each row has exactly two nonzero entries with opposite signs: every edge sits in exactly two triangles, and because all twelve are oriented outward, those two appearances cancel. This row pattern is the geometric face of $\partial^2 = 0$.

VVerifying $\partial_1 \circ \partial_2 = 0$

Take triangle $t_5 = [v_1, v_2, v_6]$. From the table,

$\partial_2 t_5 \;=\; e_1 + e_{10} - e_{15}\,,$

and applying $\partial_1$,

$\partial_1\bigl(e_1 + e_{10} - e_{15}\bigr) \;=\; (v_2 - v_1) + (v_6 - v_2) - (v_6 - v_1) \;=\; 0\,. \quad \checkmark$

More structurally, the $(i,j)$-entry of $\partial_1 \partial_2$ counts, with sign, how many times vertex $v_i$ appears at the boundary of triangle $t_j$. For any triangle $[v_a, v_b, v_c]$, each of $v_a, v_b, v_c$ appears once with $+$ and once with $-$ in the boundary expansion, so every column of the product is zero. Multiplying the explicit $8 \times 18$ and $18 \times 12$ matrices above gives the zero matrix exactly.

VIHomology, computed

$H_0$ — connected components

$H_0 = \ker(\partial_0)/\mathrm{im}(\partial_1) = \mathbb{R}^8/\mathrm{im}(\partial_1)$. The image of $\partial_1$ is spanned by the 18 vectors $\{v_j - v_i\}$. Because the 1-skeleton is connected (any vertex reaches any other through edges), this image is exactly the 7-dimensional subspace

$\Bigl\{ \textstyle\sum_i a_i v_i \;:\; \sum_i a_i = 0 \Bigr\} \subset \mathbb{R}^8\,.$

Hence $\mathrm{rank}(\partial_1) = 7$ and

$\dim H_0 \;=\; 8 - 7 \;=\; 1\,.$

$H_2$ — top-dimensional cycles

$H_2 = \ker(\partial_2)$, since there is no $\partial_3$. We claim the kernel is $1$-dimensional, spanned by the fundamental class

$[\Sigma] \;:=\; t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + t_4 + t_5 + t_6 + t_7 + t_8 + t_9 + t_{10} + t_{11} + t_{12}\,.$

Why is $\partial_2 [\Sigma] = 0$? Sum the 12 boundary equations from §IV. Every edge $e_k$ appears in exactly two triangle boundaries (the two triangles incident to it), with opposite signs because the triangles are consistently outward-oriented. The signs cancel, leaving zero.

For example, $e_5 = [v_5, v_6]$ appears in $\partial t_3$ with coefficient $+1$ and in $\partial t_6$ with coefficient $-1$, so its total contribution to $\partial[\Sigma]$ is zero. The same pattern holds for all 18 edges (the rows of $\partial_2$ all sum to zero, encoding this).

To see the kernel is no bigger: any 2-cycle is determined up to scalar by a single triangle's coefficient. The cancellation condition on shared edges forces neighbouring triangle coefficients to be equal, and the surface is connected, so all twelve coefficients must agree. Therefore

$\dim H_2 \;=\; \dim \ker(\partial_2) \;=\; 1\,, \qquad \mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) \;=\; 12 - 1 \;=\; 11\,.$

$H_1$ — loops modulo boundaries

By rank–nullity for $\partial_1$,

$\dim \ker(\partial_1) \;=\; 18 - \mathrm{rank}(\partial_1) \;=\; 18 - 7 \;=\; 11\,.$

And we just computed $\mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) = 11$. Since $\mathrm{im}(\partial_2) \subseteq \ker(\partial_1)$ and they share the same dimension, they coincide:

$\dim H_1 \;=\; \dim \ker(\partial_1) - \mathrm{rank}(\partial_2) \;=\; 11 - 11 \;=\; 0\,.$

$H_0 \,\cong\, \mathbb{R}\,, \qquad H_1 \,=\, 0\,, \qquad H_2 \,\cong\, \mathbb{R}\,.$

This is the homology of the 2-sphere: one connected component, no nontrivial loops, one enclosed void. The answer is independent of the triangulation, of orientations, of orderings — those are scaffolding. The output is a topological invariant, and it correctly sees that this collection of 38 simplices is a sphere.

An Euler-characteristic accounting confirms the count from another direction:

$\chi \;=\; \dim H_0 - \dim H_1 + \dim H_2 \;=\; 1 - 0 + 1 \;=\; 2 \;=\; V - E + F\,. \quad \checkmark$

Coefficients in $\mathbb{R}$. Over $\mathbb{Z}$ the same calculation gives $H_0 \cong H_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}$, since the cube surface is orientable and torsion-free. — Roll Tide —
