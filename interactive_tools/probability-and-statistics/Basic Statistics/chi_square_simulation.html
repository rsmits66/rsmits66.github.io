What to notice

The crimson curve is the theoretical χ²k−1 density from the formula f(x) = x(k−1)/2 − 1 · e−x/2 / (2(k−1)/2 · Γ((k−1)/2)). The gray histogram is the empirical null distribution built by running 100,000 random multinomial experiments and computing X² each time. When all expected counts are comfortably above 5, the two match closely and the simulated p-value agrees with the theoretical one within Monte Carlo error.

Try this

Switch k to 4 and enter 9, 3, 3, 1 for the null probabilities with observed counts 315, 108, 101, 32 (Mendel's original pea data) — the simulated and theoretical p-values should both land near 0.92, showing the famous near-perfect agreement with Mendelian genetics. Try k = 6 with equal probabilities and counts 10, 9, 11, 10, 8, 12 to simulate rolling a fair die 60 times. Use unequal null probabilities like 0.5, 0.3, 0.2 with counts 48, 32, 20 to test a specific composition hypothesis. Notice how the crimson curve's shape changes with df — it peaks at the origin for df ≤ 2, but pulls away from zero and becomes roughly bell-shaped for df ≥ 3.
