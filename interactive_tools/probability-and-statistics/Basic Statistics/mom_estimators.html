An Essay on Point Estimation

The Method
of Moments

a guided treatment, with seven worked examples

I. Preface

The method of moments is among the oldest and most intuitive techniques for constructing point estimators. Conceived by Karl Pearson in 1894, the procedure rests on a single, almost philosophical principle: the sample, in expectation, ought to look like the population. If one equates as many sample moments as there are unknown parameters, the resulting system, when invertible, yields estimators in closed form.

For a random variable \(Y\) with distribution depending on parameters \(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_t\), the \(k\)th population moment about the origin is

\[ \mu_k' \;=\; E(Y^k), \]

and its sample counterpart, computed from \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\), is

\[ m_k' \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i^{\,k}. \]
Note.   The Weak Law of Large Numbers ensures \(m_k' \xrightarrow{\,P\,} \mu_k'\) whenever the moment exists; method-of-moments estimators are therefore consistent under mild regularity conditions.

II. The Procedure

  1. For a population indexed by \(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_t\), choose the first \(t\) population moments \(\mu_1',\ldots,\mu_t'\).
  2. Equate each population moment to the corresponding sample moment: \(\mu_k' = m_k'\), \(k=1,\ldots,t\).
  3. Solve the resulting system for \(\hat\theta_1,\ldots,\hat\theta_t\). These are the method-of-moments estimators.

One could, in principle, use any \(t\) moments — but custom and convenience favour the lowest \(t\) available. Higher moments are noisier, and exotic moments are seldom interpretable.

III. Examples

Ex. 1
Example 1

The Uniform Distribution on \((0,\theta)\)

Suppose \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\) is a random sample from \(\text{Uniform}(0,\theta)\), with density \(f(y;\theta) = 1/\theta\) for \(0 \le y \le \theta\).

The first population moment is

\[ \mu_1' \;=\; E(Y) \;=\; \frac{\theta}{2}, \]

and equating with the sample mean \(m_1' = \bar Y\) gives

Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat\theta \;=\; 2\bar Y.\)

One readily verifies \(E(\hat\theta) = \theta\) and \(V(\hat\theta) = \theta^2 / (3n)\), so the variance vanishes as \(n\to\infty\). By Chebyshev's inequality, \(\hat\theta\) is a consistent estimator of \(\theta\).

A curiosity.   The maximum likelihood estimator here is \(\hat\theta_{\text{MLE}} = Y_{(n)}\), the sample maximum — a quite different creature, biased downward but with smaller variance for large \(n\). MoM and MLE need not agree.

Ex. 2
Example 2

The Gamma Distribution

Let \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Gamma}(\alpha,\beta)\), with density \(f(y;\alpha,\beta) = y^{\alpha-1}e^{-y/\beta} / [\beta^{\alpha}\Gamma(\alpha)]\), \(y\ge 0\).

Two parameters demand two moments. We have

\[ \mu_1' = \alpha\beta, \qquad \mu_2' = \alpha\beta^2 + (\alpha\beta)^2. \]

Equating to \(\bar Y\) and \(m_2' = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum Y_i^2\):

\[ \alpha\beta = \bar Y, \qquad \alpha\beta^2 + \bar Y^{\,2} = m_2'. \]

From the first, \(\beta = \bar Y/\alpha\). Substituting into the second,

\[ \frac{\bar Y^{\,2}}{\alpha} + \bar Y^{\,2} \;=\; m_2' \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \hat\alpha \;=\; \frac{\bar Y^{\,2}}{m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2}}, \]

and consequently

Joint MoM Estimators \(\displaystyle \hat\alpha \;=\; \frac{\bar Y^{\,2}}{m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2}}, \qquad \hat\beta \;=\; \frac{m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2}}{\bar Y}.\)

The denominator \(m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2}\) is, of course, the (uncorrected) sample variance — a quantity to which we shall return repeatedly.

Ex. 3
Example 3

The Normal Distribution

Let \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\), with \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(\sigma^2 > 0\) both unknown.

The first two moments are well known:

\[ \mu_1' = \mu, \qquad \mu_2' = \sigma^2 + \mu^2. \]

Equating with the sample moments \(\bar Y\) and \(m_2'\):

\[ \hat\mu = \bar Y, \qquad \hat\sigma^2 + \bar Y^{\,2} = m_2'. \]
Method-of-Moments Estimators \(\displaystyle \hat\mu \;=\; \bar Y, \qquad \hat\sigma^2 \;=\; m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2} \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \bar Y)^2.\)

Notice that \(\hat\sigma^2\) is the uncorrected sample variance — divided by \(n\), not \(n-1\). It is therefore biased: \(E(\hat\sigma^2) = \tfrac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2\). The bias vanishes as \(n\to\infty\), and consistency is preserved.

Aside.   In this case the MoM and MLE estimators coincide exactly. This is a happy accident, not a general rule.

Example 4

The Exponential Distribution

Suppose \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\) are iid \(\text{Exp}(\beta)\), with density \(f(y;\beta) = (1/\beta)\,e^{-y/\beta}\) for \(y \ge 0\).

A single parameter requires only the first moment:

\[ \mu_1' \;=\; E(Y) \;=\; \beta. \]

Equating to \(\bar Y\) gives the trivially simple estimator

Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat\beta \;=\; \bar Y.\)

Since \(E(\bar Y) = \beta\) and \(V(\bar Y) = \beta^2/n\), the estimator is unbiased and consistent. If one prefers the rate parameter \(\lambda = 1/\beta\), then \(\hat\lambda = 1/\bar Y\); this estimator is no longer unbiased (Jensen's inequality), but remains consistent.

Example 5

The Bernoulli & Binomial Distributions

Let \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)\), so that each \(Y_i \in \{0,1\}\) with \(P(Y_i = 1) = p\).

Here \(\mu_1' = E(Y) = p\), and equating to \(\bar Y\) gives

Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat p \;=\; \bar Y \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i.\)

That is, the proportion of successes. Unbiased, consistent, with \(V(\hat p) = p(1-p)/n\).

Binomial extension.

If instead \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Binomial}(m, p)\) with \(m\) trials per observation known and \(p\) unknown, then \(\mu_1' = mp\) and

Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat p \;=\; \frac{\bar Y}{m}.\)

A more interesting case arises when both \(m\) and \(p\) are unknown — a problem famously studied by Olkin, Petkau, and Zidek (1981). One then uses the first two moments and obtains a coupled system whose solution is unstable for moderate \(n\); the method of moments illustrates here both its reach and its limits.

Example 6

The Poisson Distribution

Let \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Poisson}(\lambda)\), with \(P(Y = y) = \lambda^{y} e^{-\lambda}/y!\) for \(y = 0,1,2,\ldots\).

One parameter, one moment:

\[ \mu_1' \;=\; E(Y) \;=\; \lambda. \]
Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat\lambda \;=\; \bar Y.\)

Note an arresting curiosity of the Poisson: \(E(Y) = V(Y) = \lambda\). One could equally well estimate \(\lambda\) by the sample variance \(S^2\); the result would be a different — and generally inferior — method-of-moments estimator built from the second central moment. The choice to use \(\mu_1'\) is a choice, and a wise one.

Diagnostic.   A large discrepancy between \(\bar Y\) and \(S^2\) is itself evidence against the Poisson model — a phenomenon called overdispersion, and a frequent gateway to negative-binomial alternatives.

Ex. 7
Example 7

The Geometric Distribution

Let \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\) be iid \(\text{Geometric}(p)\), counting trials until the first success: \(P(Y = y) = (1-p)^{y-1} p\) for \(y = 1, 2, \ldots\).

The first moment is

\[ \mu_1' \;=\; E(Y) \;=\; \frac{1}{p}. \]

Setting \(1/p = \bar Y\) and solving:

Method-of-Moments Estimator \(\displaystyle \hat p \;=\; \frac{1}{\bar Y}.\)

The estimator is not unbiased — \(E(1/\bar Y) \ne 1/E(\bar Y)\) by Jensen's inequality — but it is consistent, since \(\bar Y \xrightarrow{P} 1/p\) and the function \(g(x) = 1/x\) is continuous on \((0,\infty)\). A clean illustration of continuous-mapping consistency.

Ex. 8
Example 8

The Beta Distribution

Suppose \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)\) on \((0,1)\) — a setting in which MLE has no closed form, so the method of moments is genuinely useful.

Recall

\[ E(Y) = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}, \qquad V(Y) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}. \]

Let \(\bar Y\) and \(S_n^2 = m_2' - \bar Y^{\,2}\) denote the first sample moment and the (uncorrected) sample variance. Setting \(E(Y) = \bar Y\) and \(V(Y) = S_n^2\), some patient algebra yields

\[ \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} = \bar Y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \beta = \alpha\left(\frac{1 - \bar Y}{\bar Y}\right). \]

Substituting into the variance equation and simplifying:

\[ \alpha + \beta + 1 \;=\; \frac{\bar Y(1 - \bar Y)}{S_n^2}. \]

The right-hand side is a single number computable from the data. Combining,

Method-of-Moments Estimators \(\displaystyle \hat\alpha \;=\; \bar Y\!\left[\frac{\bar Y(1-\bar Y)}{S_n^2} - 1\right], \quad \hat\beta \;=\; (1-\bar Y)\!\left[\frac{\bar Y(1-\bar Y)}{S_n^2} - 1\right].\)

Both estimators are positive precisely when \(S_n^2 < \bar Y(1-\bar Y)\) — a condition that, agreeably, holds with probability one for any genuine Beta sample of size \(n\ge 2\).

V. Closing Remarks

The method of moments is, in modern statistical parlance, a workhorse of first resort. It gives closed-form estimators that are typically consistent and asymptotically normal; it requires only that the relevant moments exist; and it serves admirably as a starting value for iterative procedures such as Newton-Raphson when MLE has no analytical solution — the Beta distribution being the canonical example.

Yet the method has limitations. Its estimators may be biased, occasionally inadmissible, and rarely efficient. They depend on which moments one chooses; a pathological example is the Cauchy distribution, whose mean fails to exist and for which the method of moments is silent altogether.

These caveats are the very reason we shall, in the next chapter, turn to maximum likelihood — a method born of a different intuition, but sharing with the method of moments a common destination: estimators that let the data speak in the parameter's stead.
