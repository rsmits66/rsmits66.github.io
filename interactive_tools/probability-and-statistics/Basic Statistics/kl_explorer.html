I
First Principles

The Definition.

Given two densities in the same family — say $f(y;\theta_1)$ and $f(y;\theta_2)$ — the Kullback–Leibler information for discriminating between them is the per-observation expected log-likelihood ratio, taken under the truth $\theta_1$:

Definition
$$ I(\theta_1\,;\,\theta_2) \;=\; n^{-1}\,\mathrm{E}_{1}\!\left[\,\log \frac{f(y;\theta_1)}{f(y;\theta_2)}\,\right]. $$

Here $\mathrm{E}_1$ denotes expectation under the density determined by $\theta_1$. After observing $n$ samples, the expected total log-LR evidence in favor of $\theta_1$ over $\theta_2$ is exactly $n\cdot I(\theta_1;\theta_2)$.

Three properties to internalize before sliding anything:

  • $I(\theta_1;\theta_2) \geq 0$, with equality iff the densities agree almost everywhere.
  • It is not symmetric: in general $I(\theta_1;\theta_2) \neq I(\theta_2;\theta_1)$, which is why we say divergence, not distance.
  • For iid samples, the law of large numbers makes $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \log\frac{f(y_i;\theta_1)}{f(y_i;\theta_2)} \to I(\theta_1;\theta_2)$ under $\theta_1$.

III
Generalizing

Gaussian Regression, One Design at a Time.

Now the model is $y = Z\beta + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim N(0,\sigma^2 I_n)$, and the parameters are $\theta = (\beta',\sigma^2)'$. After taking $\mathrm{E}_1$ in the log-LR you get a clean two-piece formula:

For Gaussian Regression
$$ I(\theta_1\,;\,\theta_2) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - \log\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - 1\right) \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{(\beta_1-\beta_2)' Z'Z\,(\beta_1-\beta_2)}{n\,\sigma_2^2}. $$

Two pieces, two intuitions. The first is a function of the variance ratio alone — call it $\psi(r)$ with $r = \sigma_1^2/\sigma_2^2$. It is zero at $r=1$, positive otherwise, and (importantly) not symmetric in the roles of $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$. The second is a Mahalanobis-style quadratic in $\beta_1 - \beta_2$, weighted by the design moment $Z'Z/n$.

V
Where the Formula Comes From

The Derivation, in Five Steps.

Why does the Gaussian regression KL split so cleanly into a scale piece and a mean piece? The arithmetic is short. Click to expand.

Show the full derivation of $I(\theta_1;\theta_2)$ for Gaussian regression
Step 1 — write the densities

The likelihood for $y = Z\beta + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim N(0,\sigma^2 I_n)$ is

$$ f(y;\beta,\sigma^2) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(y-Z\beta)'(y-Z\beta)}{2\sigma^2}\right). $$
Step 2 — take the log-ratio
$$ \log\frac{f(y;\theta_1)}{f(y;\theta_2)} = -\frac{n}{2}\log\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - \frac{(y-Z\beta_1)'(y-Z\beta_1)}{2\sigma_1^2} + \frac{(y-Z\beta_2)'(y-Z\beta_2)}{2\sigma_2^2}. $$
Step 3 — take $\mathrm{E}_1$, term by term

Under $\theta_1$, write $y = Z\beta_1 + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma_1^2 I_n)$. Then

$$ \mathrm{E}_1\!\left[(y-Z\beta_1)'(y-Z\beta_1)\right] = \mathrm{E}\!\left[\varepsilon'\varepsilon\right] = n\sigma_1^2. $$

For the alternative,

$$ \mathrm{E}_1\!\left[(y-Z\beta_2)'(y-Z\beta_2)\right] = \mathrm{E}\!\left[(\varepsilon + Z(\beta_1-\beta_2))'(\varepsilon + Z(\beta_1-\beta_2))\right] = n\sigma_1^2 + (\beta_1-\beta_2)'Z'Z(\beta_1-\beta_2). $$

The cross term vanishes because $\mathrm{E}[\varepsilon] = 0$.

Step 4 — assemble
$$ \mathrm{E}_1 \log\frac{f_1}{f_2} = -\frac{n}{2}\log\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - \frac{n}{2} + \frac{n\sigma_1^2}{2\sigma_2^2} + \frac{(\beta_1-\beta_2)'Z'Z(\beta_1-\beta_2)}{2\sigma_2^2}. $$
Step 5 — divide by $n$
$$ I(\theta_1;\theta_2) = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - \log\frac{\sigma_1^2}{\sigma_2^2} - 1\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{(\beta_1-\beta_2)'Z'Z(\beta_1-\beta_2)}{n\sigma_2^2}. \quad\square $$

The result has a satisfying interpretation: variance mismatch and design-weighted mean shift are independent contributions to discriminability, and they trade off cleanly. Notice the role of $Z'Z/n$ — a more spread-out design separates the models faster, which is the same instinct that drives optimal experimental design.

VI
The Bigger Picture

Why It Matters.

Neyman–Pearson tells you the LR test is most powerful for the simple-vs-simple problem. KL tells you how powerful: the LR test's Type II error rate decays exponentially in $n \cdot I(\theta_1;\theta_2)$ (Stein's lemma). So $I$ is literally the discrimination rate.

For Gaussian regression, the decomposition above sharpens this into a usable design principle: variance heterogeneity and design-weighted mean shifts contribute additively, and the design's information content $Z'Z/n$ multiplies the mean-shift term — the same quantity that determines the asymptotic variance of the OLS estimator.

KL also underlies maximum likelihood (the MLE asymptotically minimizes KL to the true distribution), AIC (an estimate of expected KL between the truth and a candidate model), and the likelihood-ratio test's asymptotic chi-squared distribution.
