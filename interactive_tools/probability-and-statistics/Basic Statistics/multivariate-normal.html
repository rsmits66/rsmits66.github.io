§ 1   Introduction

The multivariate normal is a collection of dependent random variables whose entire dependence structure is captured by just two objects: a mean vector \boldsymbol{\mu} and a covariance matrix \boldsymbol{\Sigma}. Every calculation — marginals, conditionals, linear transformations — reduces to block matrix algebra. Most remarkably, for multivariate normals, independence is equivalent to being uncorrelated.

§ 9   Independence = Uncorrelated

Theorem — Independence for Multivariate Normals
Let \mathbf{Y} = (\mathbf{Y}_1', \ldots, \mathbf{Y}_m')' \sim N_n(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}). The subvectors \mathbf{Y}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{Y}_m are independent if and only if \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{ij} = \mathbf{0} for all i \neq j.

Proof (⇐). If all off-diagonal blocks are zero, the joint MGF factors: m_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{t}) = e^{\mathbf{t}'\boldsymbol{\mu} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{t}'\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{t}} = \prod_{i=1}^m m_{\mathbf{Y}_i}(\mathbf{t}_i).

(⇒). If independent, then \text{Cov}(\mathbf{Y}_i, \mathbf{Y}_j) = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{ij} = \mathbf{0}.

Key point: In general, uncorrelated does not imply independent. For multivariate normals, it does. This is because the multivariate normal is completely determined by its first two moments.

Key insight: The conditional mean is a linear function of the conditioning value \mathbf{c}_2 — this is the basis of linear regression. The conditional covariance does not depend on \mathbf{c}_2 at all.

§ 12   Interactive: Scatter Matrix (n ≥ 3)

Beyond three dimensions, geometric visualization breaks down — we cannot draw a 4D ellipsoid. The scatter matrix is the standard substitute: every pairwise marginal laid out at once with full coordinate axes. Histograms on the diagonal show each Y_i with the theoretical normal curve. Below the diagonal: pairwise scatter with 50%/95% density ellipses (the joint geometry). Above the diagonal: the same data with the theoretical regression line E[Y_i \mid Y_j = x] = \mu_i + (\Sigma_{ij}/\Sigma_{jj})(x - \mu_j) overlaid — the conditional mean is exactly linear for the multivariate normal. Samples are drawn via \mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{Z} + \boldsymbol{\mu} where \mathbf{L} is the Cholesky factor of \boldsymbol{\Sigma}.

Diagonal: histogram + normal curve. Below: scatter + density ellipses. Above: scatter + regression line.
Reading the matrix. Each cell carries its own coordinate axes. Below the diagonal the red ellipses are the theoretical 50% and 95% probability contours derived from the 2×2 submatrix of \boldsymbol{\Sigma} — tight, elongated ellipses signal strong correlation; round clouds signal near-independence. Above the diagonal the red line is the exact conditional mean E[Y_i \mid Y_j] — its slope is \Sigma_{ij}/\Sigma_{jj}, the population regression coefficient. For multivariate normals these two summaries (geometry below, regression above) capture everything there is to say about pairwise dependence.
