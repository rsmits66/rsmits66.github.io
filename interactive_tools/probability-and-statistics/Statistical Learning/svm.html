I
The Theory

Margins, slack, and the kernel trick

The separating hyperplane

A support vector machine is, at heart, a way to draw a boundary between two classes of points. In a p-dimensional feature space that boundary is a hyperplane — a flat affine subspace of one dimension less than the space itself. In two dimensions it is a line; in three, an ordinary plane.

A hyperplane is the set of points x satisfying a single linear equation:

β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + ⋯ + βᵖxᵖ = 0

The left-hand side, written f(x), is positive for points on one side and negative for points on the other. That sign is the prediction: if f(x) > 0 we label the point +1, and if f(x) < 0 we label it −1. The magnitude |f(x)| tells us how far from the boundary the point lies — how confident the call is.

The maximal margin classifier

If the two classes can be separated by a hyperplane at all, they can usually be separated by infinitely many. Which one should we choose? The natural answer: the one that sits as far as possible from every training point. The distance from the hyperplane to the nearest point is called the margin, and the maximal margin classifier is the hyperplane that makes that margin as wide as it can be — the fattest possible street with all points off the curb.

Formally, it solves the optimization problem:

maximizeM
over β₀, β₁, …, βᵖ and the margin width M
subject to∑ᵡ βⱼ² = 1
yᵢ f(xᵢ) ≥ M  for every point i

The clever part is the constraint yᵢ f(xᵢ) ≥ M. Because yᵢ is ±1, multiplying by it forces f(xᵢ) to carry the correct sign and to clear the margin M by a comfortable distance. With ∑βⱼ²=1, the quantity yᵢf(xᵢ) is exactly the signed perpendicular distance from point to plane.

Support vectors

Only the handful of points that lie exactly on the margin’s edge press against the solution. These are the support vectors. Move any other point — even a long way — and the boundary does not budge. Move a support vector and the whole classifier shifts. The fitted machine depends only on this small, decisive set.

When no clean split exists

Real data rarely cooperates. Two classes usually overlap, and then no separating hyperplane exists at all — the maximal margin problem simply has no solution. Worse, even when a split is possible, the maximal margin classifier is brittle: a single new observation near the boundary can swing it dramatically, a sign of high variance and poor generalization.

The fix is to permit a few violations — to let some points sit inside the margin, or even on the wrong side of it — in exchange for a boundary that is steadier and more honest about the noise.

The support vector classifier

The support vector classifier, or soft-margin classifier, builds that tolerance in directly. Each point gets a slack variable εᵢ ≥ 0 measuring how badly it violates the margin, and a budget C caps the total slack allowed:

maximizeM
subject to∑ᵡ βⱼ² = 1
yᵢ f(xᵢ) ≥ M(1 − εᵢ)
εᵢ ≥ 0,  ∑ᵢ εᵢ ≤ C

Read the slack like a traffic report. If εᵢ = 0 the point is safely on the right side of its margin. If 0 < εᵢ < 1 it has crept inside the margin but is still correctly classified. If εᵢ > 1 it has crossed all the way to the wrong side — a genuine misclassification.

The budget C is the model’s single most important dial, and it governs the bias–variance trade-off. A large C is permissive: it tolerates many violations, yielding a wide, soft margin supported by many points — lower variance, higher bias. A small C is strict: it allows few violations, producing a narrow margin that hugs the data — lower bias, higher variance. Now every point on or inside the margin is a support vector, so widening the margin recruits more of them and steadies the fit.

A note on terminology. A support vector machine controls its errors with a soft margin — a slack budget — not with a softmax. Softmax is the function that turns scores into class probabilities in logistic-style and neural-network classifiers; it is a different mechanism belonging to a different family of models. An SVM’s output is the signed distance f(x), and its “softness” lives entirely in the slack variables above.

Nonlinear boundaries

A straight boundary is often hopeless — picture one class forming a ring around the other. The remedy is the same trick that linear regression uses for curves: enlarge the feature space. Instead of fitting in the original coordinates x₁, …, xᵖ, add transformed features — squares xⱼ², cross-terms xⱼxₖ, higher powers — and fit a support vector classifier in that bigger space.

A boundary that is perfectly flat in the enlarged space projects back down as a curved boundary in the original space. The mathematics never leaves the comfortable world of hyperplanes; the curvature is an illusion produced by the coordinate change.

Kernels and the kernel trick

Enlarging the feature space by hand is awkward and, for rich enough expansions, impossible — the new space can have thousands of dimensions, or infinitely many. Here the support vector classifier reveals a remarkable property: its solution depends on the training data only through inner products between pairs of points, ⟨xᵢ, xₘ⟩. The individual coordinates never appear on their own.

So we never need the coordinates of the enlarged space at all. We simply replace each inner product with a kernel K(xᵢ, xₘ) — a function that returns what the inner product would have been in some expanded space, computed directly from the original points. This shortcut is the celebrated kernel trick, and the fitted machine becomes:

f(x) = β₀ + ∑i∈S αᵢ K(x, xᵢ)

where S is the set of support vectors. Different kernels build different geometries:

linearK(x,x′) = ⟨x, x′⟩
recovers the ordinary flat support vector classifier
polynomialK(x,x′) = (1 + ⟨x, x′⟩)d
boundaries that curve like degree-d polynomials
radialK(x,x′) = exp(−γ ‖x − x′‖²)
smooth, local boundaries — the workhorse used below

The radial kernel

The radial kernel — also called the RBF or Gaussian kernel — measures similarity by distance: two points score near 1 when close together and fade toward 0 as they separate. A test point is therefore influenced almost entirely by training points in its immediate neighborhood; distant support vectors have essentially no vote. This gives the radial SVM its characteristic local behavior.

The parameter γ > 0 sets the reach. A small γ makes the kernel wide and gentle, each point felt far and wide, yielding a smooth, almost-linear boundary. A large γ makes it narrow and sharp, each point felt only nearby, yielding a wiggly boundary that can wrap individual observations — powerful, but prone to overfitting.

More than two classes

The story so far is binary. With K classes, two extensions are standard. One-versus-one trains a separate SVM for every pair of classes and lets them vote. One-versus-all trains one SVM per class against the combined rest, and assigns the class whose f(x) is largest. The two–class machine remains the fundamental building block.

Choosing the dials

C and γ are not learned from the data the way αᵢ are — they are hyperparameters set from outside. Training accuracy cannot guide the choice, since a large γ can memorize the training set perfectly. The honest tool is cross-validation: try a grid of (C, γ) pairs, estimate each one’s out-of-sample accuracy by repeatedly holding out folds, and keep the pair that generalizes best. The Data

A radial-kernel machine on credit-card defaults

30,000 cardholders, and one question: default next month?

Our example uses the well-known UCI default of credit card clients dataset — 30,000 Taiwanese credit-card customers, each labelled by whether they defaulted on the following month’s payment. To keep the geometry visible we classify on the two most informative features: PAY_0, the most recent repayment status, and LIMIT_BAL, the credit limit. Both are standardized to mean 0 and unit variance.

Defaults are the minority — about 22% of all clients — so the training and test samples below are deliberately enriched to roughly 40% defaulters, letting the minority class show up clearly on the plot. The boundary itself is fit with a radial kernel, and its hyperparameters are chosen by 5-fold cross-validation.

Why a grid, and why folds?

Training accuracy is a flatterer: a large γ can memorize these 300 points almost perfectly and still fail on new clients. So we judge each (C, γ) pair by 5-fold cross-validation — the training set is split into five parts, the machine is fit on four and scored on the fifth, and this rotates until every part has been the test fold once. The five scores are averaged.

The grid sweeps C down the rows and γ across the columns. The crimson-ringed cell marked ★ is the best-scoring pair — the responsible choice. Notice that the strongest cells avoid the high-γ corner: on data this overlapping, a smoother boundary generalizes better than a wiggly one.
