Background — what logistic regression is doing, and the two ways this page fits it

The question behind a yes/no outcome

A Falcon 9 booster either lands or it does not — there is no middle value to average. Record the result of launch i as yi = 1 for a recovered booster and yi = 0 for a lost one. What can change smoothly with a launch's payload mass x is not that label but the chance the label comes up 1. That chance is the quantity the model is built around: write p(x) = Pr(Y = 1 | x), the probability of a successful landing at payload x. It is a number trapped between 0 and 1, and the 0/1 outcomes in the data are the coin flips it produced.

A linear predictor, but kept on a leash

A straight line β0 + β1x is the natural starting point — it is cheap to fit and each coefficient has a plain reading. Used directly as the probability, though, it misbehaves in two ways. First, a line has no ceiling or floor: extend it far enough in either direction and it reports “probabilities” below 0 or above 1, which are not probabilities at all. Second, its slope never changes, so it insists that an extra ton of payload moves the chance of success by the same amount whether that chance started at 50% or at 99% — yet a probability near its limits can barely budge, while one in the middle is free to swing.

The remedy keeps the convenient linear term η = β0 + β1x and feeds it through a function that smoothly compresses the entire number line into the open interval (0, 1):

p(x) = eη ⁄ (1 + eη),  with  η = β0 + β1x  — the logistic, or sigmoid, transform

Whatever value η takes, p(x) lands strictly inside (0, 1); the curve climbs fastest through its middle and levels off toward each end — exactly the cautious behaviour a probability shows as it nears certainty.

Reading the coefficients: odds and log-odds

Because the sigmoid bends the relationship, β1 is no longer “the change in probability per ton” — that change depends on where x already sits. The coefficients become interpretable again once the model is rewritten in terms of the odds, the ratio of the chance of success to the chance of failure. Solving the equation above for that ratio gives

p(x) ⁄ [1 − p(x)] = eβ0+β1x

and a logarithm straightens it back into a line — the log-odds, or logit:

log( p(x) ⁄ [1 − p(x)] ) = β0 + β1x

On this scale the model is linear and the coefficients read cleanly. Because the predictor is centred, x = 0 corresponds to the average payload, so β0 is the log-odds of success for a typical launch; β1 is the shift in log-odds bought by each additional ton. Exponentiating, every extra ton multiplies the odds by the fixed factor eβ1. Its sign is the headline — a negative β1 means heavier payloads make recovery less likely — while its pull on the probability itself is felt most in the steep part of the curve and fades to almost nothing out in the flats.

Two routes to β0 and β1 — and this page walks both

Maximum likelihood. Any candidate pair (β0, β1) assigns a probability to every launch on record: p(xi) to the boosters that landed and 1 − p(xi) to those that did not. Multiplying these numbers together scores how well that pair accounts for the data as a whole,

ℓ(β0, β1) = ∏ successes p(xi) × ∏ failures [1 − p(xi)]

and the maximum-likelihood estimate is just the pair that drives this product — or, in practice, its logarithm, ∑i[ yi log p(xi) + (1 − yi) log(1 − p(xi)) ] — as high as it will go. No tidy formula delivers it the way one delivers a least-squares line, so the page climbs the log-likelihood surface numerically with Newton's method. In Maximum likelihood mode you can also drop your own (β0, β1) onto that surface and see how far your log-likelihood falls short of the summit.

Bayesian sampling with MCMC. The second route declines to commit to a single pair. It opens with a prior — here independent Normal(0, σ2) distributions, a mild statement that implausibly large coefficients are unlikely before any data are seen — and lets the likelihood reshape it into a posterior:

posterior(β0, β1) ∝ likelihood × prior

This posterior has no closed form, so it is charted by simulation. A random-walk Metropolis sampler wanders the (β0, β1) plane, at each step proposing a small random move and keeping or discarding it according to how the posterior density changes. Left to run, it dwells in each region in proportion to that region's posterior probability — so the cloud of retained draws is the answer, its centre supplying point estimates and its spread supplying credible intervals.

Throughout, the payload predictor is centred: the sample mean is subtracted from every value. This leaves the slope β1 and the shape of the fit untouched — it only re-anchors β0 to the average payload and keeps the sampler's two coordinates from moving in lockstep, which lets it explore more efficiently.
