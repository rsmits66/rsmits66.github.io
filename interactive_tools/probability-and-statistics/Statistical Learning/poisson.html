▸ Background — why ordinary linear regression breaks on count data, and how the Poisson model repairs it

The response is a non-negative integer

The Capital Bikeshare system records how many bicycles are rented in each hour. That number — call it Y — is a count: it lives in {0, 1, 2, …} and can never be negative. Ordinary least-squares regression treats Y as an unbounded continuous quantity, and that mismatch has real consequences.

Fit a linear model to these 17,379 hours and it does something nonsensical: it predicts a negative number of bikers for 1,600 of them, dipping as low as −185 rentals on cold, quiet nights. A model that confidently forecasts −185 bicycles is telling you its assumptions are wrong.

The variance is tied to the mean

Linear regression also assumes the noise has constant variance at every level of the prediction. Counts refuse to cooperate. A sleepy 4 a.m. hour scatters by a handful of bikes; a 5 p.m. rush hour scatters by hundreds. The spread grows with the level of demand.

The Poisson distribution

To handle counts we model Y with the Poisson distribution. If Y is Poisson with rate λ > 0, then

Pr(Y = k) = e−λ λk ⁄ k! ,  k = 0, 1, 2, …

and crucially E(Y) = Var(Y) = λ. The mean and variance are the same parameter — so the model builds in exactly the behaviour we observed: louder hours are also more variable hours.

A log-linear model for the rate

We let the rate λ depend on covariates — the hour of day, the month, the weather, the temperature. To keep λ strictly positive we model its logarithm with a linear equation:

log λ(X) = β0 + β1X1 + ··· + βpXp

Because the log is linear, the covariates act multiplicatively on demand: a coefficient β means that a one-unit change in its predictor multiplies expected ridership by eβ. Warm weather doesn't add a fixed number of riders — it scales the whole daily curve upward.

Fitting

The coefficients β are estimated by maximum likelihood. The likelihood equations are non-linear, so they are solved iteratively by Newton–Raphson — equivalently, iteratively reweighted least squares. The panels below fit this model live, in your browser, on all 17,379 hourly records.
