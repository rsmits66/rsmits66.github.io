Reading the four behaviours

The shape of γ(h) as h→0 reveals how the field changes at small scales. Quadratic origin (Gaussian/squared-exponential covariance) means the field is mean-square differentiable — realizations look like smooth terrain with well-defined gradients. Linear origin (exponential covariance) means continuous but nowhere differentiable — like Brownian motion's roughness preserved everywhere. Nugget effect means γ(h) jumps to c₀ > 0 at any positive lag, so even arbitrarily-close samples are partially uncorrelated; the field shows random salt-and-pepper texture on top of any underlying structure. Pure nugget is the limiting case of total decorrelation — spatial white noise.

Slide the Nugget c₀ control to add a measurement-error or microvariation component on top of any of the structured models. In modes A and B, the nugget reveals itself as a discontinuity at the origin in the variogram plot. In mode D the nugget controls the entire variance, and the structural sliders are inactive. In real geostatistical practice, the nugget is fitted from the experimental variogram: you extrapolate the curve back to h=0 and the height of its intercept above zero is c₀. Causes include true short-scale variability below your sampling resolution, plus measurement error in the assays themselves.

Choosing the right model is the heart of variogram fitting. A geologist seeing a quadratic-origin variogram on a deposit would expect smooth, gradual mineralization — perhaps a disseminated porphyry. A linear origin suggests something rougher — alluvial gold, fracture-controlled mineralization. Nugget-dominated variograms are common in nugget gold deposits (literally — the term comes from there) where coarse gold particles produce wildly different assays in adjacent samples. Each behaviour points to different geology, and getting the origin right is essential for kriging to give honest standard errors.
