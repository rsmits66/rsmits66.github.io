Notes on Probability No. 2 — Order Statistics, via the Multinomial 2026

Order statistics, by way of three buckets.

A continuous-distribution use of the multinomial coefficient: by partitioning the real line at a single point and counting how many of the $n$ samples fall in each piece, we read off the density of any order statistic — first one at a time, then two at a time.

A worked derivation · The $k$th order statistic · Joint density of $X_{(j)}$ and $X_{(k)}$

I.The setting

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be independent draws from a continuous distribution with probability density $f$ and cumulative distribution function $F$. Sort them and write $X_{(1)} \le X_{(2)} \le \cdots \le X_{(n)}$ for the resulting order statistics: $X_{(1)}$ is the smallest of the $n$ values, $X_{(n)}$ the largest, and $X_{(k)}$ the $k$th smallest.

The question we want to answer is: what is the probability density of $X_{(k)}$ at a point $y$? The standard textbook derivation differentiates the cdf of $X_{(k)}$. The multinomial argument we give below is more direct, and reads off the density formula by counting alone.

II.Three buckets

Fix a point $y$ on the real line and a small width $dy$. By definition, the density satisfies

$$f_{X_{(k)}}(y)\, dy \;\approx\; P\!\left(y \le X_{(k)} < y + dy\right).$$

Now ask: what does it take for the $k$th smallest sample to land in that small slab $[y, y+dy)$? Exactly three things must happen, all at once. Some $k - 1$ of the $n$ samples must fall to the left of $y$. Exactly one of them must fall in the slab $[y, y+dy)$ — that one will be $X_{(k)}$. And the remaining $n - k$ must fall to the right of $y + dy$. Three buckets, three counts.

A generic density $f$ on the real line, partitioned by the value $y$.

The probabilities for a single sample, read straight off the cdf, are

left bucket
$k - 1$ of the $n$ samples
probability $F(y)$
slab at $y$
exactly $1$ sample
probability $\approx f(y)\,dy$
right bucket
$n - k$ of the $n$ samples
probability $\approx 1 - F(y)$

Because the $n$ samples are independent and each one independently lands in one of the three buckets, the count vector $(N_1, N_2, N_3) = (k-1,\, 1,\, n-k)$ is multinomial with probabilities $\bigl(F(y),\, f(y)\,dy,\, 1 - F(y)\bigr)$ — and the multinomial PMF gives the probability of this particular allocation directly:

$$P(\text{counts} = (k-1, 1, n-k)) \;=\; \frac{n!}{(k-1)!\,1!\,(n-k)!}\; F(y)^{k-1}\,\bigl[f(y)\,dy\bigr]^{1}\,\bigl[1 - F(y)\bigr]^{n-k}.$$

This probability is exactly $f_{X_{(k)}}(y)\, dy$. Dividing both sides by $dy$ gives the result.

$$\boxed{\quad f_{X_{(k)}}(y) \;=\; \frac{n!}{(k-1)!\,(n-k)!}\, F(y)^{k-1}\,\bigl[1 - F(y)\bigr]^{n-k}\, f(y).\quad}$$

The terms partition cleanly. The polynomial $F(y)^{k-1}\,[1-F(y)]^{n-k}$ is the chance of getting the right split of how many fall on each side of $y$; the factor $f(y)$ is the density of placing a sample exactly at $y$; and the multinomial coefficient counts the number of ways the labels can be assigned — which of the $n$ samples is the one at $y$, which $k-1$ are below it, and which $n-k$ are above.

A subtle but important point: the rigorous version of this argument needs to handle the $o(dy)$ corrections — the chance of getting $\ge 2$ samples in the slab $[y, y+dy)$ shrinks like $dy^2$ and so vanishes in the limit. But the bookkeeping with one-sample-in-slab is exactly what survives, and the multinomial coefficient is the right counting object. The argument is an honest derivation, not just a mnemonic.

III.The result, looked at

For the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$, where $F(y) = y$ and $f(y) = 1$ on the unit interval, the density formula above collapses to

$$f_{X_{(k)}}(y) \;=\; \frac{n!}{(k-1)!\,(n-k)!}\, y^{k-1}\,(1-y)^{n-k} \;=\; \mathrm{Beta}(k,\, n-k+1).$$

So the $k$th order statistic of $n$ uniforms is a Beta random variable with shape parameters $k$ and $n - k + 1$. The mean is $k/(n+1)$ and the mode is $(k-1)/(n-1)$, both of which give exactly the intuition the picture should give: the $k$th smallest of $n$ uniforms lives, on average, near the $k/(n+1)$ marker on the unit interval.

The widget below shows this for $n = 17$. Drag the slider to choose $k$. The curve is the theoretical Beta density; the bars are a histogram of $1500$ simulated samples — each obtained by drawing $17$ uniforms, sorting them, and recording the $k$th smallest.

$X_{(k)} \sim \mathrm{Beta}(k,\, n-k+1)$ for $X_i \sim U(0,1)$. Bars: $1500$ Monte Carlo replicates. Curve: theoretical density. Dashed line: mean $k/(n+1)$.

# Density of the kth order statistic: the multinomial-coefficient formula
order_stat_density <- function(y, k, n, f, F) {
  factorial(n) / (factorial(k - 1) * factorial(n - k)) *
    F(y)^(k - 1) * (1 - F(y))^(n - k) * f(y)
}

# For uniform draws this is exactly Beta(k, n - k + 1)
n <- 17; k <- 9
y <- seq(0, 1, length = 400)
plot(y, dbeta(y, k, n - k + 1), type = "l", lwd = 2, col = "#9a1a1a",
     xlab = "y", ylab = "density",
     main = sprintf("Density of X_(%d) for n = %d uniforms", k, n))

# Verify by Monte Carlo
sims <- replicate(5000, sort(runif(n))[k])
hist(sims, breaks = 40, freq = FALSE, add = TRUE,
     col = rgb(0.18, 0.39, 0.39, 0.4), border = NA)

IV.Five buckets — two order statistics at once

The same reasoning generalizes immediately. Suppose we want the joint density of two order statistics $X_{(j)}$ and $X_{(k)}$ at points $y_j < y_k$, where $j < k$. Pick small widths $dy_j$ and $dy_k$. The event $\{y_j \le X_{(j)} < y_j + dy_j,\, y_k \le X_{(k)} < y_k + dy_k\}$ is exactly the event that the $n$ samples partition themselves into five intervals with the right counts:

The same density $f$, partitioned now at the two values $y_j$ and $y_k$.

far left
$j - 1$ samples
probability $F(y_j)$
slab at $y_j$
exactly $1$
probability $\approx f(y_j)\,dy_j$
middle
$k - j - 1$ samples
probability $F(y_k) - F(y_j)$
slab at $y_k$
exactly $1$
probability $\approx f(y_k)\,dy_k$
far right
$n - k$ samples
probability $1 - F(y_k)$

Multinomial bookkeeping with five categories gives

$$\frac{n!}{(j-1)!\,(k - j - 1)!\,(n - k)!}\; F(y_j)^{j-1}\,\bigl[F(y_k) - F(y_j)\bigr]^{k - j - 1}\,\bigl[1 - F(y_k)\bigr]^{n - k}\; f(y_j)\, f(y_k)\, dy_j\, dy_k,$$

where the two factorials of $1$ in the denominator have been suppressed. This is $f_{X_{(j)},\, X_{(k)}}(y_j, y_k)\, dy_j\, dy_k$, so dividing through:

$$\boxed{\quad f_{X_{(j)},\,X_{(k)}}(y_j, y_k) \;=\; \frac{n!}{(j-1)!\,(k-j-1)!\,(n-k)!}\, F(y_j)^{j-1}\bigl[F(y_k) - F(y_j)\bigr]^{k-j-1}\bigl[1-F(y_k)\bigr]^{n-k}\, f(y_j)\,f(y_k)\quad}$$

valid for $y_j < y_k$ (and zero otherwise, since $X_{(j)} \le X_{(k)}$ by construction). The general pattern is now visible: for any subset $X_{(k_1)}, \ldots, X_{(k_r)}$ of order statistics, the joint density at compatible points is read off a multinomial with $2r + 1$ buckets — the gaps between the points and the slabs at the points themselves.

V.Seventeen uniforms, the ninth and the thirteenth

Take $17$ independent draws from $U(0,1)$, sort them, and ask for the joint density of $X_{(9)}$ and $X_{(13)}$ at the point $(y_j, y_k) = (0.3,\, 0.7)$ — which is the question raised at the end of the dice-and-intervals lecture. With $F(y) = y$ and $f(y) = 1$, the formula simplifies. The probability factors are

$$F(y_j) = 0.30, \qquad F(y_k) - F(y_j) = 0.40, \qquad 1 - F(y_k) = 0.30,$$

and the multinomial coefficient is

$$\frac{17!}{8!\,3!\,4!} \;=\; 61{,}261{,}200.$$

Putting it together:

$$f_{X_{(9)},\,X_{(13)}}(0.3, 0.7) \;=\; 61{,}261{,}200 \times (0.3)^{8} \times (0.4)^{3} \times (0.3)^{4} \;\approx\; 2.0836.$$
Joint density
f(9, 13)(0.3, 0.7) ≈ 2.0836

For comparison, the marginal densities at the same points are $f_{X_{(9)}}(0.3) \approx 0.828$ and $f_{X_{(13)}}(0.7) \approx 3.469$. Their product is $2.871$ — strictly greater than the joint $2.084$, which is the signature of the (positive) dependence between the two order statistics being examined at discordant values: $0.3$ sits below the mean $0.500$ of $X_{(9)}$, while $0.7$ sits very near the mean $0.722$ of $X_{(13)}$, so the two events together are less likely than independence would suggest.

# Joint density of X_(j) and X_(k) for n iid samples from a uniform
joint_uniform <- function(yj, yk, j, k, n) {
  if (yj >= yk) return(0)
  factorial(n) / (factorial(j - 1) *
                  factorial(k - j - 1) *
                  factorial(n - k)) *
    yj^(j - 1) * (yk - yj)^(k - j - 1) * (1 - yk)^(n - k)
}

joint_uniform(yj = 0.3, yk = 0.7, j = 9, k = 13, n = 17)
#> [1] 2.083622

# Sanity-check by Monte Carlo: count how often (X_(9), X_(13)) lands
# in a small box around (0.3, 0.7), divide by box area and N
set.seed(3)
N <- 5e6; eps <- 0.02
hits <- replicate(N, {
  s <- sort(runif(17))
  abs(s[9] - 0.3) < eps/2 && abs(s[13] - 0.7) < eps/2
})
mean(hits) / eps^2      # Monte Carlo estimate of the joint density
#> [1] 2.087   (close to 2.0836)

VI.What we have

The multinomial gives the entire order-statistics theory in one move. The density of $X_{(k)}$ comes from a three-bucket partition with counts $(k-1,\, 1,\, n-k)$; the joint density of $X_{(j)}$ and $X_{(k)}$ comes from a five-bucket partition with counts $(j-1,\, 1,\, k - j - 1,\, 1,\, n - k)$; and an $r$-fold joint comes from a $(2r+1)$-bucket partition by the same logic. The multinomial coefficient counts the ways to assign the $n$ sample labels to those buckets, the powers of the cdf differences supply the probability of getting the right split, and the factors of $f$ at the chosen points fill in the slabs. The dice picture, the interval picture, and the order-statistic picture are all the same picture.
