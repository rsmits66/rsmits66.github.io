From p-values to e-values

A coin flipped ten times
By Georgia Smits — a walk through statistical evidence
Part I · The sample space

An experiment

Suppose we flip a coin ten times. Our null hypothesis H0 says the flips are independent, and that each is equally likely heads or tails.

An outcome is a sequence of ten letters — for instance, HHTHTTHHTH. The set of all such outcomes is the sample space Ω, with 210 = 1024 points: HHHHHHHHHH, HHHHHHHHHT, …, TTTTTTTTTT. Together with the power-set σ-algebra ℱ on Ω and the probability measure P induced by H0 — every singleton has mass 1/1024 — the triple (Ω, ℱ, P) is the probability space we work in throughout.

Two random-variable summaries of evidence will concern us. A p-variable is a random variable 𝔭: Ω → [0,1] super-uniform under P, meaning P(𝔭 ≤ α) ≤ α for every α ∈ [0,1] — small 𝔭 is evidence against H0. An e-variable is a nonnegative random variable 𝔢: Ω → [0,∞] with EP[𝔢] ≤ 1 — large 𝔢 is evidence (Markov: P(𝔢 ≥ k) ≤ 1/k). Part II builds four classical p-variables on this Ω; Part III calibrates them into e-variables.

Part II · A first hypothesis test

The classical p-value

We pit the fair-coin null against three classical alternatives:

H0: p = ½ the coin is fair
H1: p > ½,  p < ½,  or  p ≠ ½ three classical alternatives

Let X be the number of heads. For p > ½ and p < ½, the level sets of X partition Ω into eleven equivalence classes. For p ≠ ½, the natural classes are level sets of |X − 5| — just six, with HHHHHHHHHH and TTTTTTTTTT collapsing into a single most-extreme class.

Each alternative defines its own p-variable Ω → [0,1]:

𝔭+(ω) = P(X ≥ X(ω)) upper-tail
𝔭−(ω) = P(X ≤ X(ω)) lower-tail
𝔭2(ω) = P(|X − 5| ≥ |X(ω) − 5|) two-sided

Less obvious tests are p-variables too. Let R be the number of consecutive runs of identical symbols: R(HHHHHTTTTT) = 2, R(HTHTHTHTHT) = 10, R(HHHTTTTHHH) = 3. Under H0, each of the nine adjacencies between consecutive flips independently changes (probability ½) or doesn't, so R − 1 ~ Bin(9, ½). We reject when R is small — long runs suggest dependence — and a fourth p-variable joins the list:

𝔭streak(ω) = P(R ≤ R(ω)) runs test

For the same observation HHTHTHTHHH (seven heads, R = 7), the four read 0.1719, 0.9453, 0.3438, and 0.9102 — same data, four verdicts. And the streak test's classes look nothing like the others: HHHHHHHHHH and TTTTTTTTTT land in the most-extreme class (R=1) together, while HTHTHTHTHT — perfectly fair-looking by head count — goes to the very top, the least extreme by R.

Geometrically, each p-variable carves Ω into level sets. As α grows from 0 to 1, the rejection region 𝔭−1([0,α]) — the set of ω with 𝔭(ω) ≤ α — sweeps Ω from the bottom upward through these nested bands, accumulating P-mass at most α at every step. The condition P(𝔭 ≤ α) ≤ α is exactly that nestedness.

A p-variable on (Ω, ℱ, P) is any random variable 𝔭: Ω → [0,1] with P(𝔭 ≤ α) ≤ α for every α ∈ [0, 1]. The classical p-values drawn here are the prototypes.

Part III · From p-values to e-values

Calibrators

A calibrator is a decreasing function f: [0,1] → [0, ∞] that turns p-variables into e-variables: for any probability space (Ω, ℱ, P) and any p-variable 𝔭, the random variable f(𝔭) is an e-variable on (Ω, ℱ, P) — nonnegative with EP[f(𝔭)] ≤ 1. A calibrator f dominates g if f ≥ g, and is admissible if not strictly dominated.

Vovk & Wang's Proposition 2.1 reduces the definition to a clean integral criterion:

f is a calibrator  ⇔  ∫01 f ≤ 1;   admissible  ⇔  f upper semicontinuous, f(0) = ∞, ∫01 f = 1.

The simplest one-parameter family is

fκ(p) := κ pκ−1,   κ ∈ (0, 1).

Each fκ is decreasing, blows up at p = 0, equals κ at p = 1, and integrates to [pκ]01 = 1; admissible. The exponent κ tunes how aggressively small p-values get amplified.

The four markers all live on a single curve at any given κ. Different choices of κ reward different ranges of evidence: small κ rewards extreme p-values dramatically and is harsh on moderate ones; large κ is gentler, with fκ(1) = κ approaching one. No single κ is best.

Averaging the family over κ — treating κ as itself uniform on [0,1] — gives a parameter-free calibrator with a closed form:

F(p) = ∫01 κ pκ−1 dκ = 1 − p + p ln pp (ln p)2

This is itself a calibrator (Fubini gives ∫ F = ∫∫ fκ dp dκ = 1; for the direct check, the substitution t = −ln p reduces ∫01 F to ∫0∞ (1 − (1+t)e−t) / t2 dt, which integrates by parts to 1). It is admissible: F(0) = ∞, F(1) = ½, monotone decreasing throughout.

As κ → 0, fκ concentrates on tiny p-values (admissible but harsh). As κ → 1, fκ → 1 uniformly — the trivial calibrator. F sits in between, smooth and parameter-free, and is the prototypical default when κ hasn't been chosen.
