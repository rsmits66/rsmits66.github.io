Worked Example · The Two-Coin Mixture

Two coins, four sequences,
and the art of guessing better.

Someone flipped one of two biased coins (A or B) ten times in a row, four times over. We don't know which coin produced which sequence. EM lets us estimate both coins' bias from the data — by alternating between soft-assigning sequences to coins and re-estimating the biases.

The Data — four sequences of 10 flips

Each row is one experiment. Only the H/T counts matter for the math, but showing the flips reminds us these are observed coin tosses.

STEP 1 The E-step — soft-assign each sequence to the coins

For every sequence we ask: given the current estimates of θA and θB, how likely was each coin to have produced this? We compute the likelihood under each coin, normalize them into probabilities, and then split that sequence's heads and tails between the two coins in proportion to those probabilities.

STEP 2 The M-step — update parameters from the totals

Once we've added up the attributed heads and tails for each coin, the update is just the obvious thing: the new estimate of P(heads) is (total attributed heads) divided by (total attributed heads + total attributed tails). Then we go back to Step 1 with the new values.

THEORY The math behind EM — why iterating Q improves L

EM works because each iteration optimizes a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood that touches the true objective at the current estimate. Here's the derivation — the same machinery applies to coins, Gaussians, hidden Markov models, and any other mixture.

Setup

Let $L(\theta) = \log p(x \mid \theta)$ be the log-likelihood we wish to maximize. The data $x$ alone makes this hard (we'd have to sum over all latent assignments inside the log). So we introduce the complete data $c$ — observed data plus the missing labels — and write:

$$ L(\theta) \;=\; \log p(x \mid \theta) \;=\; \log \int_C p(x, c \mid \theta)\, dc $$
xobserved data (the H/T counts) ccomplete data — observed + the latent coin label θparameter we want to estimate θ̂⁽ᵗ⁾our current estimate, at iteration $t$

Introduce the current estimate, multiply by 1

Since $c$ encodes $x$, we can drop $x$ inside the joint: $p(x, c \mid \theta) = p(c \mid \theta)$. Then we multiply and divide by $p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})$ — the posterior over latents given where we currently sit:

$$ \begin{aligned} L(\theta) &= \log \int_C p(c \mid \theta)\, dc \\ &= \log \int_C \frac{p(c \mid \theta)}{p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})}\; p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})\, dc \\ &= \log\, E_{c \mid x,\, \hat\theta^{(t)}}\!\left[\frac{p(c \mid \theta)}{p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})}\right] \end{aligned} $$

Apply Jensen's inequality

Because $\log$ is concave, $\log E[Y] \geq E[\log Y]$ for any positive random variable $Y$. That gives us a lower bound on $L(\theta)$:

$$ L(\theta) \;\geq\; E_{c \mid x,\, \hat\theta^{(t)}}\!\left[\log \frac{p(c \mid \theta)}{p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})}\right] $$

Split the log of a ratio into a difference:

$$ \begin{aligned} L(\theta) &\geq\; \underbrace{E_{c \mid x,\, \hat\theta^{(t)}}[\log p(c \mid \theta)]}_{\displaystyle Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(t)})} \;+\; \underbrace{E_{c \mid x,\, \hat\theta^{(t)}}[-\log p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})]}_{\displaystyle h(c, x, \hat\theta^{(t)})} \end{aligned} $$

The second term $h$ does not depend on $\theta$ — it's the entropy of the current posterior. So maximizing the bound over $\theta$ is the same as maximizing $Q$.

Why improving Q improves L

We just showed $L(\theta) \;\geq\; Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(t)}) + h$. At $\theta = \hat\theta^{(t)}$ specifically, the inequality becomes an equality (the ratio inside the log is 1 for the current parameters), so:

$$ L(\hat\theta^{(t)}) \;=\; Q(\hat\theta^{(t)} \mid \hat\theta^{(t)}) + h $$

Define $\hat\theta^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_\theta Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(t)})$. Then chaining together:

$$ L(\hat\theta^{(t+1)}) \;\geq\; Q(\hat\theta^{(t+1)} \mid \hat\theta^{(t)}) + h \;\geq\; Q(\hat\theta^{(t)} \mid \hat\theta^{(t)}) + h \;=\; L(\hat\theta^{(t)}) $$
The guarantee. Each EM iteration is monotone in the actual log-likelihood — it can never go down. It can stall at a local maximum or a saddle point, which is why the choice of initial guesses (above!) matters.

What is the Q function for our coins?

Each sequence $i$ has $x_i$ heads and $10 - x_i$ tails. Conditional on coin $c \in \{A, B\}$, the complete-data log-likelihood for one sequence is $x_i \log\theta + (10 - x_i)\log(1-\theta)$. Taking the expectation under the current posterior $p(c \mid x, \hat\theta^{(t)})$ — which is exactly the $P(A)$, $P(B)$ from the E-step table — and summing over sequences:

$$ \begin{aligned} Q(\theta \mid \hat\theta^{(t)}) &= \sum_{c \in \{A,B\}} \sum_{i=1}^{4} \Big[ x_i\, \log\theta_c \;+\; (10 - x_i)\log(1-\theta_c) \Big]\, p(c \mid x_i,\, \hat\theta^{(t)}) \end{aligned} $$

Group the heads and tails by coin — that's the totals row of the worksheet — and the entire expression collapses to:

$$ Q \;=\; H_A \log\theta_A + T_A \log(1-\theta_A) + H_B \log\theta_B + T_B \log(1-\theta_B) $$

where $H_A, T_A, H_B, T_B$ are the attributed totals. Now differentiate with respect to $\theta_A$:

$$ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial \theta_A} \;=\; \frac{H_A}{\theta_A} \;-\; \frac{T_A}{1 - \theta_A} \;=\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \theta_A^{(t+1)} \;=\; \frac{H_A}{H_A + T_A} $$

And symmetrically for $\theta_B$. That's exactly the M-step update we performed by hand in the worksheet.

Tying it together

Every column in the worked example has a place in this picture. The likelihoods $L(A), L(B)$ are the inputs to the posterior. The columns $P(A), P(B)$ are the posterior $p(c \mid x_i, \hat\theta^{(t)})$ — the weights inside the expectation defining $Q$. The attributed totals $H_A, T_A, H_B, T_B$ are the sufficient statistics of $Q$. And the M-step formula $\theta_A^{(t+1)} = H_A / (H_A + T_A)$ is the closed-form maximizer of $Q$.

EXAMPLE 2 Gaussian mixtures — same algorithm, continuous data

Instead of coin flips, imagine each datum is a 2D point drawn from one of two Gaussian distributions $\mathcal{N}(\mu_A, I)$ and $\mathcal{N}(\mu_B, I)$ (equal variance, for simplicity). We want to estimate the two means $\mu_A$ and $\mu_B$.

If we knew the labels

Each point is colored by its true source. Estimating each mean is trivial — just average the points in each group. The "+" markers below show those true sample means.

If we don't

The same points, with no colors. Where are the cluster centers? We can guess from the shape — but with overlapping clusters, higher-dimensional data, or unequal variances, this gets hard fast.

EM for Gaussian Means — Interactive

Same two steps as the coin example, but $\gamma_i = p(\text{coin A} \mid x_i, \hat\mu_A^{(t)}, \hat\mu_B^{(t)})$ is now computed from Gaussian densities rather than Bernoulli ones. The M-step becomes a weighted average: $\mu_A^{(t+1)} = \sum_i \gamma_i x_i / \sum_i \gamma_i$.
