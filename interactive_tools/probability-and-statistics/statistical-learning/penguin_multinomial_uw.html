Background — how logistic regression stretches from two classes to several

When the answer has more than two boxes

Ordinary logistic regression answers a yes/no question with a single probability. A penguin, though, is not a yes/no: every bird in this dataset is an Adélie, a Chinstrap, or a Gentoo. With K = 3 possible labels the model must report three probabilities at once — one per species — and, since the bird is certainly some species, those three numbers have to add up to 1. Write them pk(x) = Pr(Y = k | x), each a function of the measurements x taken from the bird.

One linear score per species, then a shared squash

The idea that carries over from the two-class case is the linear predictor. Give every species its own linear score from the measurements,

ηk(x) = βk0 + βk1x1 + βk2x2

A score is just a number on the whole real line — larger means “this measurement profile looks more like species k.” To turn three scores into three probabilities that are positive and sum to 1, they are passed together through the softmax transform: exponentiate each score and divide by the total.

pk(x) = eηk(x) ⁄ ∑ j eηj(x)

Only the differences between scores matter to the softmax, so one species can be pinned as a reference with its score fixed at zero (here Adélie, η0 ≡ 0). That leaves two genuine score equations to estimate — Chinstrap-versus-Adélie and Gentoo-versus-Adélie — and reading eβ still gives an odds interpretation, now an odds relative to the reference species.

From probabilities to a map

At any point in the measurement plane the model produces three probabilities; the species with the largest one is its prediction. Sweep across the whole plane and those predictions tile it into decision regions — the coloured patches in the main chart. The borders between regions are where the top two species are tied, and because each score is linear, every border is a straight line. The three per-species panels below show the same fit a different way: each is the smooth probability surface for one species on its own.

Fitting, and a gentle brake

Estimation works exactly as in the two-class case. Each candidate set of coefficients assigns every observed penguin the probability the model gives to that bird's true species; multiplying those across all 342 birds gives the likelihood, and the fit climbs the log-likelihood with Newton's method. One wrinkle is special to clean data: the three penguin species separate so neatly that, left unchecked, the coefficients would race off toward infinity chasing a perfect fit. A small ridge penalty −(λ/2)∑β2 on the slopes acts as a brake — it subtracts a little for large coefficients, so the fit settles at finite, sensible values. The λ slider sets how firm that brake is; nudging it to 0 on a well-separated pair of measurements shows the coefficients beginning to bolt.

Both measurements are standardized (each centred and scaled to unit standard deviation) before fitting, so the estimated slopes are directly comparable and Newton's method stays well-conditioned.
