A walkthrough for undergraduates

Handling Sparsity
via the Horseshoe

On the prior that mimics Bayesian model averaging — and the Markov chain that brings it to life.

After Carvalho · Polson · Scott  ·  AISTATS 2009
I

The sparsity problem, restated

Imagine a regression with hundreds of candidate predictors and a quiet suspicion that most of the true coefficients are exactly zero. You want an estimator that does two contradictory things at once: erase the noise and leave the real signals alone.

These goals fight each other. Classical regularizers — ridge, the LASSO — have a single shrinkage knob. Turn it up and you successfully kill noise but also crush genuine signals. Turn it down and signals survive, but noise leaks through. Every one-knob method must compromise.

The model under discussion is the standard linear-Gaussian setup,

$$ y \sim N(X\beta,\, \sigma^2 I), \qquad X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}, \quad \beta \in \mathbb{R}^{p} $$

with the twist that $p$ may be large and $\beta$ is believed to be sparse. From a Bayesian view there are two ways to encode that belief. The first — the discrete mixture, or spike-and-slab — assigns each $\beta_i$ exact probability of being zero. Philosophically clean, computationally vicious: with $p$ predictors there are $2^p$ sparsity patterns to consider. The second — the shrinkage prior — uses a continuous prior centered at zero that pulls estimates inward but never sets them exactly to zero. Easy to fit, but never truly sparse.

The horseshoe lives in the second camp and tries, remarkably successfully, to behave like the first.

II

The horseshoe prior

The construction is a two-level scale mixture of normals,

$$ \begin{aligned} \beta_i \mid \lambda_i, \tau &\sim N\!\big(0,\; \lambda_i^2 \tau^2\big) \\ \lambda_i &\sim C^+(0,1) \\ \tau &\sim C^+(0,1) \end{aligned} $$

Each coefficient is normal with its own variance, and each variance has a half-Cauchy distribution. Two scales do all the work: $\tau$ is the global shrinkage parameter, calibrating overall sparsity, and $\lambda_i$ is the local shrinkage parameter, letting individual coefficients escape the global pull. The half-Cauchy is the secret ingredient — its heavy tail means a genuine signal can grab a large $\lambda_i$ and avoid being crushed even when $\tau$ is small.

The resulting marginal density on $\beta_i$ has two features that no single-knob shrinkage prior can match: an infinitely tall spike at the origin, which squashes noise components to zero, and Cauchy-like flat tails, which let strong signals survive un-shrunk.

The horseshoe prior (purple) compared with the Laplacian (gold, used by the LASSO) and the Student-t with one degree of freedom (slate). All three are symmetric about zero with heavy tails, but only the horseshoe has an unbounded spike at the origin — its solid curve transitions into dashes as it climbs past the chart bounds, signalling the blow-up to infinity as $\beta \to 0$.

§The horseshoe shape

Fix $\sigma = \tau = 1$ and define the shrinkage coefficient

$$ \kappa_i = \frac{1}{1 + \lambda_i^2} \in [0,1], \qquad E(\beta_i \mid y_i, \lambda_i) = (1 - \kappa_i)\, y_i. $$

Then $\kappa_i = 0$ means "no shrinkage, trust the data," and $\kappa_i = 1$ means "total shrinkage to zero." Under $\lambda_i \sim C^+(0,1)$, the induced prior on $\kappa_i$ turns out to be $\mathrm{Beta}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$ — a U-shaped density that piles probability at both endpoints. That shape resembles a horseshoe, and that is where the prior gets its name.

Compare this to the Laplacian prior behind the LASSO, whose induced density on $\kappa$ flattens out and never commits enthusiastically to either extreme. It cannot say "this is signal, leave it alone." It cannot say "this is noise, zero it out." It can only compromise.

Induced priors on the shrinkage weight $\kappa \in [0,1]$ for four scale-mixture priors. Only the horseshoe is unbounded at both endpoints — the signature U-shape that lets the model commit decisively to "signal" ($\kappa \approx 0$) or "noise" ($\kappa \approx 1$).

The horseshoe shrinks globally through $\tau$, yet acts locally through $\lambda_i$.

§Robustness, formally

There is a clean identity for the posterior mean: $E(\beta \mid y^\ast) = y^\ast + \frac{d}{dy^\ast} \ln m(y^\ast)$, where $m$ is the marginal of the data. A well-behaved sparse-friendly prior should make that derivative vanish for large $|y^\ast|$ — meaning the estimator stops shrinking when the data shout. The horseshoe satisfies this. The LASSO does not: it shrinks every coefficient by a constant amount regardless of magnitude, and that constant grows precisely when $\tau$ is small. In sparse problems, the LASSO over-shrinks the very signals it most needs to recover.

IV

Markov chain Monte Carlo

The posterior

$$ \pi(\beta, \lambda, \tau, \sigma^2 \mid y) \;\propto\; p(y \mid \beta, \sigma^2)\, \pi(\beta \mid \lambda, \tau)\, \pi(\lambda)\, \pi(\tau)\, \pi(\sigma^2) $$

has no closed form. We cannot evaluate its normalizing constant, we cannot integrate it analytically, and the parameter space has dimension $2p + 2$. What we can do is evaluate it pointwise up to proportionality. That is exactly the situation Markov chain Monte Carlo was invented for: a family of algorithms that turn "I can evaluate the density up to a constant" into "I can draw samples from it."

The specific algorithm that suits the horseshoe is the Gibbs sampler. Rather than update the whole vector at once, update one block at a time, each draw coming from that block's full conditional distribution. A theorem of Geman & Geman guarantees that the resulting Markov chain has the target posterior as its stationary distribution; the practical art is choosing blocks whose full conditionals are easy to sample from. The scale-mixture-of-normals representation makes that easy here — conditional on the scales, everything is Gaussian.

a

Update $\beta$ given $\lambda, \tau, \sigma^2$

Let $\Lambda = \tau^2\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1^2, \ldots, \lambda_p^2)$. The full conditional is a standard ridge-regression posterior with a per-coefficient penalty:

$$ \beta \mid \cdot \sim N(\mu_\beta, \Sigma_\beta), \quad \Sigma_\beta = \!\left(\frac{X^\top X}{\sigma^2} + \Lambda^{-1}\right)^{\!-1}\!, \quad \mu_\beta = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\, \Sigma_\beta X^\top y. $$
b

Update $\sigma^2$ given $\beta$

With Jeffreys' prior $\pi(\sigma) \propto 1/\sigma$, the residual sum of squares gives a clean inverse-gamma update:

$$ \sigma^2 \mid \cdot \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}\!\left(\tfrac{n}{2},\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\|y - X\beta\|^2\right). $$
c

Update each $\lambda_i^2$ via auxiliary variables

The half-Cauchy is not conjugate, so use the identity $\lambda_i^2 \mid \nu_i \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, 1/\nu_i)$ with $\nu_i \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)$. Both updates are then conjugate:

$$ \nu_i \mid \cdot \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}\!\left(1,\; 1 + \tfrac{1}{\lambda_i^2}\right), \quad \lambda_i^2 \mid \cdot \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}\!\left(1,\; \tfrac{1}{\nu_i} + \tfrac{\beta_i^2}{2\tau^2}\right). $$
d

Update $\tau^2$ via the same trick

With auxiliary $\xi \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)$ and $\tau^2 \mid \xi \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, 1/\xi)$,

$$ \xi \mid \cdot \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}\!\left(1,\; 1 + \tfrac{1}{\tau^2}\right), \quad \tau^2 \mid \cdot \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}\!\left(\tfrac{p+1}{2},\; \tfrac{1}{\xi} + \sum_{i=1}^p \tfrac{\beta_i^2}{2\lambda_i^2}\right). $$

Iterate steps a–d for $M$ rounds (typically $M \approx 10^4$). Discard the first $B$ samples as burn-in. The remaining $\beta^{(B+1)}, \ldots, \beta^{(M)}$ are correlated draws from the posterior, and the Bayes estimator is their average:

$$ \hat\beta_i = \frac{1}{M - B} \sum_{m = B+1}^{M} \beta_i^{(m)}, \qquad \hat\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{M - B} \sum_{m = B+1}^{M} (\sigma^2)^{(m)}. $$

Beyond point estimates, the chain gives you the full posterior almost for free — credible intervals come from sample quantiles, and the posterior of $\kappa_i$ tells you whether the model thinks coefficient $i$ is signal or noise.

V

A benchmark: Experiment 2, Case 1

The clearest illustration of the horseshoe's advantage comes from the regression experiment in Carvalho, Polson & Scott (2009). A ten-dimensional vector of true coefficients is fixed, then padded with $(p - 10)$ zeros to test how well each method recovers the signal as the ambient dimension grows. Random design matrices are simulated with mildly correlated columns; $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ with standard-normal noise. Sample size $n$ grows linearly with $p$.

— ANNOTATION —

Two structural features of Case 1 explain the gap. First, the vector is genuinely sparse: ten nonzero entries out of $p$, with the sparsity proportion growing from 50% at $p=20$ to 2.5% at $p=400$. The LASSO's single shrinkage parameter has no way to be aggressive enough on the zeros without bleeding into the small signals.

Second, and more decisively, one coefficient equals $20$ — an extreme outlier among the signals. The LASSO's bounded-influence bound does not decay to zero, so it shrinks this $20$ as forcefully as it shrinks a $2$. The horseshoe's heavy-tailed local scale $\lambda_i$ lets that one coefficient grow its own private variance and escape the global pull. The Bayes estimator approaches $y_i$ rather than dragging it toward the mean.

At $p = 100$ the horseshoe's MSE is roughly one-third of the LASSO's. At $p = 200$ it is less than half. The horseshoe is not winning by a small constant factor — it is winning because it is doing something the LASSO structurally cannot.

VI

Watch the chain run

The algorithm above is one thing on paper; it is another to see a chain actually move. The panel below simulates Case 1 with $p = 20$, $n = 24$, $\sigma = 1$, generates a fresh design matrix $X$ and response $y$, and then runs the Gibbs sampler from Section IV for $M = 10{,}000$ iterations.

Five snapshots are displayed in log-spaced order. Iteration $m = 1$ is the very first draw — essentially nonsense, sampled from a near-flat conditional starting at $\beta^{(0)} = 0$. Iterations $m = 10$ and $m = 100$ are burn-in; the chain is finding its way toward the posterior's high-probability region. By $m = 1000$ the chain has converged, and $m = 10{,}000$ is the final draw. The posterior mean $\hat\beta$, averaged over the post-burn-in samples, sits at the bottom and should reliably recover the true $\beta_{1:10} = (2,2,2,2,2,2,2,2,5,20)$ with the remaining ten entries near zero.
