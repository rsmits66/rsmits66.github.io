What you're seeing

Each realization is a synthetic deposit drawn from the same probability distribution. The field is Gaussian, has constant mean ≈ 225, and has the variogram shown on the right. The "sample snapshot" overlay shows what a 4×4 grid of 1×1 sampling cells would yield on this particular realization — i.e., the average of the field over each 1×1 cell. Click "New realization" and the snapshots change because you're seeing a different deposit.

The variogram has a substantial nugget (c₀=10000, about a third of the structured sill C=28000), which shows up as cell-to-cell jitter on top of the smooth spherical structure. The range a=5.75 sample-spacings means features extend roughly 5–6 sample-widths before becoming uncorrelated — visible as patches of similar grade about that size. The total sill of 38000 corresponds to a field standard deviation of about 195 around the mean of 225.

The pedagogical loop: each realization gives you a fresh "synthetic dataset" of 16 sample values. You could feed those 16 values into kriging, recover an estimated variogram, compute block estimates, and compare against the truth (which you have, because you generated the field). This is exactly how geostatistical algorithms are validated in practice — generate ground truth from a known model, run your method on samples drawn from it, see how well it recovers the truth. Conditional simulation is the same idea but with the 16 values held fixed: every realization passes through the data points exactly while still respecting the variogram, giving a posterior ensemble of plausible deposits.
