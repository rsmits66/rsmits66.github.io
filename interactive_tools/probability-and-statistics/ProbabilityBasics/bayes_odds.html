Bayes Theorem
the Odds Way

The Recipe

① Write down prior weights — one number per hypothesis.

② Write down likelihoods — for each hypothesis, how likely is the evidence you observed?

③ Multiply element-wise (Hadamard ⊙). That gives posterior odds.

④ Normalize only at the very end if you need a probability.

That's it. No denominator. No Law of Total Probability. The "denominator" P(E) is just the sum of the posterior weights — it falls out automatically.

The Machinery

Think of it as a single-layer network. Each prior weight feeds through its own likelihood gate — no cross-connections — and the product emerges as the posterior odds. The dashed rectangle around the likelihood column marks the "evidence layer" that mediates the update.

Arrows go only horizontally. Each prior is scaled by its own likelihood — no cross-connections.

Binary Case & the Bayes Factor

When there are two hypotheses (H vs. Hᶜ), the ratio of posterior weights is the posterior odds:

O(H | E)  =  [P(E|H) / P(E|Hᶜ)]  ×  O(H)

The ratio P(E|H) / P(E|Hᶜ) is the Bayes factor — how many times the evidence multiplied the odds.

Examples

1 Restaurant Safety Inspections ▼

A health department found that 82% of restaurants in a city district had "pass" ratings and 18% had "warning" ratings. A year later, 30% of the warning-rated restaurants improved to pass, while 15% of the pass-rated restaurants slipped to warning — meaning 85% of pass-rated restaurants still have a pass rating. If you walk into a restaurant that currently has a pass rating, what is the probability it had a warning rating last year?

Setup

Hypotheses: H₁ = was Pass last year, H₂ = was Warning last year.
Evidence E = currently has a Pass rating.
Prior weights: [.82, .18]
Likelihoods of "currently Pass": if it was Pass, it stays Pass with probability .85 (since 15% slipped, 85% still pass). If it was Warning, it improved to Pass with probability .30. So [.85, .30]

Normalize

sum = 0.697 + 0.054 = 0.751

P(was Warning | currently Pass) = 0.054 / 0.751 ≈ 7.2%

2 Park Fire — Lightning as Cause ▼

In a national park, 20% of fires occur in the North zone, 50% in the Central zone, and 30% in the South zone. If a fire occurs in the North, there is a 40% chance lightning was the cause: P(L|N) = .4. Similarly P(L|C) = .7 and P(L|S) = .2. A fire is reported and determined to have been caused by lightning. What are the updated odds for each zone?

Setup

Prior weights: [.20, .50, .30]
Likelihoods of lightning: [.40, .70, .20]

Normalize

sum = 0.08 + 0.35 + 0.06 = 0.49

P(North | lightning) ≈ 16.3%   P(Central | lightning) ≈ 71.4%   P(South | lightning) ≈ 12.2%

Lightning as the cause shifted the probability mass toward Central, which had the highest lightning-likelihood.

3 Factory Defect Tracing ▼

Factories 1 through 5 have defective ratios of .1, .2, .3, .4, and .5 respectively. A company buys 10% of its product from Factory 1, 15% from Factory 2, 25% from Factory 3, 30% from Factory 4, and 20% from Factory 5. If a product is found defective, what are the updated chances it was purchased from factory i?

Setup

Prior weights (purchase shares): [.10, .15, .25, .30, .20]
Likelihoods (defect rates): [.10, .20, .30, .40, .50]

Normalize

sum = 0.010 + 0.030 + 0.075 + 0.120 + 0.100 = 0.335

F1 ≈ 3.0%   F2 ≈ 9.0%   F3 ≈ 22.4%   F4 ≈ 35.8%   F5 ≈ 29.9%

Factory 4 is the most likely source (≈ 36%), followed by Factory 5 (≈ 30%). Note that the overall probability of drawing a defective item (0.335) fell out as the sum of the weight vector.

4 Airport Security Screening ▼

An airport X-ray scanner correctly flags a prohibited item 96% of the time (sensitivity). It also incorrectly flags 4% of harmless bags (false-positive rate). Suppose 0.3% of bags actually contain a prohibited item. A bag is flagged — what is the chance it contains a prohibited item?

Setup

Prior weights: [.003, .997]  (prohibited vs. harmless)
Likelihoods of being flagged: [.96, .04]

Normalize

P(prohibited | flagged) = 0.00288 / 0.04276 ≈ 6.7%

The Bayes factor is 0.96 / 0.04 = 24. The evidence is 24× more likely if the bag is truly dangerous. But the prior odds were so low (0.003 : 0.997 ≈ 1 : 332) that even a 24-fold boost only brings us to about 1 : 14, or 6.7%.
