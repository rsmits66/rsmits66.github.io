§ 0  Prelude

Three kinds of weight.

Probability is the art of placing a unit of weight on the real line. The strange thing is how many qualitatively different ways the weight can sit there.

A coin pressed flat against a table puts all of its mass at one point. A pool of water spread across the kitchen floor puts none of its mass at any individual point — yet every region of finite size carries some. Between these two regimes lives a stranger object: a distribution that puts no mass at any point, no mass on any interval that misses a particular fractal dust, and yet still adds up to one.

The probability density function describes only the second case. The probability mass function describes only the first. The third — the Cantor distribution and its kin — has neither, and is invisible to both.

The cumulative distribution function $F(x) = P(X \le x)$ describes all three with a single definition. It is the only honest answer to the question how much weight lies to the left of $x$ — and that question, it turns out, is the only question we ever needed to ask.

Discrete distributions place finite mass on points of dimension zero. Continuous distributions place vanishing mass on a continuum of dimension one. Singular distributions place positive mass on a set of dimension somewhere strictly between. The thesis of this monograph
§ I  The Discrete Case

Atoms on a dust of points.

The simplest distributions live on a set of isolated points and assign each point a finite share of the total mass.

Toss a fair coin four times and count heads. The result is one of five integers, with chances $\tfrac{1}{16},\ \tfrac{4}{16},\ \tfrac{6}{16},\ \tfrac{4}{16},\ \tfrac{1}{16}$ — the binomial$(4, \tfrac12)$ distribution. Five lonely points carry the entire unit of probability between them. Everywhere else, there is nothing.

The Lebesgue measure of the support is zero — five isolated points form a set of total length zero — yet the probability measure is one. This is what we mean by finite mass on a zero-dimensional set: pointlike supports that nevertheless carry weight.

Watch the cumulative function. It is flat — utterly flat — between atoms, because no probability is being collected. At each integer it leaps upward by exactly the amount of mass parked there. The function is right-continuous by convention, the limit from the right matching the value at the jump.

A staircase. Pure jumps, no slope.

§ II  The Absolutely Continuous Case, First Encounter

A density poured on the line.

Here, no individual point carries weight. The mass is infinitesimally thin — a density — yet integrates to one over its support.

The exponential$(1)$ distribution has density $f(x) = e^{-x}$ on $[0, \infty)$. The probability of exactly hitting any specific value is zero: $P(X = 3) = 0$, and so on for every other point. The individual atoms are gone.

What survives is the integral. Probability accumulates over intervals, not at points. The CDF is the running area under the density — smooth, monotone, climbing without jumps.

Notice the structural contrast with the binomial. The discrete CDF was a step. The exponential CDF is a slope. Each value of the density $f(x)$ is the rate at which the CDF rises through $x$. We have traded jumps for derivatives.

§ III  The Absolutely Continuous Case, Continued

The bell, and its integral.

The standard normal is the same kind of object as the exponential — mass spread thinly across a one-dimensional set — but it has earned a printed table.

The CDF of the standard normal has no closed form in elementary functions. For a century, it lived as a printed lookup table at the back of statistics textbooks, columns of $\Phi(z)$ to four decimals. What that table tabulated is precisely $F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2/2}\, dt$ — area under the bell curve.

Two distributions, different shapes — one skewed and bounded below, the other symmetric and unbounded — but the same kind of object. Both have densities. Both CDFs are smooth and strictly increasing on their supports. Both belong to the absolutely continuous family.

§ IV  The Singular Case

Mass on a fractal.

Now for an object that is neither discrete nor continuous — and which most undergraduate curricula politely never mention.

Start with $[0, 1]$. Remove the open middle third. From each remaining piece, remove its open middle third. Iterate forever. What is left is the Cantor set — uncountably many points, Lebesgue measure zero, Hausdorff dimension $\log 2 / \log 3 \approx 0.6309$. A dust between dimensions.

Place a probability distribution on this dust by assigning each of the two pieces at every level half of the remaining mass. The resulting CDF is the Cantor function, sometimes called the devil's staircase: continuous everywhere, with derivative zero almost everywhere — yet rising from $0$ to $1$. It increases without ever being seen to do so.

The bottom panel is the Cantor CDF that the bars integrate to — climbing through values it never lingers at.

In the limit, neither a PMF nor a PDF survives. There is no PMF: no individual point carries positive probability, because each point belongs to nested intervals whose mass halves at every level. There is no PDF either: away from the dust the density would have to be zero, but the dust itself has length zero, so an integral against any density would yield zero — not one. The density bars above are an approximation; the limiting object has neither.

The reckoning. Sets that are too small to register with the Lebesgue measure can still carry an entire distribution. The PDF concept fails here not because of a calculation difficulty, but because no Radon–Nikodym derivative against Lebesgue measure exists.
§ V  The Unification

Three CDFs, one definition.

The PMF and PDF are species. The CDF is the genus.

Every distribution we have considered — the binomial staircase, the smooth exponential and normal slopes, the devil's staircase of the Cantor set — yields, without modification, the same object: a function $F : \mathbb{R} \to [0,1]$ that is non-decreasing, right-continuous, and limits to $0$ and $1$ at the two ends of the line. Any function with these three properties is the CDF of some random variable, and every random variable has one.

The discrete CDF rises only by jumps. The normal CDF rises only by slope. The Cantor CDF rises by neither — it has no jumps and its derivative is zero almost everywhere — and yet it rises, all the way from zero to one.

The decomposition theorem

Lebesgue's decomposition theorem says that every CDF can be written, uniquely, as a sum of these three pure types:

$$F(x) \;=\; \alpha_d\, F_d(x) \;+\; \alpha_{ac}\, F_{ac}(x) \;+\; \alpha_s\, F_s(x), \qquad \alpha_d + \alpha_{ac} + \alpha_s = 1$$ discrete · absolutely continuous · singular

Three components, three flavors of weight. The discrete part lives on a countable set. The absolutely continuous part lives on a set of positive Lebesgue measure and admits a density. The singular continuous part lives, as Cantor's example warns, on a fractal between the two.

The user's intuition turns out to be exactly right. The discrete case is finite mass on a zero-dimensional support; the absolutely continuous case is mass with zero pointwise weight on a one-dimensional support; the singular case is mass on a support of fractional dimension, neither atomic nor smeared. The CDF does not care which kind it is encoding.

§ VI  Expectation

One integral for all three.

The pedagogical price of treating the discrete and continuous cases separately is that we end up writing the same theorem twice. The Stieltjes integral lets us write it once.

Asked for the expected value of $g(X)$, the standard textbook answer forks. If $X$ is discrete, it is a sum:

$$E[g(X)] \;=\; \sum_i g(x_i)\, p_i.$$

If $X$ has a density, it is an integral:

$$E[g(X)] \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x)\, f(x)\, dx.$$

And if $X$ is Cantor — the textbook usually shrugs.

But all three formulas are special cases of a single object: the Riemann–Stieltjes integral of $g$ against the cumulative distribution function itself.

$$E[g(X)] \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x)\, dF(x).$$ the Stieltjes form — one definition for every distribution

The trick is the differential $dF(x)$. Where $F$ jumps, $dF$ is an atom of size equal to the jump, and the integral collapses to a sum. Where $F$ is differentiable, $dF = f(x)\, dx$, and the integral is the familiar one. Where $F$ is the Cantor function, $dF$ is the singular Cantor measure, and the integral remains perfectly well-defined — even though no sum and no Riemann integral would suffice.

The CDF is not just a presentation of the distribution. It is the distribution, in the precise sense that integration against $dF$ computes every probability and every expectation we will ever need. It is the universal reference frame in which discrete, continuous, and singular all become the same kind of statement.

The PMF and PDF are descriptions of distributions in their dialects. The CDF is the distribution speaking in its own voice. The unification, restated

An even purer form  —  expectation as area

The Stieltjes integral is honest, but it still asks the reader to know what $dF$ means. There is a more elementary version, available whenever $X \geq 0$, that uses nothing but the CDF as an ordinary function:

$$E[X] \;=\; \int_0^\infty P(X > t)\, dt \;=\; \int_0^\infty \bigl(1 - F(t)\bigr)\, dt.$$ the tail integral — survives any kind of $F$

A short Fubini argument explains it: $\int_0^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\{X > t\}}\, dt = X$, so taking expectations and swapping order yields the formula. But more striking than the proof is the picture. The integrand $1 - F(t)$ is precisely the height of the gap between the CDF curve and the line $y = 1$. The integral is therefore the area of that gap. Expectation, drawn directly on the CDF plot.

For real-valued $X$ that may also be negative, the formula generalizes symmetrically:

$$E[X] \;=\; \int_0^\infty \bigl(1 - F(t)\bigr)\, dt \;-\; \int_{-\infty}^0 F(t)\, dt.$$

The expectation is a signed area in the CDF plot: the region above $F$ on the positive side, minus the region below $F$ on the negative side. For the standard normal these two regions are mirror images of one another, and the difference is zero — a fact one can read off the picture without computing a single Gaussian integral.

The shaded regions above are the integrals $\int_0^\infty (1 - F(t))\, dt$ for the binomial, exponential, and Cantor distributions — and their areas are, exactly, $2$, $1$, and $\tfrac{1}{2}$. The Cantor expectation in particular requires no density argument and no symmetry trick: the area between the devil's staircase and $y = 1$, on $[0, 1]$, simply is $\tfrac{1}{2}$.

The four expectations, computed uniformly

With the tail integral in hand, every expectation in this monograph becomes the same calculation: integrate $1 - F$.

  • Binomial$(4, \tfrac12)$: $\quad E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{3} P(X > k) = \tfrac{15}{16} + \tfrac{11}{16} + \tfrac{5}{16} + \tfrac{1}{16} = 2.$
  • Exponential$(1)$: $\quad E[X] = \int_0^\infty e^{-t}\, dt = 1.$
  • Standard normal: $\quad E[X] = \int_0^\infty \bigl(1 - \Phi(t)\bigr)\, dt - \int_{-\infty}^0 \Phi(t)\, dt = 0$ —  the two areas cancel by symmetry.
  • Cantor: $\quad E[X] = \int_0^1 \bigl(1 - F(t)\bigr)\, dt = \tfrac{1}{2}$ —  the area above the devil's staircase, no density required.

One formula, four answers. The tail integral does the work that would otherwise have required a sum, a Riemann integral, a symmetric Gaussian argument, and a measure-theoretic appeal to symmetry on a fractal — and it does it by reading the area off a single curve.

This is what the CDF really is: the distribution itself, regarded as a function of one variable, geometric enough that even its first moment can be measured with a ruler.

§ VII  Coda

What the CDF is.

The cumulative distribution function is the only description of a real- valued random variable that survives every refinement of the underlying measure theory. It exists when no PMF exists, when no PDF exists, and when both fail simultaneously. It makes the probability of every event $\{X \le x\}$ explicit and the probability of every other event derivable. It carries within itself, by Lebesgue's theorem, the precise mixture of three pure ingredients that defines its character.

To learn probability through PMFs and PDFs is to learn it through two special cases. To learn it through the CDF — and through expectation defined as a Stieltjes integral against $dF$ — is to learn the thing itself, with the special cases falling out as theorems rather than definitions.
