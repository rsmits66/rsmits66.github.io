Notes on Probability No. 1 — The Multinomial Distribution 2026

Four phenotypes, one hundred draws.

A small case study in the multinomial distribution, using the blood-type proportions of Alabama as a working example — with simulations, a probability calculation, and the variance-covariance structure that ties the four counts together.

A worked example · Sample size n = 100 · Categories k = 4

I.The setting

The proportion of people in Alabama with blood phenotypes A, B, AB, and O are reported as $0.36$, $0.14$, $0.03$, and $0.47$, respectively. We will treat these as the population probabilities and ask what happens when we sample $n=100$ Alabamians at random and tally how many fall into each category.

Let $Y_A, Y_B, Y_{AB}, Y_O$ be those four counts. Together they form a single random vector $\mathbf{Y} = (Y_A, Y_B, Y_{AB}, Y_O)$ following a multinomial distribution with parameters $n = 100$ and $\mathbf{p} = (0.36, 0.14, 0.03, 0.47)$.

III.An exact probability

Suppose we take a random sample of size $100$. What is the chance that exactly $44$ are of type A, $9$ of type B, $6$ of type AB, and $41$ of type O? The multinomial probability mass function answers this directly.

$$P(Y_A=y_A,\, Y_B=y_B,\, Y_{AB}=y_{AB},\, Y_O=y_O) \;=\; \frac{n!}{y_A!\, y_B!\, y_{AB}!\, y_O!}\; p_A^{y_A}\, p_B^{y_B}\, p_{AB}^{y_{AB}}\, p_O^{y_O}$$

Plugging in $n=100$ and the proposed counts $(44, 9, 6, 41)$:

$$P \;=\; \frac{100!}{44!\,9!\,6!\,41!}\;(0.36)^{44}(0.14)^{9}(0.03)^{6}(0.47)^{41}$$

This is most easily evaluated in software. In R the call is a single line:

dmultinom(x = c(44, 9, 6, 41),
          prob = c(0.36, 0.14, 0.03, 0.47))
#> [1] 6.532538e-05
Answer
P ≈ 6.5325 × 10⁻⁵ ≈ 0.0000653

A useful sanity check: $44$ is eight above the mean of $36$, while $41$ is six below the mean of $47$ — both perfectly plausible single-sample outcomes — yet the joint event is rare because we are insisting on all four counts simultaneously. Most of the mass of the multinomial sits on a tight neighborhood of the mean vector $(36, 14, 3, 47)$, and any single integer 4-tuple within that neighborhood already has a small probability.

IV.Variances and covariances

Each individual count $Y_i$ has a familiar marginal distribution. If we lump every observation that isn't type $i$ into a single "other" bin, the count $Y_i$ becomes a binomial $\mathrm{Bin}(n, p_i)$. Therefore

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_i] = n p_i, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = n p_i (1 - p_i).$$

The covariances are where the multinomial parts company with a collection of independent binomials. The four counts must add to $n$, which forces them to compete for observations: a higher count in one category mechanically pulls another down. The result is a strictly negative covariance for every pair,

$$\mathrm{Cov}(Y_i, Y_j) \;=\; -\,n\, p_i\, p_j \qquad (i \neq j).$$

For $n = 100$ and the Alabama proportions, here are the numbers. The diagonal carries the variances $n p_i (1-p_i)$; the off-diagonal entries are the covariances $-n p_i p_j$.

Where the formula comes from

One quick derivation. Consider the indicator variable $X_{k,i}$ which equals $1$ if the $k$-th person sampled is of type $i$ and $0$ otherwise. Then $Y_i = \sum_{k=1}^n X_{k,i}$, and across people the indicators are independent. For a single person and a pair $i \neq j$, the indicators $X_{k,i}$ and $X_{k,j}$ cannot both equal $1$, so $\mathbb{E}[X_{k,i} X_{k,j}] = 0$ and

$$\mathrm{Cov}(X_{k,i}, X_{k,j}) \;=\; 0 - p_i p_j \;=\; -p_i p_j.$$

Summing over the $n$ independent people gives $\mathrm{Cov}(Y_i, Y_j) = -n p_i p_j$, exactly the formula above.

Verifying it from a simulation

The matrix can be checked empirically by drawing many samples and computing the sample covariance of the count vectors:

# 100,000 multinomial samples; rows = phenotypes, cols = samples
sim <- rmultinom(n = 100000, size = 100, prob = p)

# Empirical variance-covariance matrix; transpose so rows are samples
round(cov(t(sim)), 2)
#         A      B     AB      O
#  A   23.05  -5.06  -1.07 -16.92
#  B   -5.06  12.06  -0.42  -6.58
#  AB  -1.07  -0.42   2.91  -1.42

V.Conditional distributions

Once we know one of the counts, the others rearrange themselves. If we are told that $Y_A = y_A$, then the remaining $n - y_A$ people partition themselves into types B, AB, and O with renormalized probabilities $p_i / (1 - p_A)$ for $i \in \{B, AB, O\}$. The conditional distribution is itself a multinomial, on one fewer category and with a smaller sample size:

$$\bigl(Y_B,\, Y_{AB},\, Y_O\bigr) \,\bigm|\, Y_A = y_A \;\sim\; \mathrm{Mult}\!\left(n - y_A,\;\; \frac{(p_B,\, p_{AB},\, p_O)}{1 - p_A}\right).$$

Marginalizing further, any single conditional count is just a binomial: pull one category out, lump the others together, and you have a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials with the new conditional probability. For our pair $(Y_A, Y_O)$,

$$Y_O \,\bigm|\, Y_A = y_A \;\sim\; \mathrm{Bin}\!\left(n - y_A,\;\; \frac{p_O}{1 - p_A}\right).$$

With $p_A = 0.36$ and $p_O = 0.47$, the conditional success probability is $0.47 / 0.64 \approx 0.734$. The conditional mean is therefore $(100 - y_A) \times 0.734$, which slides downward as $y_A$ climbs — the visible signature of the negative dependence.

Conditional PMF $P(Y_O = y \mid Y_A = y_A)$, evaluated exactly from the binomial formula.

# Conditional distribution of Y_O given Y_A = y_A
y_A <- 36
n   <- 100
p_A <- 0.36; p_O <- 0.47

# Y_O | Y_A = y_A  ~  Binomial(n - y_A, p_O / (1 - p_A))
n_cond <- n - y_A
p_cond <- p_O / (1 - p_A)

y <- 0:n_cond
pmf <- dbinom(y, size = n_cond, prob = p_cond)
plot(y, pmf, type = "h", lwd = 3, col = "#2d6363",
     main = paste("Y_O | Y_A =", y_A),
     xlab = "y", ylab = "P(Y_O = y | Y_A)")
abline(v = n_cond * p_cond, lty = 2)   # conditional mean

Notice what happens at the extremes. With $y_A = 25$ — fewer A's than expected — the remaining 75 people lean toward O, pushing $\mathbb{E}[Y_O \mid Y_A = 25] \approx 55$. With $y_A = 50$ — many more A's than expected — the conditional mean falls to $\approx 36.7$. The unconditional mean of $47$ sits, as it must, in between.

VI.The multivariate normal approximation

The multinomial is a sum of $n$ independent identically distributed indicator vectors, so the multivariate central limit theorem applies. As $n \to \infty$,

$$\sqrt{n}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Y}}{n} - \mathbf{p}\right) \;\xrightarrow{\;d\;}\; \mathcal{N}_k\!\left(\mathbf{0},\, \Sigma\right), \qquad \Sigma_{ii} = p_i(1-p_i), \quad \Sigma_{ij} = -p_i p_j.$$

Equivalently, for the count vector itself, $\mathbf{Y} \,\dot{\sim}\, \mathcal{N}_k(n\mathbf{p},\, n\Sigma)$, where $\dot{\sim}$ stands for "approximately distributed as." With $n = 100$ and our $\mathbf{p}$, the approximation is already very serviceable.

A singular covariance

One subtlety: the covariance matrix $n\Sigma$ is singular. Because the four counts always sum to $n$, the random vector lives on a three-dimensional hyperplane inside $\mathbb{R}^4$, and no four-dimensional density exists. The clean fix is to drop one component — say $Y_O$, recoverable as $n - Y_A - Y_B - Y_{AB}$ — and work with the remaining three, whose covariance matrix is non-singular and given by the same formulas with the dropped row and column removed.

$$\begin{pmatrix} Y_A \\ Y_B \\ Y_{AB} \end{pmatrix} \;\dot{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}_3\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} 36 \\ 14 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \;\; \begin{pmatrix} 23.04 & -5.04 & -1.08 \\ -5.04 & 12.04 & -0.42 \\ -1.08 & -0.42 & 2.91 \end{pmatrix} \right)$$

Seeing it in two dimensions

The clearest visualization is a scatter of $(Y_A, Y_O)$ from many simulated samples, with the $95\%$ ellipse from the bivariate normal approximation drawn on top. The cigar shape is the negative correlation $\rho \approx -0.71$ made geometric.

VII.Pearson's chi-square goodness-of-fit

The natural test statistic for the multinomial is Karl Pearson's $X^2$, a weighted sum of squared deviations from the expected counts:

$$X^2 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{(Y_i - n p_i)^2}{n p_i}.$$

Under the null hypothesis that the data really came from $\mathrm{Mult}(n, \mathbf{p})$, and as $n$ grows large,

$$X^2 \;\xrightarrow{\;d\;}\; \chi^2_{\,k-1}.$$

The degrees of freedom are $k - 1$, not $k$, because the $k$ counts are constrained to sum to $n$ — exactly the singular dimension we set aside in the previous section. In fact, $X^2$ is the squared length of the standardized residual vector after that constraint is imposed: it is the quadratic form $(\mathbf{Y} - n\mathbf{p})^\top D^{-1} (\mathbf{Y} - n\mathbf{p})$ with $D = \mathrm{diag}(np_1, \dots, np_k)$, and a short calculation shows it equals $(\widetilde{\mathbf{Y}} - n\widetilde{\mathbf{p}})^\top (n\widetilde{\Sigma})^{-1} (\widetilde{\mathbf{Y}} - n\widetilde{\mathbf{p}})$ on the dropped-component vector — a Mahalanobis distance, which under multivariate normality is exactly $\chi^2_{k-1}$.

Consider two hypothetical samples of size $100$, both to be tested against the Alabama proportions $\mathbf{p} = (0.36, 0.14, 0.03, 0.47)$. The expected vector is $n\mathbf{p} = (36, 14, 3, 47)$.

A practical caveat. The $\chi^2_3$ approximation rests on the multivariate central limit theorem, which in turn requires that every expected count $n p_i$ be large enough — a common rule of thumb asks for $n p_i \geq 5$ for each cell. Here $n p_{AB} = 3$ skirts that line, which is why simulation-based $p$-values (or an exact multinomial test) are sometimes preferred when one of the categories is rare.

VIII.What we have

From the four phenotype proportions of Alabama we drew a complete picture. The exact multinomial PMF gives any joint count probability we ask for, including the $6.5 \times 10^{-5}$ chance of seeing $(44, 9, 6, 41)$. The marginals are binomial, the pairwise covariances are uniformly negative because the counts compete for $n$ trials, and the correlation between the two big categories A and O is the strongest at $-0.71$. Conditioning on one count tightens the others into a smaller multinomial, which is itself a multinomial. As $n$ grows the whole vector spreads out as a (singular) multivariate normal, and the squared standardized residual collapses to a $\chi^2_{k-1}$ distribution that is the workhorse of categorical hypothesis testing. The same four numbers, viewed through these four lenses, give the multinomial in full.
