§ IThe general constrained problem

Real optimization problems rarely come with equality constraints alone. We must also obey inequalities — budgets, capacities, physical limits, non-negativity. The general smooth problem reads

$$\begin{aligned}\min_{\mathbf{x}}\quad & f(\mathbf{x}),\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0},\\ & \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}.\end{aligned}$$

The feasible set is the intersection of the equality surface $\{\mathbf{x}: \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}\}$ with the sub-level region $\{\mathbf{x}: \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}\}$. Inequalities introduce a new behaviour absent from the equality case: at the optimum, an inequality $h_j(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0$ may be binding — satisfied with equality and acting locally like a wall — or it may be slack, contributing nothing to the local picture. Which inequalities bind at $\mathbf{x}^*$ is itself part of the answer.

This combinatorial element is what makes inequality-constrained optimization different. The first-order optimality conditions accordingly carry more structure than the bare gradient-alignment of Lagrange multipliers: they are the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions, which we develop in stages.

§ IIActive and inactive constraints

At any feasible point $\mathbf{x}$, partition the inequalities into two classes:

  • Active (or binding): $h_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0$. The point sits exactly on the boundary of this constraint.
  • Inactive (or slack): $h_j(\mathbf{x}) < 0$. There is room to spare; small perturbations cannot violate this constraint.

The set of active inequalities at $\mathbf{x}$ is denoted

$$\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}) = \{\, j : h_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \,\}.$$

At an optimum $\mathbf{x}^*$, the active inequalities $\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}^*)$ behave exactly like equality constraints: they pin $\mathbf{x}^*$ to a lower-dimensional surface, and they participate in the gradient-alignment conditions. The inactive ones do not enter the local first-order picture at all — we could delete them from the problem without changing $\mathbf{x}^*$.

§ IIIThe Lagrangian

To absorb both kinds of constraint into a single objective, introduce one multiplier per scalar constraint. The Lagrangian is

$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\lambda}^\top \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\mu}^\top \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}),$$

with $\boldsymbol{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^p$ for the equalities and $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^q$ for the inequalities. Crucially, the $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ multipliers carry a sign restriction: $\boldsymbol{\mu} \geq \mathbf{0}$ component-wise.

The reason for the sign is physical. An inequality $h_j(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0$ defines a wall; if the optimum sits against it, the wall pushes back. Push and counter-push must be opposed, hence the multiplier — the magnitude of the constraint's resistance — is non-negative. A negative $\mu_j$ would correspond to a wall that pulls you outward, which is geometrically impossible for a one-sided constraint.

Equality multipliers carry no such restriction; an equality is a wall on both sides and can push either way.

§ IVThe KKT conditions

The first-order optimality conditions for the inequality-constrained problem — the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions — require four statements to hold simultaneously at $(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*, \boldsymbol{\mu}^*)$:

The Four KKT Conditions
  1. Stationarity $\displaystyle \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*, \boldsymbol{\mu}^*) = \mathbf{0}$, equivalently $\;\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_i \lambda_i^* \nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_j \mu_j^* \nabla h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}.$
  2. Primal feasibility $\;\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$ and $\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq \mathbf{0}$. The optimum lies in the feasible set.
  3. Dual feasibility $\;\mu_j^* \geq 0$ for every $j$. The inequality multipliers respect their sign restriction.
  4. Complementary slackness $\;\mu_j^* \, h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$ for every $j$. For each inequality, either the constraint is active or its multiplier is zero (or both).

Stationarity says that $-\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ is a particular linear combination of constraint gradients: a free combination of equality gradients, plus a non-negative combination of inequality gradients. Together with complementary slackness, only the active inequalities contribute — inactive ones have $\mu_j^* = 0$ and drop out of the sum entirely.

Sufficiency

For convex problems — convex $f$, convex inequalities $h_j$, affine equalities $\mathbf{g}$ — the KKT conditions are sufficient: any KKT point is a global minimizer. For general (non-convex) problems they are necessary at any local minimum where a constraint qualification holds.

§ VThe geometry of complementary slackness

Complementary slackness $\mu_j^* h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$ encodes a switch:

  • If $h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) < 0$ (inactive), then $\mu_j^* = 0$. The constraint exerts no force.
  • If $\mu_j^* > 0$, then $h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$. The constraint binds.
  • The degenerate case $\mu_j^* = 0$ and $h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$ is permitted — the constraint is active in name only.

Geometrically, an inactive constraint sits at a strict distance from $\mathbf{x}^*$. We could perturb $\mathbf{x}^*$ slightly in any direction and not violate it. So this constraint cannot be holding the optimum in place; it cannot be exerting any force; its multiplier must be zero. The remaining (active) constraints alone determine the local KKT system.

This is what allows the active-set view of the problem: at the solution, the inequality-constrained problem is equivalent to an equality-constrained problem on $\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}^*)$, which we already know how to solve. The combinatorial difficulty of inequality-constrained optimization is, in essence, identifying $\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}^*)$.

§ VIFeasible directions

A geometrically transparent way to see the KKT conditions is via the cone of feasible directions. From a feasible point $\mathbf{x}$, a direction $\mathbf{d}$ is feasible if a small step $\mathbf{x} + \alpha \mathbf{d}$ remains feasible for some $\alpha > 0$. To first order, this requires

$$\nabla g_i(\mathbf{x})^\top \mathbf{d} = 0 \quad \forall i, \qquad \nabla h_j(\mathbf{x})^\top \mathbf{d} \leq 0 \quad \forall j \in \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}).$$

Equality constraints are bilateral: motion is constrained to their tangent space. Active inequalities are unilateral: motion is allowed only into the half-space pointing away from $\nabla h_j$. Inactive inequalities impose no constraint on $\mathbf{d}$ — perturbations small enough to stay clear of their boundary do not violate them.

For $\mathbf{x}^*$ to be optimal, no feasible direction can decrease $f$:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)^\top \mathbf{d} \geq 0 \qquad \text{for every feasible } \mathbf{d}.$$

By the Farkas lemma — a duality between linear systems — this is equivalent to $-\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ lying in the cone generated by the active constraint gradients with non-negative coefficients on the inequalities. That is exactly the KKT stationarity statement.

§ VIIActive-set methods

For quadratic programs — objective $f(\mathbf{x}) = \tfrac{1}{2} \mathbf{x}^\top Q \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c}^\top \mathbf{x}$ with linear constraints — the KKT system is itself linear once the active set $\mathcal{A}^*$ is fixed. The combinatorial work, then, is finding $\mathcal{A}^*$. Active-set methods do this by trial and revision: maintain a working set of inequalities, treat them as equalities, solve, and revise.

The algorithm sketch

At iteration $k$ we have an estimate $\mathcal{W}^k$ of the active set and a feasible iterate $\mathbf{x}^k$. The step is:

  1. Solve the equality-constrained subproblem. Minimize $f(\mathbf{x})$ subject to $h_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ for all $j \in \mathcal{W}^k$ and $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$. Call the resulting search direction $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{sub}} - \mathbf{x}^k$.
  2. If $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{0}$. The current iterate is optimal for the subproblem. Examine the multipliers $\mu_j$ of $j \in \mathcal{W}^k$:
    • If $\mu_j \geq 0$ for every $j \in \mathcal{W}^k$: KKT is satisfied for the original problem. Stop.
    • Otherwise, drop the constraint with the most negative multiplier from $\mathcal{W}^k$ and continue. (A negative multiplier means the constraint is pushing the wrong way — releasing it should let $f$ decrease.)
  3. If $\mathbf{p} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Take a step $\mathbf{x}^{k+1} = \mathbf{x}^k + \alpha_k \mathbf{p}$, where $\alpha_k \in (0, 1]$ is the largest step keeping all non-working inequalities feasible. If $\alpha_k < 1$, the step is blocked by some $j' \notin \mathcal{W}^k$; add $j'$ to $\mathcal{W}^{k+1}$.

Each iteration alters $\mathcal{W}$ by exactly one element — either dropping a misfit or adding a blocker. With non-degeneracy, the algorithm is finite.

Correctness, briefly

The algorithm terminates exactly when the working set equals $\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}^k)$ and all multipliers in $\mathcal{W}$ are non-negative and $\mathbf{x}^k$ minimizes $f$ on the working subspace. Those three together are the KKT conditions for the original inequality-constrained problem.

Example I

A quadratic minimum on a triangle

Solve the problem displayed in Fig. I:

$$\min_{\mathbf{x}}\; \tfrac{1}{2}(x_1 - 1.4)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(x_2 - 1.6)^2 \quad \text{s.t.}\quad x_1 + x_2 \leq 2,\; x_1 \geq 0,\; x_2 \geq 0.$$

Write the inequalities in standard form $\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \mathbf{0}$:

$$h_1 = x_1 + x_2 - 2,\qquad h_2 = -x_1,\qquad h_3 = -x_2.$$
  1. Try the unconstrained minimum. $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$ at $(1.4, 1.6)$. But $h_1(1.4, 1.6) = 1.0 > 0$ — infeasible. So at least one inequality must be active at the solution.
  2. Guess the active set. $h_1$ is most violated at the unconstrained min; try $\mathcal{A} = \{1\}$.
  3. Solve the equality-constrained subproblem. Lagrangian $$\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}(x_1-1.4)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(x_2-1.6)^2 + \mu_1 (x_1 + x_2 - 2).$$ Stationarity: $x_1 - 1.4 + \mu_1 = 0,\ x_2 - 1.6 + \mu_1 = 0$. So $x_1 = 1.4 - \mu_1$, $x_2 = 1.6 - \mu_1$.
    Active constraint: $x_1 + x_2 = 2 \Rightarrow (1.4 - \mu_1) + (1.6 - \mu_1) = 2 \Rightarrow \mu_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
    Therefore $\mathbf{x}^* = (0.9, 1.1)$, $\mu_1^* = 0.5$.
  4. Check all four KKT conditions.
    • Stationarity ✓ (by construction).
    • Primal feasibility: $h_1 = 0$ ✓, $h_2 = -0.9 < 0$ ✓, $h_3 = -1.1 < 0$ ✓.
    • Dual feasibility: $\mu_1^* = 0.5 \geq 0$ ✓ (others trivially zero).
    • Complementary slackness: $\mu_1 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot (-0.9) + 0 \cdot (-1.1) = 0$ ✓.

The candidate satisfies every condition, so $\mathbf{x}^* = (0.9, 1.1)$ with $f(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0.25$ is the global minimum (the problem is convex). Geometrically, the level circle of radius $1/\sqrt{2}$ about $(1.4, 1.6)$ is tangent to the line $x_1 + x_2 = 2$ exactly at $\mathbf{x}^*$.

Example II

A quadratic in a box

Bound-constrained problems are everywhere — and they are pure inequality problems with simple structure:

$$\min_{\mathbf{x}}\; \tfrac{1}{2}(x_1 + 1)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(x_2 - 0.5)^2 \quad \text{s.t.}\quad 0 \leq x_i \leq 1, \; i = 1, 2.$$

Each variable has two box constraints: $h_{i,\text{lo}} = -x_i \leq 0$ and $h_{i,\text{hi}} = x_i - 1 \leq 0$. The active set picks out which of the four bounds are tight.

  1. Unconstrained min: $\mathbf{x}_{\text{u}} = (-1, 0.5)$. The $x_1$ component violates $x_1 \geq 0$; project that bound onto the active set: $\mathcal{A} = \{1,\text{lo}\}$.
  2. Solve the subproblem with $x_1 = 0$. Lagrangian $\mathcal{L} = f + \mu (-x_1)$. Stationarity in $x_2$: $x_2 - 0.5 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2^* = 0.5$. Stationarity in $x_1$: $x_1 + 1 - \mu = 0$ with $x_1 = 0$ gives $\mu = 1$.
  3. Check. $\mathbf{x}^* = (0, 0.5)$ satisfies $0 \leq x_i \leq 1$. The active multiplier $\mu = 1 \geq 0$, all other bound multipliers are zero, and complementary slackness holds. KKT ✓.

So the solution is $\mathbf{x}^* = (0, 0.5)$ with $f(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0.5$. The unconstrained minimizer was projected onto the active face $\{x_1 = 0\}$, picking up a single positive multiplier. The other bounds remain slack and silent.

Example III

An active-set walkthrough

Project the point $\mathbf{c} = (-0.5, -0.5)$ onto the non-negative orthant:

$$\min_{\mathbf{x}}\; \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.}\quad x_1 \geq 0,\; x_2 \geq 0.$$

The unconstrained minimizer $\mathbf{c}$ is infeasible, so the active-set method walks from a feasible starting point along directions blocked, in turn, by each constraint. Take $\mathbf{x}^0 = (1, 0.5)$, which sits in the interior of the feasible orthant, and $\mathcal{W}^0 = \emptyset$:

Iter. $\mathcal{W}^k$ Subproblem & step Action
0 ∅ Unconstrained sub-solution: $\mathbf{c} = (-0.5, -0.5)$. Step from $(1, 0.5)$. Maximum feasible $\alpha = \tfrac{1}{2}$, blocked by $h_2$ ($x_2 \to 0$). Move to $(0.25, 0)$. Add $h_2$.
1 {2} Sub-solution with $x_2 = 0$: $(-0.5, 0)$. Step from $(0.25, 0)$. Maximum feasible $\alpha = \tfrac{1}{3}$, blocked by $h_1$ ($x_1 \to 0$). Move to $(0, 0)$. Add $h_1$.
2 {1, 2} Sub-solution with $x_1 = x_2 = 0$: just $(0, 0)$. Step direction $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{0}$. Multipliers $\mu_1 = \mu_2 = 0.5$, both $\geq 0$. KKT satisfied. Stop.

The KKT optimum is $\mathbf{x}^* = (0, 0)$ with $\mu_1^* = \mu_2^* = 0.5$. Complementary slackness holds because both inequalities are active. Geometrically, projecting a point in the third quadrant onto the first quadrant lands at the origin — the corner where both axis-bounds bind.

Notice how the working set evolved: $\emptyset \to \{2\} \to \{1, 2\}$, growing by one element per iteration whenever the step was blocked. In larger problems the working set may also shrink, when a previously-active multiplier turns negative and signals that the algorithm should release that constraint.

§ XISummary

The KKT conditions extend the geometric intuition of Lagrange multipliers from equalities to inequalities. The four conditions — stationarity, primal feasibility, dual feasibility, complementary slackness — together encode a single statement: at the optimum, $-\nabla f$ lies in the cone of active constraint normals, with non-negative weights on the inequality contributions.

Complementary slackness is the bridge between continuous and combinatorial: it tells us that, although the problem nominally involves all $q$ inequalities, only a subset is "live" at the solution. Identifying that subset is what active-set methods do. Each iteration solves an equality-constrained subproblem — for which the Lagrange-multiplier machinery suffices — and updates the working set by one element. The two ideas, KKT optimality and active-set search, are inseparable.

In summary

Inequality-constrained optimality is a statement about the cone of active constraint normals: $-\nabla f$ must lie inside it, with strictly non-negative coefficients on inequality multipliers. The slack constraints contribute nothing; the active ones contribute everything. Active-set algorithms exploit this by alternating between identifying $\mathcal{A}^*$ and solving the equality-constrained subproblem on it.
