§ 1  ·  The Idea

What is a dual space?

A vector space $V$ holds the things you build with — arrows, polynomials, functions. Its dual space $V^*$ holds the things you measure with: the linear ways of turning a vector into a single number. If $V$ is the space of polynomials of degree $\le 2$, then "evaluate at $x = 3$" is one such measurement. So is "integrate from $0$ to $1$." Both eat a polynomial and return a number, and both respect addition and scalar multiplication. They are elements of $V^*$.

Definition

Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$. A linear functional on $V$ is a linear map $L : V \to \mathbb{R}$. The set of all linear functionals on $V$, with pointwise addition and scalar multiplication, is itself a vector space, called the dual space $V^*$.

The first surprise is that $V^*$ has the same size as $V$.

Theorem  ·  The dimension theorem for duals

If $V$ is finite-dimensional, then $\dim V^* = \dim V$.

Proof. Fix a basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ of $V$. Every $v \in V$ has unique coordinates $v = \sum c_i v_i$. Define $f_i \in V^*$ by

$$f_i(v) = c_i \quad \text{i.e.} \quad f_i(v_j) = \delta_{ij}.$$

Each $f_i$ is linear (coordinates depend linearly on $v$), so $f_i \in V^*$. We claim $\{f_1, \ldots, f_n\}$ is a basis of $V^*$.

Independence. If $\sum a_i f_i = 0$, applying both sides to $v_j$ gives $a_j = 0$, for every $j$.

Spanning. Take any $L \in V^*$. Set $a_i = L(v_i)$. Then for each basis vector $v_j$,

$$\left(\sum_i a_i f_i\right)(v_j) = \sum_i a_i \delta_{ij} = a_j = L(v_j),$$

so $L$ and $\sum a_i f_i$ agree on a basis, hence on all of $V$. Thus $\{f_i\}$ spans $V^*$, and $\dim V^* = n = \dim V$.

The basis $\{f_1, \ldots, f_n\}$ is called the dual basis to $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$.

A basis of $V$ tells you how to build vectors. A dual basis of $V^*$ tells you how to measure them. Many famous "formulas" in mathematics are nothing more than the expansion of one functional in terms of another.
§ 2  ·  First Surprise

Lagrange interpolation,
or: a dual basis you've already used

Let $V = P_n$, polynomials of degree $\le n$. Pick distinct nodes $t_0, \ldots, t_n$ and define the evaluation functionals

$$L_i(p) = p(t_i).$$

One can show (Vandermonde) that $\{L_0, \ldots, L_n\}$ is a basis of $V^*$. What is the basis $\{p_0, \ldots, p_n\}$ of $V$ dual to it? It must satisfy $L_i(p_j) = p_j(t_i) = \delta_{ij}$ — that is, $p_j$ vanishes at every node except $t_j$, where it equals $1$. The unique such polynomial is

$$p_j(x) = \prod_{i \ne j} \frac{x - t_i}{t_j - t_i}.$$

These are the Lagrange basis polynomials. Expanding any $p \in V$ in this dual basis yields the famous formula

$$p(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{n} p(t_j)\, p_j(x),$$

which says: the unique polynomial of degree $\le n$ taking prescribed values $c_0, \ldots, c_n$ at $t_0, \ldots, t_n$ is $\sum c_j p_j(x)$. The dual basis is the interpolation formula.

What if $f$ is not a polynomial?

Given any continuous $f$, sample it at $n+1$ nodes and form the polynomial through those samples:

$$P_f(x) = \sum_j f(t_j)\, p_j(x).$$

$P_f$ agrees with $f$ at the nodes and is the "best polynomial mimic of $f$ at the sample points." For smooth $f$ it is close to $f$ everywhere on the interval — usually. The Runge example below shows that for some $f$, increasing the number of evenly-spaced nodes can make things worse.

§ 3  ·  Quadrature, Part I

The trapezoidal rule

Now let the integration functional $I(p) = \int_a^b p(x)\, dx$ play the role of $L$. Since $\{L_0, \ldots, L_n\}$ is a basis of $V^* = (P_n)^*$, there are unique weights $w_j$ with

$$\int_a^b p(x)\, dx \;=\; \sum_j w_j\, p(t_j) \quad \text{for every } p \in P_n.$$

Plugging the dual basis polynomial $p_k$ into both sides yields the punchline:

The weight formula

$$w_k \;=\; \int_a^b p_k(x)\, dx.$$

Quadrature weights are nothing but integrals of Lagrange basis polynomials.

For $n = 1$ with nodes at the endpoints $a$ and $b$, both basis polynomials integrate to $(b-a)/2$, giving the trapezoidal rule:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx \;\approx\; \frac{b-a}{2}\bigl[f(a) + f(b)\bigr].$$

Applying it on each panel of an even partition of $[a,b]$ produces the composite trapezoidal rule: shared endpoints get weight $1$ counted twice (so $2$), and the two outermost endpoints get weight $1$.

§ 4  ·  Quadrature, Part II

Composite Simpson's rule

For $n = 2$ with nodes $a$, $(a+b)/2$, $b$, integrating the three Lagrange polynomials yields weights $\tfrac{b-a}{6}, \tfrac{2(b-a)}{3}, \tfrac{b-a}{6}$ — the famous $1\!:\!4\!:\!1$ ratio. Simpson's rule is

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \frac{b-a}{6}\!\left[\,f(a) + 4f\!\bigl(\tfrac{a+b}{2}\bigr) + f(b)\,\right].$$

Apply it on each pair of subintervals of an even partition, and the shared inner endpoints add up: two adjacent $1$s become a $2$. The composite weight pattern is the iconic

$$\frac{h}{3}\bigl[\,1,\, 4,\, 2,\, 4,\, 2,\, 4,\, \ldots,\, 4,\, 2,\, 4,\, 1\,\bigr].$$

Geometrically, instead of a flat-topped trapezoid on each panel we get a parabolic arc — the unique parabola through three consecutive samples.

§ 5  ·  One Step Higher

Beyond Simpson: Boole's rule

Nothing stops us from going to $n=4$. With five nodes per panel — $a$, $a+h$, $a+2h$, $a+3h$, $a+4h$ where $h = (b-a)/4$ — integrating the five Lagrange polynomials produces weights $\frac{2h}{45}\,[\,7,\,32,\,12,\,32,\,7\,]$. This is Boole's rule:

$$\int_a^b f\,dx \;\approx\; \frac{2h}{45}\bigl[\,7f_0 + 32f_1 + 12f_2 + 32f_3 + 7f_4\,\bigr].$$

It is exact for polynomials up to degree $5$ (one degree better than the cubic-exactness of Simpson's, due to a similar symmetry windfall). Composite Boole's rule fits a smooth quartic on every group of four subintervals, hugging $f$ tighter than parabolas can.

Side-by-side, same node budget

The fairest comparison fixes the number of function evaluations and asks: which rule wins? With a generous budget the answer is "Boole, by orders of magnitude" — provided $f$ is smooth enough to support the higher-order error term. Try the square-root function, whose derivative blows up at $x=0$, and watch all three methods slow to a crawl.

Absolute error vs. number of subintervals on a log–log scale. For smooth functions the slopes are $-2$ (trapezoid), $-4$ (Simpson), $-6$ (Boole). For non-smooth functions the slopes flatten, since the higher-order error formulas need higher-order derivatives that no longer exist.
§ 6  ·  A Different Dual Basis

Hermite interpolation,
and the road to splines

The Lagrange story used only point evaluations. But the dual basis machinery is indifferent to the type of functional involved — any independent collection that spans $V^*$ will do. Mix in derivative evaluations and you get Hermite interpolation.

Take $V = P_3$ (cubics) and consider the four functionals on two endpoints $a$ and $b$:

$$E_a(p) = p(a), \quad D_a(p) = p'(a), \quad E_b(p) = p(b), \quad D_b(p) = p'(b).$$

One checks they are independent and so form a basis of $V^* = (P_3)^*$. The dual basis is the four Hermite cubics $h_{00}, h_{10}, h_{01}, h_{11}$ on the unit interval, with $t = (x-a)/(b-a):$

$$\begin{aligned} h_{00}(t) &= 2t^3 - 3t^2 + 1, & h_{10}(t) &= t^3 - 2t^2 + t, \\ h_{01}(t) &= -2t^3 + 3t^2, & h_{11}(t) &= t^3 - t^2. \end{aligned}$$

Expanding $f$ in the dual basis gives the Hermite formula

$$P_f(x) \;=\; f(a)\,h_{00}(t) + (b-a)\,f'(a)\,h_{10}(t) + f(b)\,h_{01}(t) + (b-a)\,f'(b)\,h_{11}(t),$$

which matches $f$ and its derivative at both endpoints.

From two points to many: piecewise Hermite

Nothing requires us to use only two endpoints. Pick $m+1$ nodes $x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_m$. On each subinterval $[x_i, x_{i+1}]$ use the Hermite formula above with the four functionals $E_{x_i}, D_{x_i}, E_{x_{i+1}}, D_{x_{i+1}}$. The result is a piecewise cubic on $[x_0, x_m]$ that matches both $f$ and $f'$ at every node.

Because both pieces meeting at an interior node $x_i$ match the same value and the same slope there, the joined curve is automatically continuous and continuously differentiable — the join is invisible to the eye. This is a $C^1$ piecewise-cubic Hermite spline, the workhorse behind smooth animation paths, font outlines, and physics-based path planning.

Number of intervals $m$: 1
Approximating $f(x) = \sin(x) + 0.3\sin(3x)$ on $[0,\pi]$. With $m=1$ this is the classic two-endpoint Hermite cubic. Increase $m$ to add interior nodes — each piece is a cubic matching $f$ and $f'$ at both ends, so the joined curve is $C^1$. Tangent arrows at every node show the shared slope.

One more constraint: cubic splines

Hermite splines need both $f(x_i)$ and $f'(x_i)$ at every node. What if you have only the values $f(x_i)$? Then you have $m+1$ value constraints and $4m$ coefficients to fix (four per cubic piece). Demanding $C^2$ continuity at the $m-1$ interior nodes — matching second derivatives, not just first — gives $3(m-1) + (m+1) = 4m - 2$ conditions, leaving two degrees of freedom usually fixed by boundary conditions (natural, clamped, periodic). The result is the cubic spline.

Cubic splines are the same idea as Hermite, with the missing slope information replaced by a smoothness demand. They are again expansions in a dual basis — the so-called B-spline basis — and they are the right tool when you have data values but no measured slopes.

§ 7  ·  Closing

One idea, many disguises

Lagrange interpolation, Newton–Cotes quadrature (trapezoid, Simpson, Boole, …), Hermite splines, and cubic splines are usually taught as separate "tricks." They are not. Each one is the expansion of some natural functional — "evaluate $f$ at $x$," "integrate $f$ over $[a,b]$," "match $f$ and $f'$ at the endpoints" — in a basis of $V^*$ chosen to make the answer concrete and computable.

Recognising the unifying structure does two things at once: it explains why these formulas work (existence and uniqueness of the dual basis), and it tells you how to invent new ones (pick a basis of $V^*$ aligned with the functional you actually care about). That is the practical payoff of taking $V^*$ seriously.

Existence and uniqueness of the dual basis is a one-line theorem. Almost every classical interpolation and quadrature formula is its corollary.
