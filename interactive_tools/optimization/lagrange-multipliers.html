§ IThe constrained problem

Suppose we wish to minimize a smooth function $f:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, but only over those points $\mathbf{x}$ that satisfy a system of equations $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$. The natural way to write this is

$$\begin{aligned}\min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & f(\mathbf{x}), \\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}.\end{aligned}$$

The constraint $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$ carves out a surface inside $\mathbb{R}^n$ — the feasible set — and we must search for a minimizer along this surface, not throughout space. The unconstrained methods of earlier chapters cannot be applied directly: at a minimizer $\mathbf{x}^*$ on the surface, $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ need not vanish. Something else must hold instead.

The reformulation we shall develop — the method of Lagrange multipliers — converts this lower-dimensional constrained problem into a higher-dimensional unconstrained one, by absorbing the constraints into a modified objective.

§ IIThe geometric necessity

Consider first the simplest case: a single equality constraint $g(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ in two dimensions, $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$. The constraint $g(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ is a curve in the plane, and the level sets $f(\mathbf{x}) = c$ form a family of curves, one for each value $c$.

Walk along the constraint curve. At each point you have a single tangent direction $\mathbf{t}$ — the only direction in which you may move while still satisfying the constraint. By definition,

$$\nabla g(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{t} = 0.$$

If at some point on the curve the gradient $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ has a non-zero component along $\mathbf{t}$, then by stepping along $\mathbf{t}$ (or against it) we can decrease $f$ while remaining feasible. That point cannot be optimal.

The optimum is therefore characterised by the absence of such an improving direction. At a minimizer $\mathbf{x}^*$, the directional derivative of $f$ vanishes in every feasible direction:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) \cdot \mathbf{t} = 0 \quad \text{for every } \mathbf{t} \text{ with } \nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*) \cdot \mathbf{t} = 0.$$

In two dimensions the tangent space is one-dimensional, so its orthogonal complement is also one-dimensional — a single line, spanned by $\nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*)$. Hence $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ must lie on this line:

$$\boxed{\;\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \lambda\, \nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*)\;}$$

The scalar $\lambda$ — the Lagrange multiplier — provides the exact rescaling needed to align the two gradients. Geometrically, the level set of $f$ passing through $\mathbf{x}^*$ must be tangent to the constraint curve at $\mathbf{x}^*$.

Geometric criterion

A point $\mathbf{x}^*$ on the surface $g(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ is a candidate optimizer if and only if the level set of $f$ passing through $\mathbf{x}^*$ is tangent to the surface there. Equivalently, $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ has no component tangent to the constraint — it lies entirely in the normal direction $\nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*)$.

§ IIIThe Lagrangian

The geometric criterion can be neatly packaged. Define the Lagrangian

$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\lambda}^\top \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}),$$

where $\boldsymbol{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ collects one multiplier for each scalar constraint. The constrained problem is then equivalent to the unconstrained saddle-point problem

$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \; \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}).$$

The multipliers act as regularizers that punish any violation of the constraint: should $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$ deviate from zero, the term $\boldsymbol{\lambda}^\top \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$ can be inflated by adjusting $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$, so the inner minimizer over $\mathbf{x}$ is forced back onto the feasible set.

By adding dimensions, we trade a low-dimensional constrained problem for a high-dimensional unconstrained one — for which the toolbox is well established.

§ IVStationarity conditions

At a saddle point $(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*)$ of the Lagrangian, both partial gradients vanish:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*) = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*) = \mathbf{0}.$$

These are the first-order optimality conditions, also called stationarity conditions.

The second condition simply recovers the constraint:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*) = \mathbf{0} \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}.$$

The first condition reproduces the gradient-alignment relation:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \boldsymbol{\lambda}^{*\top} \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0} \;\Longrightarrow\; \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f(\mathbf{x}^*) = -\boldsymbol{\lambda}^{*\top} \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}^*).$$

For a single equality constraint this collapses to $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = -\lambda^* \, \nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*)$, as we derived geometrically. The sign of $\lambda^*$ is a convention — what matters is that the two vectors are parallel.

Recipe

To solve a constrained problem with $m$ equality constraints, write down the Lagrangian and solve the $(n+m)$ stationarity equations $\nabla \mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$ for the $(n+m)$ unknowns $(\mathbf{x}^*, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^*)$. The candidates so produced must then be classified as minima, maxima, or saddle points.

§ VThe lifted picture

It is illuminating to view the same situation in three dimensions. Plot $f(x_1, x_2)$ as a surface above the $(x_1, x_2)$-plane. The constraint $g(x_1, x_2) = 0$ is a curve in the plane; lifting it onto the graph of $f$ produces a one-dimensional curve traced on the surface. The constrained optimum is the lowest point of this lifted curve — not the lowest point of the surface itself.

Rotate the scene below to see the relationship: the bowl is the graph of $f(x_1,x_2) = x_1^2 + x_2^2$, the green curve in the floor plane is the constraint $x_1 + x_2 = 1$, and the red curve on the bowl is its lift $f(x_1, x_2)|_{x_1+x_2=1}$. The marked low point on the red curve is the constrained minimum.

Example I

The closest point on a line

Find the point on the line $x_1 + x_2 = 1$ closest to the origin.

Equivalently, minimize the squared distance,

$$\min_{\mathbf{x}}\; f(\mathbf{x}) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 \qquad \text{s.t.}\qquad g(\mathbf{x}) = x_1 + x_2 - 1 = 0.$$
  1. Form the Lagrangian. $$\mathcal{L}(x_1, x_2, \lambda) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \lambda(x_1 + x_2 - 1).$$
  2. Stationarity in $\mathbf{x}$. $$\partial_{x_1}\mathcal{L} = 2x_1 + \lambda = 0, \qquad \partial_{x_2}\mathcal{L} = 2x_2 + \lambda = 0.$$ Hence $x_1 = x_2 = -\lambda/2$.
  3. Stationarity in $\lambda$. The constraint $x_1 + x_2 = 1$ gives $-\lambda/2 - \lambda/2 = 1$, i.e. $\lambda^* = -1$.
  4. Read off the solution. $$\mathbf{x}^* = \left(\tfrac{1}{2},\, \tfrac{1}{2}\right), \qquad f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \tfrac{1}{2}.$$ The minimum distance is $\|\mathbf{x}^*\| = 1/\sqrt{2}$.

Check the geometry. $\nabla f = (2x_1, 2x_2) = (1, 1)$ at the optimum, while $\nabla g = (1, 1)$. They are parallel, with $\nabla f = -\lambda^* \nabla g = (1,1)$. The level set $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 1/2$ is a circle that is tangent to the line $x_1+x_2=1$ exactly at $(1/2, 1/2)$.

Example II

Maximum of a product on the unit circle

Maximize $f(\mathbf{x}) = x_1 x_2$ subject to $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 1$. Geometrically, what is the largest-area axis-aligned rectangle inscribed in the unit circle (up to a constant)?

  1. Lagrangian. $$\mathcal{L}(x_1, x_2, \lambda) = x_1 x_2 + \lambda(x_1^2 + x_2^2 - 1).$$
  2. Stationarity in $\mathbf{x}$. $$\partial_{x_1}\mathcal{L} = x_2 + 2\lambda x_1 = 0, \qquad \partial_{x_2}\mathcal{L} = x_1 + 2\lambda x_2 = 0.$$ Solving simultaneously: substituting $x_2 = -2\lambda x_1$ into the second equation gives $x_1(1 - 4\lambda^2) = 0$, so either $x_1 = 0$ (which forces $x_2 = 0$, infeasible) or $\lambda = \pm 1/2$.
  3. Stationarity in $\lambda$. The constraint gives $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 1$. Combined with $x_2 = \mp x_1$, this yields $x_1 = \pm 1/\sqrt{2}$.
  4. Four candidates. $$(x_1, x_2) \in \left\{ \pm\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}, \tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}\right),\;\pm\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}, -\tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}\right) \right\}.$$ Evaluating $f = x_1 x_2$: the first pair give $f = +1/2$ (maxima), the second pair give $f = -1/2$ (minima).

The level sets of $x_1 x_2 = c$ are hyperbolas, and the unit circle is tangent to four of them — one for each candidate.

Example III

A linear functional on an ellipse

Maximize $f(\mathbf{x}) = x_1 + 2 x_2$ on the ellipse $\tfrac{x_1^2}{4} + x_2^2 = 1$.

  1. Lagrangian. $$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = x_1 + 2 x_2 + \lambda\!\left(\tfrac{x_1^2}{4} + x_2^2 - 1\right).$$
  2. Stationarity in $\mathbf{x}$. $$1 + \tfrac{\lambda}{2} x_1 = 0, \qquad 2 + 2 \lambda x_2 = 0,$$ giving $x_1 = -2/\lambda$ and $x_2 = -1/\lambda$.
  3. Constraint. Substituting into the ellipse equation, $$\frac{(-2/\lambda)^2}{4} + (-1/\lambda)^2 = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} + \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} = 1 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \lambda = \pm\sqrt{2}.$$
  4. Two candidates. $$\lambda = -\sqrt{2}:\;\; \mathbf{x}^* = (\sqrt 2,\, 1/\sqrt 2),\quad f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{2} = 2\sqrt{2} \;\;(\text{max}),$$ $$\lambda = +\sqrt{2}:\;\; \mathbf{x}^* = (-\sqrt 2,\, -1/\sqrt 2),\quad f(\mathbf{x}^*) = -2\sqrt{2}\;\;(\text{min}).$$

Each level set $x_1 + 2x_2 = c$ is a line of slope $-1/2$. We translate this line until it just touches the ellipse — the points of tangency are the optima.

Example IV

A saddle on the unit circle

Find the extrema of $f(\mathbf{x}) = x_1^2 - x_2^2$ on the unit circle $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 1$. The objective has a saddle at the origin — on the circle, it must oscillate.

  1. Lagrangian. $$\mathcal{L} = x_1^2 - x_2^2 + \lambda(x_1^2 + x_2^2 - 1).$$
  2. Stationarity. $$2x_1(1 + \lambda) = 0, \qquad -2 x_2(1 - \lambda) = 0.$$ The cases split:
    • $x_1 = 0$ and $\lambda = 1$: then $x_2 = \pm 1$, giving $f = -1$ (minima).
    • $x_2 = 0$ and $\lambda = -1$: then $x_1 = \pm 1$, giving $f = +1$ (maxima).
  3. Result. The four critical points sit at $(\pm 1, 0)$ and $(0, \pm 1)$, alternating between maxima and minima around the circle.

The level sets of $x_1^2 - x_2^2 = c$ are hyperbolas — opening left/right when $c > 0$, up/down when $c < 0$, degenerating into the pair of lines $x_1 = \pm x_2$ when $c = 0$. The unit circle is tangent to four members of this family.

§ XSeveral constraints, briefly

When $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ has $m$ scalar components, the feasible set is the intersection of $m$ hypersurfaces. At a regular point of this intersection, the normal space is no longer one-dimensional — it is the $m$-dimensional span of the gradients $\nabla g_1, \ldots, \nabla g_m$.

The optimality criterion generalises in the natural way: $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ must lie in this normal subspace, expressible as a linear combination

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = -\sum_{j=1}^{m} \lambda_j^*\, \nabla g_j(\mathbf{x}^*).$$

The coefficients $\lambda_j^*$ are the higher-dimensional Lagrange multipliers, recovered as components of the saddle point of the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L} = f + \boldsymbol{\lambda}^\top \mathbf{g}$.

In summary

Constrained optimality is a statement about directions. The objective gradient must be expressible as a combination of the constraint normals — nothing more, nothing less. The multipliers $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ are precisely the coefficients of that combination, and they emerge automatically from the Lagrangian.
