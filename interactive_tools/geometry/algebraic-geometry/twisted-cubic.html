A note on curves in affine 3-space

The twisted cubic, &
the surfaces that hold it

On why two equations are almost, but not quite, enough.

The map \( t \mapsto (t, t^2, t^3) \) sweeps out one of the oldest objects in algebraic geometry: the twisted cubic. It is smooth, rational, and — despite living in \( \mathbb{A}^3 \) — genuinely three-dimensional in the sense that its three coordinates are algebraically independent up to the defining relations. Sit with it for a moment and surfaces start to appear around it. Three especially natural ones show up immediately:

\( y = x^2, \qquad z = xy, \qquad xz = y^2. \)

Each is a quadric. Each contains the curve — check by substituting \( x = t,\ y = t^2,\ z = t^3 \):

\( t^2 = t^2, \qquad t^3 = t \cdot t^2, \qquad t \cdot t^3 = (t^2)^2. \)

They are in some sense the three simplest surfaces the twisted cubic lies on. And yet they stand in a slightly awkward relationship to one another — none of them is redundant, but no two of them pin the curve down completely. That awkwardness is the subject of the note.

§1The three surfaces, geometrically

It is worth pausing to see what each surface actually looks like, because the shapes do a lot of the explanatory work.

\( y = x^2 \) — the parabolic cylinder. The equation says nothing about \(z\), so the surface is just the parabola \(y = x^2\) in the \(xy\)-plane, swept vertically. The twisted cubic winds up its face as \(z = t^3\) climbs.

\( z = xy \) — the hyperbolic paraboloid (saddle). A classical doubly-ruled surface: fix \(x\) and \(z\) is linear in \(y\); fix \(y\) and \(z\) is linear in \(x\). It is the graph of the symmetric function \(xy\).

\( xz = y^2 \) — a quadric cone. Parametrize as \((s, st, st^2)\): every point lies on a line through the origin with direction \((1, t, t^2)\). So this surface is literally swept out by the tangent directions of the twisted cubic at origin's neighborhood — a cone over the rational normal curve of degree 2 (a conic).

§2Pairwise intersections

The curve lives on all three. The natural question is: does any pair of them cut it out? Pick two equations and solve. There are three cases.

\( \{y = x^2\} \cap \{z = xy\} \)
complete intersection
Substitute the first into the second: \(z = x \cdot x^2 = x^3\). Together with \(y = x^2\), this parametrizes precisely the twisted cubic. Nothing else.
\( \{y = x^2\} \cap \{xz = y^2\} \)
plus extra
Substitute \(y = x^2\) into \(xz = y^2\) to get \(xz = x^4\), i.e. \(x(z - x^3) = 0\). So either \(z = x^3\) (the cubic) or \(x = 0\) (the entire \(z\)-axis, since \(y = x^2 = 0\) too).
\( \{z = xy\} \cap \{xz = y^2\} \)
plus extra
Substitute \(z = xy\) into \(xz = y^2\) to get \(x^2 y = y^2\), i.e. \(y(x^2 - y) = 0\). So either \(y = x^2\) (giving the cubic) or \(y = 0,\ z = 0\) (the \(x\)-axis).

Two pairs out of three come with a line of “debris” stuck to the curve. Only the pair \( \{y - x^2,\ z - xy\} \) is clean.

Try it above: turn off xz = y² and only the clean pair is showing. Both surfaces meet exactly along the orange curve, with nothing trailing.

§3Why two equations are never quite enough

The clean pair feels like it solves the problem. It does, affinely. As an ideal in \(k[x, y, z]\), the twisted cubic is cut out by

\( I(C) = (\, y - x^2,\ z - xy\,) \)

and here the curve really is a complete intersection of two hypersurfaces. This is already a pleasant fact.

The trouble arrives when one crosses into projective space. Homogenize with \(x_0 = 1,\ x_1 = x,\ x_2 = y,\ x_3 = z\), and the twisted cubic becomes the image of

\( [s : t] \longmapsto [s^3 : s^2 t : s t^2 : t^3] \)

in \(\mathbb{P}^3\). It has degree \(3\). But by Bézout, the intersection of any two quadric surfaces in \(\mathbb{P}^3\) — if it is a curve at all — has degree \(4\). So whichever two quadrics you choose from our list, their projective intersection has one degree too many; the leftover degree is an extra component out at infinity (or, projectively visible, a line on the surfaces). You saw affine shadows of this above: the \(x\)-axis and the \(z\)-axis are exactly the finite parts of those extra lines.

The conclusion is that the twisted cubic, viewed projectively, is not a complete intersection. Its homogeneous ideal cannot be generated by two polynomials; three are required. This is arguably the simplest example of the phenomenon, and it is the standard first example in any course that treats non-complete intersections.

§4The determinantal picture

There is a much cleaner way to encode all three quadrics at once. Arrange the coordinates in a \(2 \times 3\) matrix of linear forms (with \(w\) thought of as the homogenizing variable, or simply \(w = 1\) affinely):

\( M \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} w & x & y \\ x & y & z \end{pmatrix} \)

Its three \(2 \times 2\) minors are

\( wy - x^2, \qquad wz - xy, \qquad xz - y^2. \)

Setting \(w = 1\), these become exactly \(y - x^2,\ z - xy,\ xz - y^2\) — our three surfaces, now recognized as the minors of a single matrix. The twisted cubic is the locus where \(M\) has rank one, and its ideal is generated by all three minors.

This is not a coincidence; it is the start of a rich story. The rational normal curve of degree \(d\) in \(\mathbb{P}^d\) is always cut out by the \(2 \times 2\) minors of a Hankel-style \(2 \times d\) matrix of coordinates. Such determinantal varieties come with beautiful free resolutions (the Eagon–Northcott complex), predictable singularities, and the degree \(3\) case — our twisted cubic — is the smallest non-trivial example. The irritation of “two equations aren't enough” turns out to be the first symptom of a deep structural fact: this curve is defined by the vanishing of minors, and minors of a \(2 \times n\) matrix with \(n \geq 3\) simply are not a complete intersection.

The extra component of \(\{y = x^2\} \cap \{xz = y^2\}\) is the \(z\)-axis. The extra component of \(\{z = xy\} \cap \{xz = y^2\}\) is the \(x\)-axis. The missing axis is in each case the one not meeting the matrix minor you left out — a small diagrammatic footprint of the determinantal structure.

§5Coda

The twisted cubic is not complicated. You can draw it, you can parametrize it, you can write down its ideal on the back of an envelope. But its stubborn refusal to be a complete intersection in projective space is a first glimpse of how algebraic geometry refuses to reduce to linear algebra, even for the simplest-looking curves. Rotate the picture above and try to see the three quadrics holding the curve together from three different angles: saddle draped over parabolic cylinder, meeting the cone at the origin. Three surfaces, one curve, and a line of extra structure tucked away wherever you look at it with fewer than three equations at once.
